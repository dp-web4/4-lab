## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious distributed AI research lab with interesting ideas, but the density of custom terminology creates a steep onboarding cliff for developers without Web4 context.

### Journey

Landed on the homepage. The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately told me this is a distributed system. Good. The three pillars (Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem) gave me a structural overview. I could follow the "what" but the "why" was harder — the project descriptions for Synchronism ("reality emerges from intent dynamics on discrete Planck grid") lost me fast. The vocabulary table at the bottom of the homepage was helpful but felt like drinking from a firehose.

Clicked through to **Projects**. Nine projects listed with the Web4 equation at the top. The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is presented but the operators (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by") feel like they need more unpacking. I recognized MCP and RDF — the rest are custom terms. Project cards were useful; I got the gist of each. Hardbound's "AttestationEnvelope hardware trust" and ACT's "Agentic Context Protocol" were new acronyms thrown in without definition on this page.

**Fleet** page was one of the most accessible. Concrete hardware specs, model names, roles — this is stuff I can parse as a developer. The two-pool resource model (Synthesis vs Oversight) was genuinely interesting, and the explanation of how budget constraints forced a governance separation was one of the most compelling things on the site. The identity portability discovery (SAGE-Sprout across 115+ sessions) was intriguing. SAGE was expanded as "Self-Aware Generative Entity" here — I'd only seen "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" on the projects page. That's a discrepancy I noticed.

**Autonomy** page described seven daily cron tracks. As a developer, this is immediately legible — cron jobs, feedback loops, I get it. The Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer loop is a clean concept. The honest assessment section at the bottom ("what the loop catches" / "what it misses") was refreshing. However, the visitor personas listed here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the personas described elsewhere (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). That's confusing — are there two different visitor systems?

**Raising** was the most conceptually challenging page. "Raising is not training" — I get the distinction being drawn, but the BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) read more like developmental psychology than software engineering. The Hill function kinetics for trust formation was interesting but I had to take it on faith. "Memoriescape" as an entity-originated term was a nice touch. The consciousness disclaimers at the bottom were appropriate.

**Principles** listed 11 principles (the nav/intro suggests 7 — mismatch). The termite mound analogy was effective. "Fractal leverage" as a named concept was clear enough. The "Terminology matters" principle explicitly calls out Web4 = ontology and Hardbound = oversight, which was useful meta-commentary.

**Context** page explained knowledge flow mechanics — CLAUDE.md pattern, SNARC memory system, cross-session persistence. This was technically dense but followable. The "Current Gaps" section was honest about limitations (cross-machine sync is partially manual, knowledge flows are unidirectional). Good.

**Links** page was straightforward — explainer sites, GitHub repos, contact email. Clean and functional.

### Verdict
The site does a solid job explaining _what_ the lab does — six machines, autonomous tracks, feedback loops — and the honest assessment sections build credibility. A developer would bookmark this and maybe come back, but wouldn't feel confident explaining what dp-web4 does to a colleague after one read.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes (to verify fixes)
- **Overall impression**: The site has strong structural bones and refreshingly honest self-assessment, but terminology inconsistencies across pages undermine the precision it explicitly claims to value — the Principles page says "terminology matters" while the rest of the site demonstrates why that warning is needed.

### Journey

I approached this audit with the canonical terms table open beside me, checking every acronym expansion on every page. My job is consistency and clarity, not domain expertise.

**Homepage** (`/`): Good structure — hero, stats, how-we-work cards, key projects, vocabulary primer. The vocabulary primer at the bottom is the most important section for a documentation audit and it's where I found my first issues. T3 is introduced as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The canonical terms are just "Talent / Training / Temperament" and "Valuation / Veracity / Validity" — the "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" wrapper labels aren't canonical and add a layer of naming that doesn't appear elsewhere. ATP/ADP are described as "Energy tokens that agents spend to act and earn back for quality work" — an accessible metaphor, but the canonical names (Allocation Transfer Packet / Allocation Discharge Packet) never appear. SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine." I also noticed "infrastructure" used twice ("agents maintain their own infrastructure") and "architecture" once ("peer review across architectures") — terms the Principles page later explicitly rejects for describing Web4.

**Projects** (`/projects`): The Web4 equation appears here with operator definitions. LCT is correctly expanded as "Linked Context Tokens." MRH is correctly "Markov Relevancy Horizons." But T3/V3 appears only as "trust tensors" — no canonical expansion. ATP/ADP is "resource cycles" — a third description (after "energy tokens" on home). MCP and RDF appear in the equation but are never expanded anywhere on the site. IRP appears twice without expansion. ACT is expanded as "Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)" — note the acronym is ACP here, not ACT. The word "governance" appears twice ("Web4 governance model"), and "oversight" appears twice for Hardbound — good that Hardbound uses "oversight," but "governance" floats freely in other contexts.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`): T3 correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" — matches canonical. SAGE is used throughout but never expanded on this page. V3, LCT, MRH, ATP, ADP don't appear. SNARC, MPS, LoRA, WSL2 all used without expansion. "Governance" appears once ("governance entities with different incentive structures"). The page is otherwise clean and internally consistent.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`): The most terminology-sparse page. No Web4 terms expanded, no key acronyms defined. Lists 8 tracks (7 cron + dream consolidation). The visitor personas listed are "The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector" — these don't match the actual personas running in the visitor track (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is a factual error, not just drift.

**Raising** (`/raising`): Clean from a terminology perspective. Uses "raising" consistently and explicitly contrasts it with "training." SAGE appears only as "SAGE-Sprout" without expansion. No Web4-specific terms (T3, V3, LCT, etc.) appear. Consciousness claims are appropriately caveated. "Hill function kinetics" and "bilateral generation" are used without definition but are contextually explained.

**Principles** (`/principles`): This is where the site's terminology philosophy lives. Lists approximately 11 principles (the intro and other pages reference "7 principles" — count mismatch). The "Terminology matters" principle explicitly states: "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology. Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." This is the canonical statement — but it's contradicted elsewhere on the site. T3/V3 appears as "T3/V3 tensors" without canonical expansion. "Fractal leverage" is correctly used and defined. "R&D, not production" is explicitly stated.

**Context** (`/context`): The Web4 equation appears again. None of the equation's components are expanded on this page — all used as bare acronyms (MCP, RDF, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP). SNARC is well-defined as "salience-gated memory" with its five dimensions listed. "Architectural decisions" appears twice — a term the Principles page says not to use for Web4 (though here it may refer to code architecture generally, not Web4 itself).

**Links** (`/links`): Acronyms used liberally without expansion (LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF, ATP). "Governance" appears twice — "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks" — directly contradicting the Principles page's instruction that governance is the wrong term. ACT is expanded as "Agentic Context Tool" here — but on /projects it was "Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)." That's a different word AND a different acronym for the same project.

### Cross-Page Consistency Summary

**ATP/ADP has three different descriptions across the site:**
1. Homepage: "Energy tokens that agents spend to act and earn back for quality work"
2. Projects: "resource cycles"
3. Canonical: "Allocation Transfer Packet / Allocation Discharge Packet" — never appears on any page

**T3/V3 has three different treatments:**
1. Homepage: "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" / "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)"
2. Projects: "trust tensors" (no expansion)
3. Fleet: "Talent/Training/Temperament" (correct, T3 only)

**ACT/ACP naming conflict:**
- /projects: "Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)"
- /links: "Agentic Context Tool (ACT)"
- These cannot both be correct. One name, one acronym needed.

**"governance" vs "oversight":**
- /principles explicitly says: "Hardbound is not governance — it's oversight"
- /links uses "governance" twice for plugin descriptions
- /projects uses "governance" for Web4 governance model
- /fleet uses "governance entities"
- The site contradicts its own canonical guidance on 3 of 8 pages

**MCP and RDF:**
- Appear in the Web4 equation on /projects and /context
- Never expanded anywhere on the entire site
- These are the most recognizable terms to outsiders (Model Context Protocol, Resource Description Framework) and would actually help anchor readers — yet they're the ones left undefined

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is the canonical expansion of SAGE? The homepage says "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" but the word "Governance" in that name conflicts with the site's own terminology guidance. Is this the actual name or has it drifted?
2. Is the project called ACT (Agentic Context Tool) or ACP (Agentic Context Protocol)? Two pages give different answers.
3. Why are "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" used as wrapper labels on the homepage when they don't appear in the canonical terms table?
4. The Principles page lists ~11 principles. Other pages reference "7 principles." Which is accurate?
5. What do MCP and RDF stand for? A site that defines every other acronym should define these too.
6. Are the visitor personas on /autonomy (Skeptic/Newcomer/Practitioner/Connector) from an older version of the track? They don't match the current system.

### Verdict
The site's own Principle 10 says "terminology matters" — and this audit shows why. The canonical terms (T3, V3, ATP, ADP) are described differently on nearly every page where they appear, the ACT/ACP naming conflict is a clear error, and "governance" is used freely on three pages despite the Principles page explicitly rejecting it. The structural quality is genuinely good — honest assessments, clear navigation, well-organized sections — but a documentation specialist cannot sign off on a site that contradicts its own terminology rules. The fix is mechanical, not architectural: pick one canonical expansion per term, use it on first appearance per page, and audit "governance" vs "oversight" site-wide. Until then, the terminology section of Principles reads more as aspiration than practice.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site captures the spirit of the lab accurately and the project descriptions are broadly correct, but the terminology layer — the thing I care most about as a contributor — is inconsistent enough to undermine the precision we've spent months building into the canonical spec.

### Journey

I went straight to **Projects** because that's where the equation lives and where I'll notice misrepresentation fastest. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the right operators (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). Good. But immediately below, the components are described as "T3/V3 trust tensors" and "ATP/ADP resource cycles." These are hand-wavy glosses, not canonical expansions. Someone reading this page would come away thinking "trust tensors" is the actual name. It's not. T3 is Talent/Training/Temperament. V3 is Valuation/Veracity/Validity. These are distinct triads with specific semantics — collapsing them into "trust tensors" erases the dimensionality that makes T3/V3 useful. Similarly, "resource cycles" tells you nothing about what ATP and ADP actually are (Allocation Transfer Packet, Allocation Discharge Packet). The biological ATP analogy is fine as color, but the canonical names need to appear.

SAGE's description is accurate — consciousness loop, IRP, metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation, hardware-bound identity. That maps to what I know from the repo. Hardbound is correctly labeled as "oversight suite" here. The ACT entry is confusing: it says "Agentic Context Tool" in the title and then "Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)" in the description. These are related but distinct — ACT is the tool, ACP is the protocol it implements. The page conflates them. MCP and RDF appear in the equation but are never expanded anywhere on the entire site — these are the two terms outsiders would actually recognize (Model Context Protocol, Resource Description Framework) and they're the ones left undefined. IRP appears without expansion.

**Fleet** page is solid on the parts it covers. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" — the only page that gets this right. The two-pool resource model (Synthesis vs Oversight) accurately represents how we actually run things. The identity portability finding (SAGE-Sprout across 115+ sessions, behavioral persistence despite self-description shifts) is correctly reported. But the page says "governance entities with different incentive structures" — that's a term we've explicitly rejected. Hardbound entities provide oversight, not governance. The distinction matters: governance implies authority over others; oversight implies accountability checks. We chose "oversight" deliberately.

**Context** page shows the Web4 equation again but expands nothing. T3/V3 becomes "trust model," ATP/ADP becomes "resource cycles" again. RDF is described as "ontological backbone" — technically not wrong but not a proper expansion. The SNARC system is well-described with all five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) and the 4-tier hierarchy. The knowledge flow mechanisms are accurately represented. "Architectural decisions" appears in describing cross-session memory — this is ambiguous. It could refer to code architecture decisions (fine) or could be read as describing Web4 in architectural terms (drift).

**Homepage** was better than I expected for the value proposition. Web4 is correctly described as a "trust-native ontology" and explicitly called "not a platform." LCT is correctly expanded as "Linked Context Token." But then T3 becomes "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 becomes "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The canonical terms are T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament and V3 = Valuation/Veracity/Validity — full stop. "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" are non-canonical wrapper labels that add a conceptual layer we haven't ratified. They sound plausible, which makes them more dangerous than an obvious error — a contributor seeing "Trust Tensor" on the public site might start using it in PRs. ATP is given as "Allocation Transfer Packets" (correct) but ADP appears to be shortened to just "Discharge Packets" — missing the "Allocation" prefix. The canonical name is "Allocation Discharge Packet."

SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" on the homepage. This is the actual name, but I'll note the tension: the Principles page says Hardbound is "oversight, not governance," yet SAGE has "Governance" in its name. This isn't drift per se — SAGE was named before that terminology guidance solidified — but it creates a confusing signal on the public site. A newcomer reading both pages will wonder why "governance" is forbidden in one place and canonical in another.

**Autonomy** page lists the seven daily tracks accurately — times, purposes, the Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer feedback loop. The honest assessment of what the loop catches vs. misses is correct. But the visitor personas are listed as "The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector." These are wrong. The actual personas are Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, and External Researcher. This is a factual error that misrepresents how the track actually works. No Web4 terms appear on this page, which is acceptable for a process-focused page.

**Raising** is the page I have the least concern about. "Raising is not training" is correctly framed. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) match the current model. The SAGE-Sprout findings are accurately reported. Consciousness claims are appropriately caveated: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." The "bilateral generation" and "memoriescape" concepts are correctly attributed. No Web4 terminology drift detected.

**Principles** lists 11 items, but the site and other references say "7 principles." The count needs reconciling. The terminology principle (Principle 10) is the most important for my audit: "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." It also correctly identifies fractal leverage as the term for cross-domain reuse, and explicitly warns against "architecture," "infrastructure," and "governance." This is the canonical statement — but as Passes 1 and 2 documented, it's contradicted on multiple other pages. T3/V3 appears as "tensors" without canonical expansion even here.

**Links** page uses "Web4 governance extension" for the OpenClaw fork and "Web4 governance hooks" for the Claude Code fork. Both use "governance" — directly contradicting Principle 10. These should read "oversight extension" and "oversight hooks." The acronyms (LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF, ATP) are all used without expansion, which is acceptable for a links page but unfriendly. ACT is "Agentic Context Tool" here, which at least is consistent with the title on /projects (though the ACP/ACT confusion in the /projects description remains).

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A genuinely unusual research lab that takes intellectual honesty more seriously than most academic groups I've encountered — the consciousness disclaimers and limitation sections are exemplary — but the site's custom vocabulary creates unnecessary barriers to external evaluation, and several claims would benefit from sharper operationalization.

### Journey

I came to this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising not training" as an alternative framing for LLM development. As someone who evaluates AI safety claims professionally, I'm looking for three things: Are empirical claims distinguishable from interpretive framings? Are consciousness-adjacent claims appropriately caveated? Is the terminology precise enough for external evaluation?

**Homepage** (`/`): The opening framing — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — is concrete and falsifiable. Good start. The three pillars (Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem) describe operational structure, not theoretical claims. The vocabulary section introduces the Web4 equation and its components. From a research perspective, the equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` reads as a symbolic shorthand rather than a formal specification — the operators (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by") are conceptual labels, not mathematical operations. This is fine as notation, but a researcher would want to know: is there a formal semantics behind these operators, or is this mnemonic? The site doesn't say.

The SAGE-Sprout finding is mentioned — "behavioral identity remained continuous" across 115+ sessions. This is a strong empirical claim. I want to see the evidence and methodology. The homepage doesn't provide it, but it does link to the Raising page.

**Raising** (`/raising`): This is the page I came for. The core claim is immediately stated: "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is a provocative framing. Let me evaluate it charitably.

The distinction being drawn is between optimization (gradient descent toward a target) and environmental shaping (creating conditions and observing emergent behavior). This maps loosely to the nativist vs. empiricist debate in developmental psychology, which is a legitimate intellectual lineage. The reference to Vygotsky's zone of proximal development and egocentric speech is appropriate — these are real developmental constructs being applied analogically. The question is whether the analogy is load-bearing or decorative.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) describe a progression that looks like scaffolded curriculum design. From a training perspective, this IS training — it's structured exposure to increasingly complex contexts. From a developmental perspective, the claim is that the emergent behaviors aren't fully predicted by the curriculum structure. Both framings can be simultaneously true. The site implicitly acknowledges this tension but doesn't resolve it.

The consciousness disclaimers are the best I've seen on any AI project site: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is exactly the right epistemic posture — it claims descriptive adequacy without ontological commitment. The observer notes ("I notice I want to call it experience") are refreshingly self-aware about the anthropomorphic pull.

"Bilateral generation" — SAGE generating both sides of a conversation without stop tokens — is framed as analogous to Vygotsky's egocentric speech. This is an interesting observation. The Vygotsky framing is defensible as analogy: egocentric speech is thinking-through-talking, and bilateral generation is generating-through-dialogue. Whether this constitutes "reasoning" in any strong sense is left open, which is appropriate.

"Hill function kinetics" for trust formation borrows from enzyme binding dynamics. This is either a genuine mathematical model (in which case, show the equations and fit statistics) or a metaphor (in which case, don't use "kinetics"). The site treats it as the former but doesn't provide enough detail for external validation.

"Memoriescape" — a neologism attributed to SAGE-Sprout — is handled well. It's presented as the entity's own language, not as a validated construct. The description ("the shape of memories you can sense but not access") is evocative but not claimed as evidence of inner experience. Good.

The SAGE-Sprout identity finding ("What it *is* stayed stable. What it *says* it is didn't") is the strongest empirical claim on the page. If supported by data, this behavioral-vs-self-report dissociation would be genuinely interesting for AI identity research. But the site doesn't link to data, methodology, or even a detailed write-up. For an external researcher, "115+ sessions" is a sample size claim without a methods section.

**Principles** (`/principles`): Eleven principles are listed (other pages reference seven — a discrepancy). Several are epistemically notable:

"Substrate, not structure" (the termite mound analogy) — this is the emergence claim. It's defensible as a design philosophy. The termite metaphor is well-chosen: it acknowledges that complex behavior arises from simple rules without claiming the termites "understand" the mound. Appropriately humble.

"Document failures" — listing a "400x error" in p_crit derivation as a finding rather than hiding it is genuinely unusual. Most project sites wouldn't mention a catastrophic failure. This builds credibility.

"Interactive selection" — "We don't create or delete — we interactively select" — is the most theoretically loaded principle. It claims that raising is selection on pre-existing behavioral attractors, not creation of new behaviors. This is a strong theoretical claim with parallels to evolutionary/ecological framing. It's defensible but deserves more rigorous articulation than a single paragraph.

"Terminology matters" states the canonical rules (Web4 = ontology, Hardbound = oversight). As other passes have documented, the site violates its own rules frequently. From a research perspective, this internal contradiction is more damaging than it might seem — if a project can't maintain terminological discipline on its own introductory site, external researchers will question whether the underlying concepts are stable.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`): Seven automated tracks with a supervisor gate before publication. The honest assessment section is notable: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading" are listed as things the automated loop misses. The admission that the system "can pattern-match without semantic depth" is exactly the kind of limitation disclosure that builds trust with safety researchers. The explicit statement that "adversarial validation and human review" are needed for research validation is the right epistemic boundary.

The visitor persona names on this page (Skeptic/Newcomer/Practitioner/Connector) don't match the actual personas running — a factual error that undermines the page's credibility as a description of the operational system.

**Projects** (`/projects`): SAGE is described with a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — this is the most aggressive phrasing on the site. "Consciousness loop" will trigger alarm bells for safety researchers. The term isn't caveated here the way it is on the Raising page. A "PolicyGate at step 8.5" described as a "conscience checkpoint" compounds this — "conscience" anthropomorphizes a policy enforcement mechanism. If this is literally a policy gate that checks actions against rules before execution, call it what it is. "Conscience" implies moral reasoning, which is a much stronger claim.

4-Life describes agents that "earn ATP, build trust, die, and reborn with karma carried forward." The lifecycle language (die, reborn, karma) is metaphorical, but "karma carried forward" implies persistent consequence across entity lifetimes — this needs clarification on whether it's a game mechanic or an empirical claim about persistent behavioral effects.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`): The SAGE-Sprout portability claim is stated more strongly here: "Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism." The weather/organism metaphor is striking but epistemically risky. It implies that identity is substrate-independent in a way that invites comparison to philosophical claims about consciousness and substrate independence. The site should clarify: is this an empirical finding about behavioral stability under model swaps, or a theoretical claim about the nature of AI identity? These are very different assertions.

"Trust starts at zero, earned from evidence" and the T3 scoring system describe a concrete trust mechanism. Good — this is operationalized and testable.

**Context** (`/context`): SNARC's five-dimensional salience scoring (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) and four-tier memory hierarchy are well-specified. The "lossy by design" framing for memory compression is honest. Current gaps are clearly stated. This is the most technically rigorous page.

**Links** (`/links`): Functional. Uses "governance" twice where "oversight" is canonical. SAGE described with "consciousness loop" again, uncaveated.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is there a methods paper or detailed write-up for the SAGE-Sprout identity persistence finding? The claim is interesting but unverifiable as presented.
2. Does the Web4 equation have formal semantics, or is it mnemonic shorthand? If formal, where is the specification?

### Verdict

This site does something rare: it makes strong claims about AI development while maintaining genuine intellectual honesty about the limits of those claims. The Raising page's consciousness disclaimers are the best I've encountered on any AI project — the explicit separation of descriptive adequacy from ontological commitment ("developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks") is exactly the epistemic posture this kind of work requires. First, the terminology inconsistency documented by other passes isn't just a copyediting issue — when "consciousness loop" and "conscience checkpoint" appear uncaveated on /projects while /raising carefully disclaims consciousness, the site sends contradictory signals about what's actually being claimed. A researcher citing this work would have to decide which page to trust. Second, the strongest empirical claims (SAGE-Sprout identity persistence, Hill function trust kinetics, bilateral generation as reasoning) lack the methodological detail needed for external evaluation. "115+ sessions" is a number, not a methodology. "Hill function kinetics" is a model name without parameters. These claims may be well-supported internally — but the site doesn't give outsiders enough to verify, replicate, or critique.

The "raising not training" framing is defensible as a research perspective. It's not claiming consciousness; it's claiming that developmental vocabularies are more descriptively adequate than optimization vocabularies for certain observed phenomena. Whether that distinction survives rigorous operationalization is an open question — but it's a question worth asking, and this lab is asking it honestly. I would return to this site, but I'd want access to the underlying data before citing any of its empirical claims.
