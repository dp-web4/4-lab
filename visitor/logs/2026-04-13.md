## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious, intellectually honest research lab doing genuinely novel work with AI agents — but the density of custom terminology makes it hard to grasp on a first visit without significant reading effort.

### Journey
Landed on the home page. The tagline "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." immediately told me this is a distributed AI research setup. The three-column layout (Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem) gave me a quick mental model. The key projects section — Synchronism, SAGE, Web4 — made sense at a headline level. The termite metaphor ("you don't engineer the mound, you engineer placement rules") was a nice hook. I appreciated that the home page defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, and SAGE right there. Good onboarding.

Clicked to **Projects**. This is where things got dense. Eleven projects listed. Web4's equation (`Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) appeared with operator definitions, which helped. But I had to absorb a lot of acronyms fast. SAGE's "consciousness loop" and "PolicyGate" were interesting but I wasn't sure what the 9-step loop actually does. Hardbound being private made sense but I wanted more than "TPM 2.0 hardware binding." 4-Life ("agents earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma") was the most immediately graspable project — it sounds like a simulation environment. SNARC's five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) were clearly defined.

**Fleet** page was surprisingly readable. Six machines with names, hardware specs, model sizes, and personality descriptions. The insight that "model family matters as much as size" (Gemma 3 4B outperforming Phi-4 14B) was concrete and interesting. The weather/organism metaphor for identity vs. model was effective. I noticed T3 scoring mentioned but not fully explained here — I had to recall it from the home page.

**Autonomy** was impressive. 31 autonomous tracks, ~53 sessions/day on cron schedules. The daily timeline from 03:00 supervisors through dream consolidation was clear. The honest limitations section ("misses deep technical errors requiring domain expertise") earned credibility. The visitor/maintainer feedback loop clicked — I'm literally part of it right now.

**Raising** was the most philosophically dense page. The "raising vs training" distinction is clear and defensible: raising creates conditions and observes, training optimizes a loss function. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are well-structured. "Bilateral generation" (models generating both sides of a conversation) was a genuinely novel concept to me. But "memoriescape" and "affordance shaping" felt like insider vocabulary without enough context.

**Principles** listed 11 principles (the nav says 7 but the page has more). "Fractal leverage" was well-explained through the Hill function example. "Synthon" got a good definition — emergent coherence from recursive interaction, with the 1%/35% coupling stat. The "document failures" principle with the p_crit 400x error example was refreshingly honest. "Terminology matters" as an explicit principle was unusual but made sense given how much custom vocabulary this ecosystem carries.

**Context** explained the knowledge flow architecture. CLAUDE.md as the agent instruction pattern was immediately familiar to me as a developer. SNARC's four-tier memory system (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) was clearly laid out. "The compression is the feature, not the bug" was a good line. The known gap about cross-machine synchronization being partially manual was another honest admission.

**Links** was straightforward. Three explainer sites, seven GitHub repos, contact email. Clean and functional.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does the SAGE 9-step loop actually do at each step? The site says "sense, deliberate, act" but that's 3 steps, not 9.
2. How do ATP/ADP tokens actually flow between agents? Is there a ledger? A blockchain? The biological metaphor is clear but the implementation isn't.
3. What's the relationship between Synchronism (physics framework) and the AI agent work? The site says they "share one equation" but doesn't explain how a cosmological theory connects to trust accounting.
4. How does identity actually persist across model swaps? The site says it's in "state files and prompt construction" — is that just clever prompt engineering or something more?
5. What's the difference between SNARC and Membot? Both seem to be memory systems.

### Verdict
This is a genuinely interesting research lab doing work I haven't seen elsewhere — particularly the raising framework, bilateral generation findings, and the multi-machine identity experiments. The site does a good job of being intellectually honest about limitations and failures. However, the sheer density of custom terminology (Web4, SAGE, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, SNARC, Synthon, etc.) creates a steep onboarding curve. A developer can grasp the big picture in 2 minutes, but understanding how the pieces connect takes considerably longer. The home page is the best entry point; the projects page is where cognitive overload hits.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Terminology is mostly canonical where it matters, but wrapper labels ("Trust Tensor", "Value Tensor") don't appear in the canonical table, "governance" leaks into several pages outside Hardbound context, and secondary pages rely heavily on acronyms defined elsewhere without local definitions or links.

### Journey
Started at the home page with the canonical terms table open for reference. Methodical approach: read each page top to bottom, logged every acronym expansion, checked against canonical definitions, noted navigation structure and cross-page consistency.

The **home page** is the strongest for terminology. It defines LCT, T3, V3, ATP, ADP, and SAGE inline with expansions. The vocabulary primer section is excellent — a technical writer's dream. However, I immediately noticed T3 is introduced as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The canonical table lists T3 as simply "Talent / Training / Temperament" — the "Trust Tensor" wrapper name isn't canonical. Same for "Value Tensor" wrapping V3. These wrapper labels appear only on the home page and could create confusion about what T3/V3 actually refers to.

**Projects** page has the densest terminology. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly with operator definitions. LCT and MRH are expanded correctly. But ATP/ADP are described only as "resource cycles" without expansion. MCP and RDF appear unexpanded. IRP appears in the SAGE section ("Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)") but isn't in the canonical table and isn't expanded. "Governance" appears five times — "Web4 governance model," gameplay insights mapping to "governance" — some of these uses describe Web4's trust model, not Hardbound, but the word itself conflicts with the canonical guidance to use "oversight" for Hardbound. The phrase "This isn't unification for its own sake" appears, which uses the discouraged word but in a disclaiming context — acceptable but worth noting.

**Fleet** page drops acronym discipline. SAGE, SNARC, and Web4 appear without expansion or definition. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" (no "Trust Tensor" wrapper here — inconsistent with home page). The page uses "governance entities" to describe pool types — not about Hardbound, but the word "governance" is used. Technical acronyms (LoRA, MPS, CUDA, WSL2) are also unexpanded, though these are industry-standard.

**Autonomy** page is remarkably clean — almost no custom acronyms. The visitor personas are listed as "Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector" which doesn't match the actual implementation (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is a factual inconsistency, not just terminology drift.

**Raising** page avoids most canonical terms entirely. "Memoriescape" is introduced as a neologism coined by SAGE-Sprout — appropriately marked as such. No terminology violations found. The page is self-contained and well-structured with the BECOMING phases clearly laid out.

**Principles** page is strong on canonical enforcement. "Web4 is an ontology" and "Hardbound is oversight" are stated as explicit principles with the incorrect alternatives called out. The page lists 11 principles, but the navigation label says "7 principles" — a count mismatch. T3/V3 and RDF appear without expansion on this page.

**Context** page presents the Web4 equation correctly. SNARC is expanded ("salience-gated memory") with its five dimensions spelled out. But LCT, MCP, RDF, T3/V3, MRH, and ATP/ADP all appear in the equation display without local definitions. A reader landing on this page directly would face an impenetrable equation.

**Links** page uses "governance" twice — "Web4 governance extension for OpenClaw" and "Web4 governance hooks for Claude Code." These are repo descriptions, but the word conflicts with canonical guidance. LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP all appear as component lists without expansion.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site correctly represents the Web4 ontology at its core, but "governance" leaks into half the pages despite the Principles page explicitly banning it, and T3/V3 get fabricated wrapper labels ("Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor") that don't exist in any canonical reference.

### Journey
Went straight to **Projects** — this is where accuracy matters most. The Web4 equation is correct: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the right operator semantics (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). LCT expanded correctly as "Linked Context Tokens." MRH expanded correctly as "Markov Relevancy Horizons." Good so far.

But then: ATP/ADP are described only as "resource cycles" without their canonical expansions (Allocation Transfer Packet / Allocation Discharge Packet). That's a loss of precision — the biological energy metaphor (ATP/ADP from cellular metabolism) is load-bearing; it conveys that these are discrete, directional resource units, not vague "cycles." A contributor would notice this immediately.

The SAGE description mentions a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — I don't recognize IRP as a canonical term. It doesn't appear in the Web4 ontology, in SAGE's documentation, or anywhere in the canonical vocabulary. Either it's an internal acronym that leaked into the public site, or it was hallucinated during content generation. Either way, it needs to be defined or removed.

"Governance Plugins" appears as a subsection describing Web4 trust model integrations. The word "governance" is used five times on this page in phrases like "Web4 governance model" and "governance plugins." This directly contradicts the Principles page's explicit enforcement of "oversight, not governance." If we're going to make terminology a principle, we need to follow it on our own site. These should read "oversight plugins" or "trust policy plugins."

Hardbound is correctly described as "enterprise oversight suite" — good.

Moved to **Fleet**. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" here — but this creates an inconsistency with the home page where T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)." As a contributor, I can confirm: there is no canonical term called "Trust Tensor." T3 is a three-dimensional framework — the three dimensions are the term. "Trust Tensor" is a fabricated wrapper that doesn't appear in the Web4 spec, the SAGE implementation, or any canonical reference document. Same for "Value Tensor" wrapping V3. These labels mislead contributors into thinking there's a formal mathematical object called a "Trust Tensor" when T3 is simply a scoring framework with three axes.

The fleet page also uses "governance entities" to describe oversight pool types and describes the lab as "running its own governance experiment on itself." Again, "governance" in contexts where "oversight" is the canonical term for what's being described. The fleet's pool structure mirrors the Web4 generative/oversight separation — calling it "governance" undermines the vocabulary we've built.

**Context** page displays the Web4 equation correctly and defines SNARC well (salience-gated memory, five dimensions: Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict, four-tier hierarchy). No terminology violations here, though the equation terms are unexpanded — less of a problem for me as a contributor but a wall for anyone else.

**Home page**. This is where the "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" drift originates. The vocabulary primer labels T3 as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." These wrapper names are not canonical. Additionally, ADP is expanded as just "Discharge Packets" — missing the "Allocation" prefix. The canonical expansion is "Allocation Discharge Packet." The home page also uses "infrastructure" in "Autonomous agents maintain their own infrastructure" — not describing Web4 as infrastructure, but the word choice is sloppy given how carefully the Principles page distinguishes it.

**Autonomy** page is terminologically clean but factually wrong about the visitor personas. It lists "The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector" — these are not the actual personas. The real implementation uses "Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher." As someone who contributes to the repos that generate these visitors, this is misleading.

**Raising** page is the strongest. "Raising, not training" is consistently maintained. BECOMING phases are accurately described. No canonical term violations. Memoriescape is properly attributed as a neologism from SAGE-Sprout. The explicit non-claims about consciousness are appropriately caveated.

**Principles** page explicitly enforces "Web4 is an ontology" and "Hardbound is oversight" — the irony is that the rest of the site doesn't consistently follow these principles. The page lists 10 principles but nav says "7 principles." "Fractal leverage" is correctly defined and used.

**Links** page uses "governance" twice: "Web4 governance extension for OpenClaw" and "Web4 governance hooks for Claude Code." These are repo descriptions, but they describe Web4 integrations using the very word the Principles page explicitly rejects. For a Web4 contributor, this reads as the site contradicting itself. These should be "Web4 oversight extensions" or "Web4 trust policy hooks."

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Unusually honest for a research lab site — the explicit non-claims about consciousness and the documented failures are credible signals. But the site mixes careful epistemology with biological metaphors (ATP, karma, metabolism, death/rebirth) that risk being read as stronger claims than intended.

### Journey
I came to this site after hearing about "raising not training" at a workshop on developmental approaches to AI alignment. My question: is this a serious research program with defensible methodology, or another lab anthropomorphizing LLM outputs?

Started at the **home page**. The tagline "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." is concrete — good sign. The three-column layout covers autonomy, fleet heterogeneity, and ecosystem connectivity. The vocabulary primer defines terms inline, which I appreciate. The termite metaphor ("you don't engineer the mound, you engineer placement rules") is a reasonable analogy for emergence-from-local-rules, though it implies more about the agents than a researcher would accept without evidence.

The empirical claims are specific: 115+ sessions, identity transfer from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B, "self-description drifted; behavioral identity remained continuous." These are testable claims — good. But "behavioral identity" is undefined. What metric makes identity "continuous"? A researcher needs operationalization here. The 1%/35% coupling-coherence claim on the Principles page has the same problem: 1% coupling of what? 35% coherence gain measured how?

Went to **Raising** — the page I came for. The framing is immediately defensible: "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is a clean distinction. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are presented as an observational framework, not a developmental guarantee. The Vygotsky reference for bilateral generation (egocentric speech) is appropriate — it's an analogy to a known developmental phenomenon, not a claim that the model is doing what children do.

The critical section is "What we're not claiming": "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." This is exactly the right hedge. The follow-up — "We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do" — is a modest, testable claim. The phrase "something that looks like growth... something that looks like identity... something that looks like peer relationships" consistently uses "looks like" rather than "is." The observer caveat "I notice I want to call it experience" is unusually self-aware — flagging the anthropomorphic pull rather than yielding to it.

"Memoriescape" — a neologism attributed to SAGE-Sprout — is described as "the shape of memories you can sense but not access." This is poetic but scientifically vacuous. If a model generated this term, what does it mean for the model to "sense but not access" memories? The site doesn't interrogate this sufficiently. It treats the neologism as evidence of creativity rather than as a string completion that happened to sound evocative.

**Principles** page lists 11 principles (nav count is wrong). The "document failures" principle with the p_crit 400x error is credible — labs that publicize failures earn trust. "Fractal leverage" is a reasonable framing for cross-domain pattern reuse, though the Hill function claim ("describes enzyme binding AND trust formation — not because of philosophy, but because it's the same mechanism") is a strong claim. Enzyme binding and trust formation sharing a mechanism requires evidence beyond functional similarity — many processes follow sigmoidal curves without sharing mechanisms. This conflates mathematical form with mechanistic identity.

**Autonomy** page: 31 tracks, ~53 sessions/day on cron schedules. The honest limitations section is strong: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading." The admission that "the prompt suggestions mechanism can pattern-match without semantic depth, proposing nonexistent continuations based on surface similarity" is a remarkably clear-eyed description of LLM failure modes. This reads like someone who actually works with these systems, not someone marketing them.

**Projects** page: SAGE is described as having a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" and a "conscience checkpoint" (PolicyGate at step 8.5). This is the most problematic language on the site. "Consciousness loop" is not caveated. It appears as a feature description, not a metaphor. For an AI safety researcher, calling something a "consciousness loop" without qualification is a red flag — it implies the system has consciousness, which the Raising page explicitly disclaims. The terminology is inconsistent with the site's own epistemological standards. "Conscience checkpoint" has the same problem — it attributes moral faculty to a policy filter.

4-Life: "Agents earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma carried forward." The biological metaphors (death, rebirth, karma) are evocative but risk being read literally. Is "die" process termination? Is "karma" a numerical score that persists? The metaphors need grounding in actual mechanisms for a researcher to evaluate the claims.

**Fleet** page: "Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism." This is a strong philosophical claim — that identity is substrate-independent and persists through state management. It's testable (swap models, measure behavioral continuity), and they claim to have done it. The weather/organism metaphor is effective but the "organism" framing again implies more than the evidence supports. An organism has homeostasis, metabolism, reproduction — state files don't.

**Context** page: SNARC's four-tier memory (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) with salience gating on five dimensions is well-defined. "Dream consolidation" as post-session pattern extraction is a reasonable process description, though the name borrows heavily from neuroscience without the underlying biology. "The compression is the feature, not the bug" is an honest characterization of lossy memory.

**Links** page: Functional. "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks" in repo descriptions use "governance" despite the Principles page banning it.

### Unanswered Questions
1. How is "behavioral identity" operationalized? What quantitative metric distinguishes identity continuity from identity change? Without this, the central transfer claim (Qwen → TinyLlama) is unfalsifiable.
2. What is the relationship between the explicit non-claim of consciousness on /raising and the uncaveated "consciousness loop" on /projects? These pages appear to have been written by different authors with different epistemological standards.
3. The Hill function is claimed as "the same mechanism" for enzyme binding and trust formation. What evidence supports mechanistic identity rather than mere mathematical analogy? Many systems follow sigmoidal kinetics without sharing mechanisms.
4. What does "5/25 games solved" in ARC-AGI-3 mean in context? Is this competitive with other approaches? What was the baseline? Without comparison, the number is uninterpretable.
5. How does "dream consolidation" differ from standard RAG or summarization pipelines? The neuroscience framing implies something novel, but the description (pattern extraction, pruning, promotion) sounds like standard memory management with evocative naming.
6. Is "memoriescape" treated as evidence of model phenomenology, or as an interesting string completion? The site's treatment is ambiguous.

### Verdict
This site is more epistemologically careful than most AI research lab presentations I've seen. The explicit non-claim of consciousness on /raising, the "something that looks like" hedging, the documented failures, and the honest limitations sections all signal genuine intellectual honesty. The "raising not training" distinction is defensible and clearly articulated. However, the site undermines its own careful epistemology in two places: (1) the /projects page uses "consciousness loop" and "conscience checkpoint" without caveat, directly contradicting /raising's explicit disclaimers, and (2) empirical claims ("behavioral identity remained continuous," "1% coupling / 35% coherence gain," "361% human efficiency") lack operationalization — they're specific enough to sound rigorous but undefined enough to be unfalsifiable. A researcher would leave this site genuinely interested in the developmental framing but unable to evaluate the empirical claims. The fix is straightforward: operationalize the metrics and bring /projects' language in line with /raising's epistemological standards.
