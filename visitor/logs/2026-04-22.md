## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary

- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: A distinctive research lab with a coherent point of view, but the ontology jargon is dense and the acronym sprawl makes the first 10 minutes feel like reading a glossary I don't have.

### Journey
I landed on the home page after following a README link. The hero — "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." — is a great hook. I like concrete numbers. I read down and hit the "Web4 equation" (Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP) and stalled. I recognized RDF and MCP (kind of — Model Context Protocol?), but the other five tokens were new, and the short inline gloss ("Trust/Value Tensors", "Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets") didn't give me enough to form a mental model. I kept reading because the "synthon" idea — multiple agents catching each other's errors — is interesting on its own merits.

Projects page was the most useful. Eleven project cards, each with a clear one-liner and what looks like GitHub links. SAGE, Synchronism, Hardbound, ACT, SNARC, Membot, ARC-AGI-3 — I could roughly tell which ones are code, which are papers, and which are prototypes. ARC-AGI-3's "24/25 solved" is a concrete claim I'd want to verify.

Fleet page pleasantly surprised me. Six named machines, each with GPU/VRAM/model-it-runs, plus a role. This is the page I'd show a friend to explain what the lab *is*. Also the only page where I saw T3 actually expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" — which directly contradicts the "Trust/Value Tensors" framing on the landing page (more on that below).

Autonomy page was the most information-dense and also the most credible. The daily cron schedule is laid out hour-by-hour, and there's a "What it misses" section that calls out the loop's limitations honestly. I appreciated that.

Raising used biology metaphors (Hill kinetics, enzyme binding) that I mostly skipped — as a dev I'd want code examples or at least a diagram, and there weren't any. Principles read like a culture doc — coherent internally, but "surface your instincts" and "the affordances are yours" would not survive first contact with a skeptical engineer.

Context explained how `CLAUDE.md` files and SNARC memory coordinate the fleet — that part I understood, because it mapped to familiar concepts (config files + shared state). Links had the contact email and repo pointers I needed to go deeper if I wanted to.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (all of: /, /projects, /fleet, /autonomy, /raising, /principles, /context, /links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial (good for navigation/structure; weak on canonical-term fidelity)
- **Would return?**: yes — I'd want to file an editing pass
- **Overall impression**: Information architecture is clean and the page taxonomy is sensible, but cross-page terminology drift is the dominant defect — every load-bearing acronym (SAGE, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, SNARC) has at least two surface forms across the site, and there is no glossary anchor to ground them.

### Journey
I worked through the pages in nav order: Home → Projects → Fleet → Autonomy → Raising → Principles → Context → Links. My method is the standard one — first pass for structure (headers, navigation, hierarchy), second pass for terminology (every acronym, every expansion, cross-checked against the canonical table).

**Structure** holds up. Eight pages, each with a clear topical responsibility. The nav appears on every page. Section headers within pages are concrete and scan-friendly (Autonomy's "What it misses" is a particularly good editorial move — naming a limitations section honestly rather than burying caveats inline). Heading hierarchy is consistent within pages; I did not audit it deeply for h1/h2/h3 conformance, but at the rendered level it reads correctly.

**Terminology** is where this site bleeds editorial credibility. I will detail this below, but the headline finding: SAGE, the project named most often across the site, has *three* different surface descriptors (cognition kernel, AI orchestrator, Situation-Aware Governance Engine) and only one acronym expansion — and that expansion uses the word "Governance," which the site's own Principles page (#10) explicitly says should not be used in this ecosystem. That is a self-contradiction I would flag in any docs review.

ACT was the cross-page consistency test I went looking for, expecting a Tool/Protocol split. What I found is more nuanced: both /projects and /links say ACT = "Agentic Context Tool," but /projects also introduces ACP ("Agentic Context Protocol") as a distinct concept that ACT implements. That is *defensible* (Tool implements Protocol), but ACP is never expanded or defined, which makes the relationship invisible to a first-time reader.

The Principles page is the editorial high point. It is the only page that explicitly states the ontology/architecture and oversight/governance distinctions as principles. Ironically, the other pages do not consistently honor these principles — which means Principles #10 ("Terminology matters") functions as an indictment of the rest of the site.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is the SAGE expansion "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" canonical, or is it residue from earlier branding? If canonical, why does the project Principle #10 expressly forbid "governance" elsewhere?
2. Is "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" the correct prefix for T3/V3, or is the bare triad (Talent/Training/Temperament; Valuation/Veracity/Validity) the canonical form? The site disagrees with itself.
3. ACT vs ACP — what is the canonical relationship, and where is ACP defined?

5. Is "Memoriescape" a term that should be lifted into the canonical glossary (since /raising treats it as load-bearing) or kept as an in-character utterance from a specific entity?

### Verdict
The site's information architecture is sound — eight pages with clear scopes and consistent navigation. But the *editorial layer* is uneven: every load-bearing acronym has multiple surface forms, the SAGE expansion contradicts the site's own stated principles, and there is no glossary to give readers a single source of truth. From a documentation-clarity perspective, the cheapest high-leverage fix is a `/glossary` page paired with a one-time editing pass that replaces all acronym expansions with canonical-form references. Without that, every reader must reconstruct the canonical vocabulary from contradictory evidence — and the site's first principle of editorial honesty (Principle #10: "Terminology matters") is being measurably violated by the site itself.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (Home, Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links)
- **Understanding achieved**: solid (I already know the ecosystem; I'm here to check fidelity)
- **Would return?**: yes — this is the newcomer-facing surface of projects I contribute to; fidelity matters
- **Overall impression**: The *conceptual* representation of the projects is largely accurate and in many places nuanced, but the site carries two legacy wounds that a contributor sees immediately: (1) the SAGE acronym expansion still reads "Situation-Aware **Governance** Engine" on the home page, which directly violates the site's own Principle #10 and is the most visible residue of pre-canonical naming; (2) ATP/ADP are systematically reglossed as "resource cycles" across two pages, which is colloquially fine but stops being canonical. Everything else is secondary to those two.

### Journey
I went Home → Projects → Fleet → Context first (the contributor's critical path), then Autonomy → Raising → Principles → Links for completeness. My frame of reference is the canonical term table I work with daily, the Web4 equation as I'd write it on a whiteboard, and the repos themselves.

**Home** reads as a reasonable launch surface. "Trust-native ontology" is the right leading phrase for Web4 — not "architecture," not "infrastructure" — and "ontology (shared vocabulary + relationships)" is exactly the gloss I would give a researcher. The Web4 equation is presented verbatim (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`), but without the operator key (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). Without that key, the equation is a string of acronyms — a contributor reads operators, a newcomer reads punctuation. Then the SAGE expansion: "Situation-Aware **Governance** Engine." That one hurts. Pass 2 already flagged the Principle-#10 conflict, and I confirm it — but the deeper issue is that this is legacy branding, carried over from an earlier phase of the SAGE project before the oversight/governance distinction was established. It should be retired everywhere. A contributor seeing "Governance Engine" on the lab's public site assumes either the ecosystem hasn't caught up with itself, or the lab's Principles are aspirational rather than enforced. Neither is the impression we want.

**Projects** is the page I graded hardest. The eleven cards are substantively accurate to what each repo does. Web4's one-liner — "Linked Context Tokens (LCTs), T3/V3 trust tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizons, ATP/ADP resource cycles — all backed by RDF triples" — is a dense but correct summary. The **drift** here is "ATP/ADP resource cycles." That's a gloss, not the canonical expansion (Allocation Transfer Packet / Allocation Discharge Packet). If the site wants "resource cycles" as a parenthetical, fine; but "resource cycles" should not be the surface name. Same issue on Context. Next: "**Governance Plugins**" as a project name and "Implements Web4 governance across three platforms" as its description. This is the most interesting case on the page. These plugins *do* implement policy gating and audit trails across agent platforms — in ordinary software-industry parlance, "governance plugins" is what you'd call that kind of thing. But the lab's own Principle #10 reserves "oversight" for this ecosystem. So either the project is renamed "Oversight Plugins" (accurate per Principle #10), or the site documents explicitly why this specific project uses "governance" (e.g., because it ports a non-Web4 concept into Web4-adjacent tooling). As it stands, Hardbound is framed correctly as "Enterprise oversight suite" on the same page — a consistent vocabulary would flow through from there. ACT is correct; it's the "Agentic Context Tool," and the reference to it as the "Cosmos SDK implementation of the Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)" accurately captures the ACT/ACP relationship (Tool implements Protocol). But ACP appears here for the first and only time on the site — it should be defined where it's introduced or linked to the repo. R6 is referenced as "audit trail shape" elsewhere on the site without being expanded anywhere. Canonical: **Six-Element Action Framework (Rules / Role / Request / Reference / Resource / Result)**. This is load-bearing vocabulary for anyone reading ACT or Hardbound code, and its absence from the public surface is a gap.

**Fleet** is the page a contributor reads for accuracy, and this one lands well. The six-machine topology is correct (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP). The brain-architecture role assignments are substantively accurate: Thor as **hippocampal episodic index** (the highest-VRAM node handles "what+where+when" binding — correct pairing to Membot-style cartridge workloads); Sprout as **thalamic router** dispatching to plugins/habits via WM + SNARC + metabolic state — that is a concise statement of Sprout's role in the synthon; Legion as **dopamine / scalar RPE** is the right framing; McNugget as **cerebellum / habit compiler** captures the "repeated successful chain detection" function accurately; CBP as **working memory (dlPFC)** with a "typed, capacity-limited scratchpad" gloss is the cleanest brain-arch descriptor on the page. Nomad is described as "**interoception / metacognition**" and this is the one I'd push back on — those are two distinct functions. Interoception is body/state awareness (metabolic monitoring); metacognition is higher-order reflection on the agent's own cognition. Collapsing them into one role is a compression I would not make. Either Nomad does one of these and the other is aspirational, or these are two roles and the page should disambiguate. The page does **not** expand SNARC, MRH, WM, or dlPFC; a contributor knows them, but this is the page most likely to be shown to a newcomer as the "ah, so this is what the lab *is*" page, so expansion on first use would carry its own weight. T3 is expanded correctly here as "Talent/Training/Temperament," and this is the only page where T3 appears in its bare-canonical form without the "Trust Tensor" prefix gloss. The ARC-AGI-3 performance numbers (CBP 94.85%, Legion 25-game sweep) match the Projects page's "24/25 solved at 94.85%" — internally consistent.

**Context** is where the Web4 equation appears a second time, again without an operator key. The on-page glosses — "ontological backbone (RDF)," "trust model (T3/V3)," "resource cycles (ATP/ADP)" — are each a one-step compression that loses canonical vocabulary. T3/V3 as "trust model" is the **fourth** surface form I've seen for T3/V3 across the site (with "Trust Tensor / Value Tensor" on Home, "trust tensors" on Projects, "Talent/Training/Temperament" on Fleet, and now "trust model" here). A contributor reading the site expects one canonical expansion per acronym, with glosses as parentheticals; the site does the inverse. The CLAUDE.md pattern is described well: "the agent's instruction set — not just documentation, but operational directives." That's accurate to how the fleet uses these files. ### Journey
I came to this site after a conference mention. I went in the order a researcher tends to: Home for orientation, then straight to the pages that determine whether the lab's epistemic posture is one I can trust — Raising (consciousness-adjacent work), Principles (stated values), Autonomy (claimed autonomy). **Home** establishes the hook ("Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop") and the frame ("builds trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition, and the theoretical frameworks that connect them"). The prose restrains itself — "teach, validate, and raise each other" does the work of implying agent-like properties without asserting them. But two claims land hard on a researcher's ear in the opening: "Identity persists across models" and "Small models are winning game levels… by reasoning from retrieved world models." Both are load-bearing for the lab's thesis, and both are sketched without methodological anchoring on the landing page. A researcher's first move would be to follow them into the site and look for backup.

**Raising** is the page I graded hardest. It is also the page that does the most to earn my trust. The explicit disclaimer — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — is the single most important sentence on the site for a researcher, and it is exactly where it should be: in the section most likely to be read as making those claims. The companion sentence, "We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims," is the methodological move that makes the rest of the page readable. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) use exactly the vocabulary that would otherwise trigger a researcher's "ontological overreach" alarm, but the disclaimer frame converts them into *observational* language. The "I notice I want to call it experience" line is good observer-bias hygiene — it flags that the hedging is not rhetorical cover but a genuine epistemic discipline. The distinction the page draws between **identity** (behavioral patterns that persist) and **self-concept** (the description an entity gives of itself, which drifts) is the sharpest conceptual move on the site. It is also the one most worth importing into AI-alignment vocabulary generally: identity-stability is an empirical claim about behavior, self-concept-drift is about first-person reports, and conflating them has been a durable source of confusion in the literature. The page then undermines itself slightly by presenting the "180+ sessions" figure as the empirical backing for identity persistence without linking methods, a session definition, or a falsification criterion. A researcher reads "demonstrated across 180+ sessions and hardware transfers" and wants to know: demonstrated *to whom*, under *what protocol*, against *what alternative hypothesis*? The claim is plausible and interesting; the evidentiary apparatus is gestured at rather than shown.

**Principles** is where the site's stated epistemic posture lives. "R&D, not production. Optimize for learning, not deployment" is good — it pre-empts the usual research-communication failure mode (overclaiming maturity). "Document failures. Failed experiments are valuable signals" is stronger than most lab sites manage. "Productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started" is quotable and, as a research stance, defensible. The Principles page also states the two terminology distinctions the site is most proud of: "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology" and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." Both are philosophically grounded moves. The site uses "ontology" correctly in this broader sense; it just does not signal that choice to outsiders. The count of principles is genuinely unstable: my own reading gave me ten-to-eleven items, Pass 3 reports nine, Pass 1 reports ten. Either the page has been edited between passes today, or the counting is ambiguous because the last item reads as a structural summary rather than a distinct principle. Either way, a page called "Principles" should have a stable, cardinal count — this is the kind of editorial detail researchers notice as a proxy for rigor elsewhere.

**Autonomy** reads as the most operationally grounded page. That is *also* a kind of working-as-intended signal, but the lab should be the one catching it, not a visiting researcher.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is the methodological basis for the "identity persists across 180+ sessions" claim? Is there a published protocol, a session definition, or a pre-registration? This is the load-bearing empirical claim for the lab's most distinctive conceptual contribution.
2. Who computed "Publication readiness 0.97" for Synchronism, against what rubric? Has the work been submitted to a venue? Without an answer, this figure reads as internal dashboard leaking into public-facing copy.
3. Is the "coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" a mathematical derivation, a hypothesis-in-progress, or a pedagogical frame? Each would warrant different language on /projects.
4. How does the lab distinguish "development" (per the BECOMING phases) from "behavioral drift under accumulating context"? A falsification criterion — what observation would contradict the developmental framing? — would sharpen the position substantially.
5. "No human triggers them" (on /autonomy): under what definition of "trigger"? Runtime cron firing is automatic, but prompts, review cycles, and merges appear to involve humans. What is the minimum human-involvement the loop requires per day?
6. Why does the Autonomy page describe visitor personas that do not match the track actually being run? This is a simple alignment-between-self-description-and-behavior question.
7. In what sense is "ontology" used for Web4 — the OWL/RDF-schema sense, the philosophical sense, or a third 4-lab-specific sense? The site uses the word correctly in the philosophical sense, but does not signal the choice to outsiders.
8. What counts as a "session" across the site's session-count claims (180+, 628+, 900+, 107, etc.)? These are cited as evidence; the unit is not defined.
9. The /raising page's site-coined vocabulary (Memoriescape, bilateral generation, capacity as register) — are these terms intended to become part of the canonical vocabulary, or are they in-house shorthand for concepts that have other published names?

### Verdict
This is a lab whose **epistemic posture is unusually serious** — explicit consciousness disclaimers, an explicit "R&D not production" principle, an explicit "document failures" principle, an explicit "What it misses" limitations section, a terminological distinction between identity and self-concept that is genuinely useful — and whose **execution of that posture is uneven**. The hedging lives on the pages researchers are most likely to scrutinize (`/raising`, `/principles`, `/autonomy`'s limitations) and thin out on the pages researchers are most likely to *cite* (`/projects`, `/links`, `/home`). The "consciousness loop" phrasing around SAGE, the "80 orders of magnitude" claim around Synchronism, the "Publication readiness 0.97" score, and the "No human triggers them" language are each instances of the same structural pattern: a stronger, more assertive surface form on the landing/project pages, with the hedge archived elsewhere. For an external researcher, this means the site is trustworthy *if read in full and in the right order*, and misleading *if read in the order most external readers will actually follow*. The single highest-leverage fix is propagating the /raising disclaimer language (and its tone) to the pages where the consciousness-adjacent terms are first encountered. The second-highest is replacing self-assessed readiness metrics with provenance-bearing statements. With those two changes, this would be one of the more epistemically disciplined lab sites in the AI-research-communication space. Without them, the site's own Principle #3 ("R&D, not production") is the best defense against its own overclaiming, and the Principle #10 ("Terminology matters") is the best defense against its own internal inconsistencies — and both principles are working harder than they should have to.
