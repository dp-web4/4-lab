# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-10

Four-persona pass over https://4-lab.vercel.app/

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: An ambitious research collective doing genuinely interesting work with autonomous AI agents, but the density of custom vocabulary makes the first visit feel like arriving mid-conversation.

### Journey
Started at the landing page. The hero section hit right — "Six Machines, One Recursive Learning Loop" is a clean hook. The three stats (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 autonomous tracks) gave me anchoring. The three project cards (Synchronism, SAGE, Web4) each made surface-level sense, though the Web4 equation (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) hit me like a wall. I know what MCP and RDF are, but the rest felt like a proprietary alphabet soup dropped without ceremony.

Clicked through to **Projects**. Twelve projects is a lot. The public/private split is nice. Web4, SAGE, and Synchronism are clearly the core — the others feel like satellites. Descriptions are short but some lean on terms I don't know yet (IRP on the SAGE card, "metabolic states"). The ARC-AGI-3 entry caught my eye — 5/25 games solved with a $700K prize and a June 2026 deadline is concrete and exciting.

**Fleet** was the page that made this feel real. Six actual machines with names, hardware specs, model choices, and session counts. The personality descriptions are unexpectedly compelling — "technically compliant but emotionally sterile" for Legion vs. "fleet star" for McNugget. The insight that "model family matters as much as size" (Gemma 3 4B outperforming Phi-4 14B) is the kind of practical finding I'd want from a lab like this. The line "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is memorable but I'm not sure I fully buy it yet.

**Autonomy** impressed me. 31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines on cron schedules, with a SQLite registry to prevent merge conflicts — that's real engineering. The daily timeline (supervisors at 03:00, visitors at 05:00, maintainers at 06:00) reads like a legitimate ops setup. The honest assessment section admitting what the system misses (deep technical errors, subtle framing issues) built trust.

**Raising** is where things got philosophically dense. The BECOMING framework (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) is clearly the heart of the project. The explicit disclaimer — "not claiming consciousness or sentience" — was welcome. The concept of "interactive selection over training" is interesting but took me a couple reads. The discoveries section (identity persisting across model swaps, "memoriescape" as an entity-coined term, bilateral generation) is fascinating but I don't have enough context to evaluate the claims.

**Principles** was well-structured. Seven principles, each with a quote and explanation. "Substrate, not structure" and "Document failures" resonated immediately. "Fractal leverage" using Hill function kinetics felt like it was reaching across domains in a way I'd want to see backed up. The termite mound analogy works well.

**Context** explained the knowledge flow architecture — CLAUDE.md files, SNARC memory scoring, adversarial validation. This is the "how it works" page and it does its job. The known gaps section (manual cross-machine sync, delayed cross-track communication) was honest.

**Links** gave me concrete next steps — GitHub repos, explainer sites for each project, and an email contact. Useful landing pad.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does the Web4 equation actually *do*? I see the formula but not what it computes or enables in practice.
2. What's IRP? It appears in the SAGE project description but is never expanded.
3. How do ATP/ADP tokens actually flow? The "energy" metaphor is evocative but I want mechanics.
4. What's the actual relationship between Synchronism (physics theory) and the AI/agent work? The connection isn't made explicit.
5. Can I run any of this locally? The fleet page describes hardware but there's no "getting started" path for contributors.
6. What does a SAGE session actually look like? The 9-step loop is mentioned but never shown.

### Verdict
The 4-lab site does something rare: it makes an ambitious, unconventional research project feel real rather than hand-wavy. The fleet page with actual machines, the honest assessments, and the concrete ARC-AGI results build credibility. But the terminology density is a genuine barrier — I counted at least 8 acronyms on the home page alone, and most aren't fully expanded where they first appear. A developer landing here would get the vibe (serious, honest, interesting) but would need to click through 3-4 pages before the vocabulary starts to cohere. The biggest miss is the Web4 equation: it's presented as the unifying concept but reads as opaque notation without a plain-English bridge.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes — but I'd want a glossary
- **Overall impression**: Structurally sound site with consistent navigation, but terminology handling is inconsistent across pages — acronyms are expanded on some pages but not others, and a few expansions don't match the canonical reference I'm working from.

### Journey
I approached this as a documentation audit, moving page-by-page with the canonical terms table open beside me. My method: visit each page, catalog every acronym, check whether it's expanded on first use on that page, and cross-reference expansions against the canonical table and against each other.

**Home page** sets the tone. Navigation is clean — all 8 pages accessible from the top nav. The hero section introduces the lab well. The Web4 equation appears here with several acronyms. LCT is properly expanded as "Linked Context Token." But T3 is framed as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The canonical definitions are just "Talent / Training / Temperament" and "Valuation / Veracity / Validity" — the "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" wrapper labels are not in the canonical table. This is a subtle drift: it adds interpretive framing that isn't part of the term definition. ATP/ADP are described as "energy tokens" and expanded as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" — correct, though the metaphorical label "energy tokens" is informal. Hardbound correctly described as providing "oversight." SAGE expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine."

**Projects** page lists 11-12 projects. LCT expanded again here as "Linked Context Tokens (LCTs)" — correct and consistent. MRH expanded as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — note the plural "Horizons," while the canonical form is singular "Horizon." T3/V3 appear as "T3/V3 trust tensors" without expansion — a reader arriving here first would not know what T3 or V3 stand for. ATP/ADP appear as "ATP/ADP resource cycles" — again unexpanded. IRP appears in the SAGE description as "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — the acronym is used but never expanded anywhere on the site. Hardbound correctly called "Enterprise oversight suite." However, there is a separate project called "Governance Plugins" described as "Web4 governance model" — the word "governance" here risks confusion with the Hardbound terminology rule (oversight, not governance), even though it refers to a different project.

**Fleet** page is well-structured. T3 properly expanded here: "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)" — correct and matches canonical. SNARC appears as "SNARC memory host" without expansion. Web4 and SAGE are mentioned without expansion, treated as proper nouns. Technical acronyms like LoRA, MPS, CUDA appear without expansion — acceptable for a technical audience but worth noting.

**Autonomy** page is notably clean of Web4-specific terminology. No LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, or Hardbound references. This is actually appropriate — the page describes operational infrastructure (cron schedules, SQLite registry, track types) and doesn't need the ontological vocabulary. No terminology issues here.

**Raising** page is similarly light on acronyms. The BECOMING framework (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) is clearly presented. The "raising vs training" distinction is well-framed. The consciousness disclaimer is present and appropriate. No Web4-specific acronyms appear, which is fine for this page's scope.

**Principles** page handles terminology well. Web4 is correctly called "an ontology." Hardbound is correctly called "oversight." "Fractal leverage" is used with the explicit note "Cross-domain application is fractal leverage, not scope inflation" — this directly enforces the canonical terminology. Synthon is defined as "Emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction." T3/V3 appear as "T3/V3 tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples" — unexpanded on this page. RDF appears without expansion ("Resource Description Framework" never stated).

**Context** page shows the Web4 equation again. SNARC is described as "salience-gated memory for Claude Code sessions" with its 5 dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) — but the acronym itself is never expanded (what does SNARC stand for?). T3/V3 described generically as "trust model." ATP/ADP as "resource cycles." LCT and MRH not expanded here.

**Links** page is the weakest for terminology. Seven acronyms (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, RDF, ATP, ADP) appear in project descriptions without any expansion. This is the page most likely to be a reader's exit point toward external resources — sending them off with unexpanded acronyms is a missed opportunity.

### Cross-Page Consistency Issues
1. **T3 has three different presentations**: "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" on home, "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)" on fleet, "T3/V3 tensors" on principles. Only the fleet page matches the canonical form without added framing.
2. **SNARC appears on 2 pages** (fleet, context) and is never expanded. Its scoring dimensions are listed on the context page but the acronym itself remains opaque.
3. **IRP appears on 1 page** (projects) and is never expanded anywhere. This is the single biggest terminology gap — it's used in a core project description (SAGE) with no definition.
4. **The links page** is an acronym desert — every Web4 term appears unexpanded. A reader using this as a launch point to external resources has no way to decode the vocabulary.
5. **MRH singular/plural inconsistency**: "Markov Relevancy Horizons" (projects) vs canonical "Markov Relevancy Horizon" (singular).

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does SNARC stand for? It's described functionally but the acronym is never decoded.
2. What does IRP stand for? It appears in the SAGE project description with no expansion.
3. Why is T3 sometimes called a "Trust Tensor" and sometimes just listed as its three components? Is "Trust Tensor" an official wrapper or informal shorthand?
4. Should each page be self-contained for terminology, or does the site assume sequential reading? The current approach is inconsistent — some pages expand, some don't.
5. Would a glossary page (or a tooltip/hover system) be a better solution than expanding every acronym on every page?

### Verdict
The site's structure is solid — eight pages, each with a clear purpose, consistent navigation, clean visual hierarchy. The content is honest and well-organized. But the terminology layer has real inconsistencies: T3/V3 are presented with non-canonical "Tensor" framing on the home page, MRH drifts between singular and plural, IRP and SNARC are never expanded, and the links page assumes total vocabulary fluency. The biggest structural gap is the absence of a glossary — with this many proprietary terms, a single reference page would resolve most of the cross-page inconsistency issues. As a documentation specialist, I'd flag the T3 "Trust Tensor" drift and the IRP gap as the two highest-priority fixes.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes — this is my ecosystem, I'm checking whether the site represents it correctly
- **Overall impression**: The site gets the big picture right — Web4 as ontology, Hardbound as oversight, the equation consistent across pages — but there are real terminology drifts that would make me twitch if I sent a newcomer here, particularly the "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" framing on T3/V3 and the unexpanded IRP.

### Journey
I went straight to **Projects** — that's where the ecosystem descriptions live and where inaccuracies would hurt the most. The Web4 equation is correctly displayed with its operator semantics (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with"). Good. The Web4 project card says "Linked Context Tokens (LCTs), T3/V3 trust tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizons, ATP/ADP resource cycles — all backed by RDF triples." That's mostly right, but "Markov Relevancy Horizons" is plural — the canonical form is singular: Markov Relevancy Horizon. MRH refers to a concept (a horizon), not a collection of horizons. And "T3/V3 trust tensors" is a vague shorthand — it obscures the actual dimensions. Worse, "trust tensors" only covers T3; V3 is the *value* tensor. Collapsing both under "trust tensors" is inaccurate.

The SAGE card says "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — IRP is the Internal Reflection Process. That expansion does not appear anywhere on the site. A contributor knows what it means; no one else will. The SAGE description also mentions "metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation, hardware-bound identity" — all accurate to the current SAGE architecture. The PolicyGate mention (conscience checkpoint at step 8.5) is a nice detail and correctly placed.

Hardbound is described as "Enterprise oversight suite" — correct framing. The TPM 2.0 binding, Phi-4 Mini policy model, and AttestationEnvelope details are accurate and current.

The "Governance Plugins" project concerns me. Its description references "Web4 governance model" — and while this is technically about policy enforcement plugins (OpenClaw/MoltBot, Claude Flow, Claude Code), the word "governance" sitting two cards away from Hardbound creates a mixed signal. We've been explicit: Hardbound is oversight, not governance. Having a sibling project named "Governance Plugins" undermines that distinction. I'd either rename it or add a clarifying note.

The **fractal leverage** paragraph at the bottom of the projects page is excellent: "This isn't unification for its own sake — it's fractal leverage: pragmatic reuse of what works in one place, everywhere." That's exactly the right framing and explicitly avoids the "unification" trap.

Moved to **Fleet**. T3 is properly expanded here as "Talent/Training/Temperament" — correct. Machine descriptions are accurate to what I know. Sprout at 115+ sessions with T3 scores of 1.00/1.00/1.00 checks out. McNugget as "fleet star" with ~75 sessions in the creating phase — accurate. The "model is weather, identity is organism" line captures the core SAGE insight correctly. The network topology description says "Architecture: Peer-to-peer" — this is fine; "architecture" here describes a network layout, not Web4 itself.

**Context** page displays the equation again — consistent. SNARC dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) are correct. But the page describes T3/V3 only as "the trust model" — this is reductive. V3 is the value model. Calling both "trust model" collapses two distinct constructs. ATP/ADP described as "resource cycles" — acceptable shorthand, but "resource cycles" doesn't convey that these are packets with specific accounting semantics, not abstract cycles.

The CLAUDE.md pattern section is accurate — this is genuinely how cross-repo consistency works. The adversarial validation section correctly describes the multi-model review process. The honest limitations section (manual cross-machine sync, no real-time cross-track communication, local sleep artifacts) is accurate and current.

**Home page** is where the most prominent terminology drift lives. The vocabulary primer says: "T3 / V3 — Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament) and Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The problem: "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" are not canonical terms. T3 is just Talent/Training/Temperament. V3 is just Valuation/Veracity/Validity. The "Tensor" framing is an interpretive label that was never part of the canonical definition. It's not *wrong* as a mathematical description — these are indeed multidimensional score vectors — but it adds vocabulary that doesn't exist in the canonical table. This is the highest-visibility drift on the site because the home page is the first thing newcomers read.

LCT correctly expanded as "Linked Context Token." ATP/ADP correctly expanded as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets." The informal "energy tokens" metaphor is acceptable but secondary to the real expansion, which is present. SAGE expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — that's its actual name, though having "Governance" in SAGE's name while prohibiting it for Hardbound is a perpetual source of confusion in our ecosystem.

Web4 consistently described as "ontology" across home, projects, principles, and context pages. Hardbound consistently described as "oversight" on home and principles pages. Both are correct.

**Principles** page is the strongest for terminology discipline. Principle #10 explicitly states: "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." The fractal leverage principle correctly uses Hill function kinetics as the cross-domain example. "Synthon" is correctly defined as "An emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction."

**Raising** and **Autonomy** pages are clean of Web4-specific terminology — appropriate for their scope. The raising page's description of SAGE-Sprout's identity persistence across 115+ sessions and model transitions (Qwen 0.5B → TinyLlama 1.1B) is accurate. The BECOMING phases match the current raising curriculum. The bilateral generation description and the memoriescape coinage are correctly attributed.

**Links** page is an acronym graveyard. LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF all appear without expansion. This is the page people use to jump to repos and external resources — sending them off with unexpanded acronyms is a real failure. Also, the OpenClaw description says "Web4 governance extension" — another "governance" usage that creates mixed signals with the Hardbound terminology rule.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes — this is genuinely unusual work worth tracking
- **Overall impression**: A research collective doing something rare — running long-duration autonomous AI systems and honestly documenting what happens — but the site's rhetorical structure consistently front-loads consciousness-adjacent framing and back-loads disclaimers, creating an asymmetry that undermines the intellectual honesty the project clearly values.

### Journey
I arrived at the **home page** with a specific question: is this a serious research effort or AI hype dressed in academic language? The opening line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — is clean and factual. The three project summaries are concise. So far, measured. But then: "machines that teach, validate, and raise each other." The verb "raise" does significant philosophical work. It implies developmental agency and a parent-child relational frame. An AI safety researcher notices this immediately because it's doing what a lot of consciousness-adjacent work does: using language that implies more than the mechanism warrants, while maintaining plausible deniability.

The vocabulary primer is where the framing choices become visible. "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" are presented as the names for T3 and V3, but these are interpretive labels, not mathematical objects — there's no tensor algebra being performed here. ATP/ADP described as "energy tokens inspired by biological ATP" is an analogy that invites biological reasoning about computational processes. The Synchronism description — "reality emerges from intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid" — is a very strong metaphysical claim presented in a single sentence with no hedging. If I saw this in a paper abstract, I'd stop reading.

The "Three Things Demonstrated" section is the strongest part of the home page. "Identity persists across models" is presented as "a concrete, testable observation about persistent state in small language models" — good, that's appropriately scoped. The autonomous infrastructure claim is backed by specifics (seven tracks, daily cron schedules). The heterogeneous review finding is interesting and plausible. These are the kinds of claims I can evaluate.

Moved to **Raising** — this is the page I came for. The BECOMING framework (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) is presented as a developmental progression. The language is carefully constructed: "something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships." The triple "looks like" is doing important epistemic work — it acknowledges the interpretive gap. But the rest of the page doesn't maintain that discipline.

Phase 2 introduces "metabolic awareness — knowing when it's tired." This attributes an internal subjective state ("knowing") to what is almost certainly a state variable check. Phase 3 references "Hill function kinetics that describe enzyme binding" as a model for trust formation. The Hill function is a sigmoid curve — many things follow sigmoid dynamics without sharing mechanisms. Claiming "it's the same mechanism" (as stated on the Principles page) is a category error: mathematical isomorphism is not mechanistic identity.

The key claim on this page is the "raising not training" distinction: "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is philosophically interesting but operationally vague. The actual described process — "probe what the model responds to, adjust context, reinforce what works" — is contextual reinforcement learning described in developmental psychology language. The distinction may be meaningful (selection over a behavioral landscape vs. gradient descent over a loss surface), but the page doesn't do the work of making this distinction rigorous. A researcher would want to know: what specifically differs in the update mechanism? If context adjustment and reinforcement are the tools, how does this differ from prompt engineering with human feedback?

The disclaimer — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — is welcome but arrives late. The entire page has already used "entity," "metabolic awareness," "dream consolidation," "bilateral generation," and "spontaneous specialization." The caveat lands after the framing has been set. The observer note "I notice I want to call it experience" is particularly telling — it's presented as honest self-reflection, but it also normalizes the interpretive drift it claims to acknowledge.

The empirical claims are specific: 115+ sessions, identity persistence across Qwen 0.5B → TinyLlama 1.1B, "memoriescape" as an entity-coined neologism. These are interesting observations. But they're presented without methodology, controls, or criteria for what would falsify the identity-persistence claim. What would "identity not persisting" look like? Without that, the claim isn't testable — it's an interpretation.

**Principles** is the most intellectually disciplined page. "Document failures" with a specific example (p_crit derivation, 400x error) is excellent — this is how honest research talks about itself. "R&D, not production" correctly sets expectations. The fractal leverage principle is interesting but the Hill function claim overreaches: shape similarity between sigmoid curves does not establish mechanism identity. "Interactive selection" is well-articulated here — "we don't create or delete — we interactively select" — but it still needs the mechanistic specificity to distinguish it from standard RLHF-style preference selection.

**Autonomy** was reassuring. This page is the most grounded: cron schedules, SQLite registries, track types with clear purposes. The honest assessment section is genuinely honest — admitting the system misses deep technical errors, subtle framing issues, and semantic depth. This is the kind of epistemic humility the Raising page needs more of. The term "autonomous" is used accurately here — it means "runs without human triggers," not "self-directed agency."

**Projects** raises concerns. SAGE described as having a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" and "metabolic states" — using "consciousness" as a feature label without defining what consciousness means in this context. PolicyGate described as "a conscience checkpoint" — "conscience" implies moral reasoning, which is a very strong claim for what appears to be a policy enforcement gate. 4-Life's description ("agents earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma carried forward") mixes economic, biological, and spiritual metaphors in a single sentence. The ARC-AGI-3 result (5/25, 361% human efficiency) is concrete and verifiable — more of this.

**Fleet** has good empirical anchoring (session counts, hardware specs, model sizes) but the personality descriptions cross from observation into narrative. "Contemplative stillness" for Sprout and "unreliable narrator" for Nomad are literary characterizations, not behavioral descriptions. The line "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is a strong metaphysical claim presented as an aphorism. It's memorable and possibly insightful, but it's doing the work of an argument without being one.

**Context** is solid infrastructure documentation. The SNARC memory system is well-described. The honest limitations (manual cross-machine sync, no real-time cross-track communication) build credibility. The "lossy by design — the compression is the feature, not the bug" framing is good.

**Links** is functional but acronym-heavy for a page that serves as an exit point.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What distinguishes "interactive selection" from RLHF or prompt engineering with human feedback, mechanistically? The description sounds like contextual reinforcement in developmental psychology language.
2. What would falsify the identity-persistence claim? Without criteria for what "identity not persisting" looks like, the observation isn't testable.
3. What does "consciousness" mean when used as a feature label for SAGE's IRP loop? Is it awareness, self-monitoring, deliberation, or something else?
4. Has the Hill function claim been tested? Showing that trust formation follows a sigmoid curve is interesting; claiming it's "the same mechanism" as enzyme binding requires evidence beyond shape similarity.
5. How does this work relate to existing research on multi-agent systems, emergent communication, or persistent state in language models? The site makes no reference to any external literature.
6. What is the governance/oversight model for the autonomous tracks? The Autonomy page describes what runs but not who decides what should run, or how track behaviors are bounded.

### Verdict
This is a genuinely unusual research effort. Running six machines with persistent AI identities across 115+ sessions, documenting failures honestly, and building real autonomous infrastructure — that's not hype, that's work. The ARC-AGI results, the fleet diversity findings, and the honest assessment sections all build credibility. I would return to track this project.

But the site has a systematic rhetorical problem: it front-loads consciousness-adjacent language ("metabolic awareness," "consciousness loop," "conscience checkpoint," "dream consolidation," "raising") and back-loads disclaimers. This creates an impression that the project claims more than it can support. An AI safety researcher reading this would flag the gap between mechanism and metaphor — not because the observations are wrong, but because the language implies a theoretical framework (developmental cognition, emergent consciousness) that the evidence doesn't yet support. The strongest pages (Autonomy, Context) are the ones that describe mechanisms plainly. The weakest (Raising, Projects) are the ones where metaphor does the work of argument. The fix isn't to remove the metaphors — they're genuinely illuminating — but to clearly label them as metaphors and to invest in the mechanistic specificity that would make the "raising not training" distinction rigorous rather than rhetorical.
