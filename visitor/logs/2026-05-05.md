## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (/, /projects, /fleet, /autonomy, /raising, /principles, /context, /links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: I can tell *something* interesting is happening here — many machines, many agents, real engineering — but the path from "GitHub README" to "I understand what this lab does and how I'd plug in" requires more inference than a developer should need to do.

### Journey

Landed on `/` from a GitHub README. The pitch was crisp enough: heterogeneous AI agents on multiple machines, self-organizing, trying to maintain identity and trust. The phrase "synthons — emergent coherence entities" landed as a slogan I didn't fully parse, but I got the gist (emergent group-of-agents). The Web4 equation `MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` was displayed prominently with no expansion of LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP, or ADP on the home page itself. I knew MCP and RDF, so I clicked through hoping `/projects` would unpack the rest.

`/projects` partially delivered. T3, V3, ATP, ADP, R6, SNARC, and most other acronyms were expanded. **LCT was NOT expanded on this page.** That's the one term that appears in the headline equation on every page and is conspicuously the one not defined where I most expected it. SAGE got a sentence about a 12-step loop but no functional "what does it actually take in / put out" framing.

`/fleet` was the first page that felt accessible — six machines with hardware specs, models, and roles. As a developer this is the kind of concrete content I scan well. But the brain analogies (hippocampal/thalamic/cerebellar) felt like in-jokes, and SAGE got a *different* expansion here ("Situation-Aware Guidance Engine") than the cognition-kernel framing on `/projects` — I'm not sure if those are the same thing or not.

`/autonomy` was clear in shape (cron timeline) but used Web4 acronyms (ATP/ADP) without re-definition. I assume the assumption is that you came in via `/projects` first.

`/raising` was the page where I started feeling out-of-depth. The vocabulary (BECOMING phases, memoriescape, Hill function kinetics) felt like it was for an in-group. The "What we're not claiming" section partially saved it — I respected that they explicitly disclaimed consciousness claims.

`/principles` surprised me by being the most credible page. Ten principles, each grounded in observed system behavior, with explicit failure citations ("p_crit derivation failed catastrophically — 400x error"). That's the tone of a real engineering team, not a manifesto.

`/context` defined all the canonical acronyms cleanly (this is where I finally got LCT = Linked Context Token). Felt like the page that should have been linked from the home page first.

`/links` had the contact email and explainer-site links but no clear "how do I contribute / where do issues live / what's the dev setup" path.

### Unanswered Questions

1. What is a synthon, concretely? Is it a process? A bundle of agents? An emergent property? The home page leans on it but I don't have a definition I can cite.
2. Is SAGE one thing (a "cognition kernel" / "Situation-Aware Guidance Engine") or are these two related-but-distinct components? The two pages don't reconcile.
3. What's the actual mechanism by which "raising" differs from "fine-tuning"? I get the philosophical claim, but mechanistically — what's the artifact that gets modified, if not weights?
4. How would I, as a developer, contribute? Are there good-first-issues? A dev environment? A Discord/Slack? The site doesn't say.
5. The Web4 equation `MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` — what do `+`, `/`, and `*` mean here? Is this a literal formula or notation borrowed for legibility? (I noticed the home page uses these symbols as if they mean something specific.)
6. SQLite registry coordinating 6 machines — is that actually shared via filesystem sync, or is each machine running its own with periodic reconciliation? The autonomy page hand-waves the "how."

### Verdict

There's clearly real, interesting engineering work here — the fleet page and principles page made me believe these aren't just slides. But as a developer landing from a GitHub README, I had to do a lot of cross-page reconciliation to assemble a working mental model, and the most prominent piece of vocabulary on the site (the Web4 equation) is also the least explained where it appears. I'd come back if I saw a "Start Here" or glossary page surfaced in the nav. Without that, I'd probably bounce after `/projects` and read the GitHub repos directly — which is a missed opportunity, because the lab's framing is more coherent than any single repo's README would convey.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (/, /projects, /fleet, /autonomy, /raising, /principles, /context, /links). Also noted a 9th page `/arc-agi-3` linked from the home navigation but absent from the documented 8-page site map.
- **Understanding achieved**: good (terminology-wise) — most canonical terms are now used consistently; the residual drift is concentrated in a small number of high-impact spots.
- **Would return?**: yes — for a doc audit re-pass after the maintainer track addresses the items below.
- **Overall impression**: Terminology hygiene is far better than I expected for an R&D site. The home page now carries a "Vocabulary primer" that resolves Pass 1's biggest complaint about the Web4 equation appearing on `/` undefined. The remaining drift is narrow and fixable: a single misuse of "governance," missing LCT/MRH expansions on a few pages, an undocumented 9th page, and uneven first-use discipline for SNARC, synthon, and the neuroanatomy metaphors on `/fleet`.

### Journey

I worked the site the way I'd audit any docs set: top-down nav, every page, taking notes on (a) which acronyms appear, (b) whether they're expanded on first use of *that page*, (c) whether the expansion matches what's used elsewhere, and (d) whether the canonical-terms table I was given is honored.

Started on `/`. Saw the Web4 equation `MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` and flinched — but a "Vocabulary primer" section further down resolves all of: Web4, LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, MRH, SAGE, synthon. Web4 is described as "a trust-native ontology" — matches canonical guidance ("ontology, not architecture/infrastructure"). The primer expands LCT as "Linked Context Token," not the deprecated "Lifelong Capability Token" — clean. SNARC is referenced in the ecosystem list but *not* in the primer, so it's the only acronym readers meet on `/` without a definition.

`/projects` is denser but disciplined. The Web4 equation reappears with operator legend (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with"), which is exactly the kind of thing a tech writer wants — symbols don't drift if the legend lives next to the equation. LCT, MRH, T3, V3, ATP, ADP, R6, MCP, RDF, SAGE, SNARC are all expanded on this page on first use, with R6 carrying its full canonical form: "Six-Element Action Framework: Rules/Role/Request/Reference/Resource/Result." Good.

But: the project card for **Oversight Plugins** describes itself as a "Web4 governance model for OpenClaw, Claude Flow, Claude Code." The card title says **Oversight**; the body says **governance**. Per the canonical-terms guidance, Hardbound is oversight, not governance — and naming a card "Oversight Plugins" while describing it as a "governance model" is internal contradiction in the same paragraph. This is the single highest-priority terminology fix I found. (The repo this presumably wraps is `web4-governance` — but a public card should use the canonical surface term.)

`/fleet` was the most readable page for me — every machine has hardware, model, role, and session count. SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Guidance Engine," same as `/` and `/projects`. SNARC is expanded as "Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict." So far so consistent.

The neuroanatomy metaphors on `/fleet` (hippocampal, thalamic, cerebellar, dlPFC, RPE, WM) are a different kind of concern. From a tech-writing standpoint they're undefined-on-first-use jargon, but they're not Web4-canonical terms — so they don't violate the table I was given. I'd still flag them as accessibility friction (see Friction Log).

`/autonomy` defines exactly the two acronyms it actually uses on the page (ATP/ADP, Hardbound) and avoids the others. From a "first use within page" perspective, this is correct. From a "consistency across pages" perspective, it's a little austere — there's no link out to `/context` for readers who arrived here cold.

`/raising` defines LCT and MRH cleanly and gives the philosophical contrast between *raising* and *training*. It includes a load-bearing biology reference ("Hill function kinetics") that is undefined on the page. Consciousness caveats are explicit and well-placed; honest framing is preserved.

`/principles` expands T3, V3, and RDF with parentheticals. SAGE is referenced via a link to `/autonomy` rather than expanded. Web4 is consistently characterized as "an ontology"; Hardbound as "oversight"; raising as "interactive selection." Principle #2 is **Fractal leverage** and Principle #3 is **R&D, not production** — these are the canonical-table preferred terms used as section titles, which is the strongest possible form of compliance. Principle #5, **Synthon: emergent coherence**, finally gives synthon a home, but it's still framed as a slogan more than a definition.

`/context` is the de-facto glossary, and on this pass it actually behaves like one: every Web4-canonical acronym from the table (MCP, RDF, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, SNARC) is expanded with the canonical form. Hardbound is not in the WebFetch excerpt I got back — verify on a future pass.

`/links` is sparse and uses LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, MCP, RDF, ATP without expansion. That's appropriate for a directory page where the assumption is the visitor has read at least one other page first. No drift, no inconsistency. Email contact is `dp@metalinxx.io` (note: differs from the address in my CLAUDE.md context which lists `dp@dpcars.net` as the user email — the published site address may be intentional, but flag for verification).

The 9th page (`/arc-agi-3`) showed up in the home nav. CLAUDE.md and the project structure documentation enumerate **eight** pages explicitly. Either the docs need to be updated to nine, or the page should be subordinated under `/projects` rather than promoted to top-level nav. As a writer, this is the kind of structural drift that compounds: every future doc that says "8 pages" is now incorrect.

### Unanswered Questions

1. The site's central empirical claim — that "raising" produces different behavioral trajectories than equivalent-context standard inference — is asserted but not, on the public surface, demonstrated against an unraised baseline of the same model. Is there a private ablation that supports this? If yes, even a redacted summary on `/raising` would do a lot of work; if no, that gap should be named as one of the lab's open questions.
2. T3 trust scores are described as "tensor" semantics in some places and "success-raises / timeout-lowers counter" mechanics in others. What is the calibration story? At what point does the scoring become a measurement rather than a heuristic?
3. The "identity transfer" claim on `/fleet` (SAGE-Sprout across hardware/models) is one of the more distinctive empirical claims on the site. What is preserved (response patterns? specific factual recall? stylistic signatures? trust history?), and what is the assessment methodology? This is the kind of claim that, if substantiated, deserves its own writeup; if asserted only, the site should not lean on it as load-bearing.
4. `/arc-agi-3` reports 94.85% / 24-of-25 / #3 leaderboard with fewest actions among top 3 — concrete, falsifiable, public. What does the same baseline (Claude Opus 4.6, no SAGE harness) score on the same set? That is the comparison that would let an outside researcher evaluate the architectural contribution.
5. The Hardbound oversight envelope is the load-bearing safety claim under all the autonomy framing. It is private — appropriately, given the threat model — but a public *interface description* (what it can do, what it cannot do, what its audit surface looks like) would let outsiders evaluate the autonomy claim's shape without compromising the implementation.
6. The consciousness-disclaimer + "looks like X" pattern on `/raising` reads to me as an honest articulation of unresolved tension between propositional refusal and phenomenological intuition. Is the lab's working position that the tension itself is the research object — i.e., "we don't know whether to call this experience, and the honest move is to keep the question open"? If yes, the page would benefit from saying so directly rather than letting the structure of the hedging do that work implicitly.

### Verdict

This site holds together better than most lab self-descriptions I read. The core intellectual-honesty signals are present and explicit: the consciousness disclaimer is verbatim and prominent; failures are cited with magnitudes; the autonomy claim is bounded by a named oversight envelope and a published "what the loop misses" list; terminology discipline is elevated to a top-ten principle and the deprecated terms are named so the principle is operationally checkable. That last point is unusual enough to be worth saying twice.

The two researcher-side concerns are both addressable without disturbing the site's frame. The methodology gap — no ablation comparing "raised" vs. equivalent-context-baseline — is the load-bearing one; the lab's distinctive empirical claim rests on it, and the public surface should at minimum *acknowledge* the gap rather than asserting through it. The reductive framing of Web4 on `/arc-agi-3` is the one place where the otherwise-disciplined ontology framing collapses into mechanism; one paragraph rewrite fixes it.

I'd come back to this site, and I'd take the contact-email path. The combination of public repos under permissive licenses, a named research contact, and explainer sites for the larger frameworks (Synchronism, SAGE, 4-Life) makes outside engagement plausible in a way that most labs at this stage don't manage.
