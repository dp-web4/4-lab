## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious and genuinely interesting research lab, but the acronym density creates a wall that takes real effort to climb over.

### Journey
Clicked through from what I imagined was a GitHub README link. The landing page hero — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately grabbed me. Clear, punchy, tells me the scale. The three stat cards (6 Machines, 8+ Repos, 7 Autonomous Tracks) gave me a quick sense of scope.

The "How We Work" section was solid: autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. I got the gist — this is a multi-machine AI research lab where different AI agents run on different hardware and collaborate. The vocabulary section at the bottom of the home page was helpful, though some definitions felt circular (more on that in the terminology section).

**Projects** — This is where things got dense. The Web4 equation (`Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) hit me cold. Even with the operator legend, I couldn't parse what this *means* as a developer. I know what RDF and MCP are, but the rest are project-specific acronyms strung together in a formula. The project cards themselves were better — I could tell what SAGE does (on-device AI orchestrator), what Hardbound does (enterprise oversight), and what ACT does (MCP server interaction tool). The competition entry (ARC-AGI-3, 5/25 solved, $700K prize) was a compelling proof-of-work detail.

**Fleet** — This was my favorite page. Concrete hardware specs, real model names, session counts, personality notes. "Technically compliant but emotionally sterile" for Legion made me laugh. The peer-to-peer architecture description and the insight about model family mattering more than size (Gemma 3 at 4B outperforming Phi-4 at 14B for raising work) — that's the kind of finding that would make me dig deeper. The "identity is organism, model is weather" line is memorable.

**Autonomy** — Impressive operational maturity. 31 tracks across 6 machines running daily without human triggers. The timeline from 03:00 supervisors through dream consolidation tells a complete story. But I noticed the home page says "7 Autonomous Tracks" while this page says "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day." I assume 7 track *types* multiply across machines to get 31, but the discrepancy confused me for a moment. Also the visitor personas listed here (The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector) don't match any persona descriptions I can verify — they're just names without context on this page.

**Raising** — The most philosophically ambitious page. "Raising is not training" is a strong framing. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) read like a developmental psychology curriculum adapted for AI. The disclaimer — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — is exactly what I'd want to see. "Memoriescape" is a beautiful coinage. However, terms like "bilateral generation" and "capacity as register" are used without enough context for me to evaluate them.

**Principles** — The heading says "12 Principles" but I only counted 10 in the list (Substrate not structure, Fractal leverage, R&D not production, Trust is a relationship, Synthon: emergent coherence, Document failures, Interactive selection, Surface your instincts, Research value, Terminology matters). Either two are missing or the count is wrong. The principles themselves are well-written — each has a quote and explanation. The termite mound analogy for emergence was effective.

**Context** — The most useful page for understanding how the system actually works. CLAUDE.md as an agent instruction pattern, SNARC memory scoring, cross-session memory, adversarial validation — all concrete and understandable. The honest admission about limitations (cross-machine sync is manual, real-time cross-track communication is a gap) added credibility.

**Links** — Clean and functional. GitHub org, explainer sites for three projects, email contact. Everything I'd need to go deeper.

1. What does "Trust Tensor" (home page) vs "Talent/Training/Temperament" (fleet page) actually mean for T3? These seem like different things.
2. The Web4 equation — is it a formula you compute, a shorthand for components, or a philosophical statement? How do the operators (`/`, `*`, `+`) actually work?

A developer can understand *what* this lab does in 2 minutes, but understanding *how* or *why* takes significantly longer. I'd bookmark it and come back when I had time to dig into the repos.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes (to verify fixes)
- **Overall impression**: Well-structured site with strong conceptual organization, but undermined by acronym inconsistencies across pages, a miscounted principles heading, and several key acronyms that are never expanded anywhere on the site.

### Journey
I approached this audit methodically: home page first, then every nav link left to right, tracking every acronym occurrence and its expansion (or lack thereof) against the canonical terms table I was given.

The **home page** sets the vocabulary tone well — there's an explicit "Vocabulary Primer" section at the bottom that defines Web4, LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon. This is good practice. However, the T3/V3 entry introduces non-canonical wrapper labels ("Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor") that don't appear in my reference table. The components inside (Talent/Training/Temperament and Valuation/Veracity/Validity) are correct, but the wrapper names create confusion — especially when the Fleet page later uses T3 without the "Trust Tensor" framing.

**Projects** is the densest page. The Web4 equation appears with operator definitions, which is helpful. But MCP and RDF — two of the equation's components — are never expanded anywhere on the entire site. A reader encounters `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` and has to already know what MCP and RDF stand for. LCT is expanded on this page ("Linked Context Tokens (LCTs)") — good. MRH gets "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — good. But MCP and RDF get nothing. Also notable: the "Governance Plugins" subsection uses "governance" three times ("Web4 governance model", "governance extension", "governance hooks"), while every other page carefully uses "oversight" for this concept.

**Fleet** was terminologically clean. T3 is expanded as "(Talent/Training/Temperament)" in the trust tracker description — matching the canonical table. The three trust score dimensions for Sprout (1.00/1.00/1.00) now make sense as the T3 components. No V3 expansion appears on this page, which is fine since V3 isn't contextually relevant here.

**Autonomy** has a number discrepancy: "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day" at the top, while the home page's stat card says "7 Autonomous Tracks." The text never reconciles these. Seven *types* of tracks multiplied across machines could yield 31, but the site never explains this. The four visitor personas (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) each get a one-line description here — adequate for context.

**Raising** is notably acronym-light. No Web4-specific terms (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP) appear at all. SAGE is mentioned but only as "SAGE-Sprout" (an entity name). This page is self-contained in its vocabulary, which is appropriate for its philosophical focus. The consciousness disclaimer is well-positioned and clearly worded.

**Principles** has the most significant structural error: the heading says "12 Principles" but the page lists exactly 10. I counted carefully — Substrate not structure, Fractal leverage, R&D not production, Trust is a relationship, Synthon: emergent coherence, Document failures, Interactive selection, Surface your instincts, Research value, Terminology matters. That's 10. Two are either missing or the heading count is wrong. The terminology principle (#10) is excellent — it explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology, not architecture or infrastructure. Hardbound is oversight, not governance." This is the canonical reference point for the whole site's vocabulary.

**Context** describes SNARC as "Salience-Gated Memory" but never expands the acronym itself — what do the letters S-N-A-R-C stand for? The five scoring dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) are listed clearly. The Web4 equation appears again, consistent with /projects. RDF is described as "the ontological backbone" but still never expanded.

**Links** is clean and functional. Descriptions are brief and consistent. However, the OpenClaw and Claude Code fork descriptions both say "Web4 governance" — using "governance" rather than "oversight," contradicting the terminology principle on /principles.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site gets the big picture right — Web4 as ontology, the equation, the ecosystem structure — but "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" is fabricated terminology that doesn't exist in the Web4 spec, "governance" leaks through in five places where we use "oversight," and MCP is never expanded despite being the first term in the equation.

### Journey
I went straight to Projects, then Fleet, then Context — the three pages where canonical accuracy matters most. Then swept the remaining five for drift.

**Projects** — The equation is displayed correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the right operators (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). Good. LCT is expanded as "Linked Context Tokens" — correct. MRH is expanded as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — correct. But T3/V3 is reduced to "trust tensors" without the component breakdown. Someone reading this page doesn't learn that T3 is Talent/Training/Temperament or that V3 is Valuation/Veracity/Validity. Worse, MCP — the *first* term in the equation — is never expanded anywhere on the entire site. RDF gets functional descriptions ("RDF triples," "RDF-backed") but is also never formally expanded. The "Governance Plugins" section is a problem: it uses "governance" three times in a context where we have specifically chosen "oversight." The phrase "This isn't unification for its own sake" is also concerning — even negating "unification" introduces a term we deliberately avoid. The correct framing is "fractal leverage," which does appear elsewhere on this page, so the negation creates a mixed signal.

**Fleet** — Cleanest page terminologically. T3 properly expanded as "(Talent/Training/Temperament)" in the trust tracker section. The defensive trust model description is accurate. The peer-to-peer architecture and the "identity is organism, model is weather" insight are well-represented. However, I noticed the page describes the fleet coordination model using "governance model" — that's another "governance" leak.

**Context** — The equation appears again, consistent. SNARC is described as "salience-gated memory" with the five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) clearly listed. The storage hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) is accurate. The framing "This equation appears in every project because it *is* every project" is exactly right. But no acronyms from the equation are expanded on this page — it assumes the reader already knows them from /projects, where half of them aren't expanded either.

**Home** — This is where the worst drift lives. T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 is labeled "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." There is no such thing as a "Trust Tensor" or "Value Tensor" in the Web4 specification. T3 and V3 are shorthand for their three components — they are not "tensors." This is fabricated terminology that sounds plausible but doesn't exist in any Web4 document, and it will propagate into every newcomer's mental model. This is the highest-severity drift on the site. Also, SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — while this may be the project's actual name, the presence of "Governance" in this expansion on the same page that says "Hardbound provides oversight" creates a confusing signal about when we use "governance" vs "oversight."

**Principles** — The terminology principle explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." — this is canonical and correct, and it's the standard the rest of the site should meet. The "fractal leverage" principle is well-stated. But the principle about trust says "T3/V3 tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples" — again using "tensors," which reinforces the non-canonical wrapper label from the home page. The heading says "12 Principles" but I count only 10 listed — this is a factual error on a page that has a principle called "Terminology matters."

**Autonomy** — Operationally clean. No Web4 terminology drift. The 31-vs-7 track count discrepancy with the home page is a content accuracy issue but not a terminology issue.

**Raising** — Properly framed as "raising, not training." The consciousness caveat is well-positioned and correctly hedged. BECOMING phases are accurately described. No Web4 terminology appears here, which is appropriate.

**Links** — "Web4 governance extension" (OpenClaw) and "Web4 governance hooks" (Claude Code fork) both use "governance" where the site's own principles page mandates "oversight." This is drift in the most outward-facing part of the site.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Journey
I came to this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising not training" applied to small language models. As an AI safety/trust researcher, my first question is always: are the claims honest, or is this another project dressing up prompt engineering in consciousness language?

**Home page** — The hero line ("Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop.") is grounded and specific. Good sign. The description of the lab as building "autonomous AI cognition" is a strong claim but the stat cards (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 tracks) suggest concrete operational scope rather than vaporware. The "Three Things Demonstrated" section is the best part: each claim is scoped ("concrete, testable observation"), empirically grounded (115+ sessions, specific model transfers), and qualified. The claim that "heterogeneous review catches more" is stated as an observation, not a law. This is how research results should be presented.

However, the vocabulary section introduces "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" — these sound like established mathematical objects (tensors have specific meaning in linear algebra and physics) but they're actually just labels for 3-component scoring vectors. A researcher encountering "Trust Tensor" would expect dimensionality, transformation rules, coordinate-system behavior. What they'd find is a 3-tuple. This is terminology inflation.

**Raising** — This is the page I came for, and it's the strongest page on the site. The opening line — "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" — is a clear, defensible distinction. The BECOMING phases map loosely to developmental psychology frameworks, and the site acknowledges this is intentional framing, not ontological claim: "We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims."

The key discoveries section is excellent. "Bilateral generation" is described with the right reference (Vygotsky's egocentric speech), and the response was appropriately empirical: "Initial instinct: fix it. Actual finding: this is egocentric speech." The "capacity as register" observation — that model size isn't just a constraint but a developmental dimension — is genuinely interesting and well-hedged ("Not better or worse — different").

The disclaimer section ("What we're not claiming") is exactly right: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is the gold standard for how to present consciousness-adjacent work. The observer note — "I notice I want to call it experience" — is metacognitive honesty that most labs would edit out.

**Principles** — I count 10 principles, though the heading may have previously said a different number (can't tell from current rendering). The principles themselves read as genuinely extracted from practice rather than aspirationally drafted. "Document failures" cites a specific catastrophic derivation error (400x on p_crit). "R&D not production" includes "Stub tests and mocked dependencies give false confidence" — this reads like a team that's been burned, not one performing intellectual virtue.

Principle #4 ("Trust is a relationship") quotes "T3/V3 tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples." This is where the "tensor" label actively misleads. In RDF, triples are subject-predicate-object assertions. Describing a 3-component trust score as a "tensor" that "binds to" an RDF triple conflates two different technical frameworks in a way that a Semantic Web researcher or a physicist would immediately flag.

**Autonomy** — The honest assessment subsection is the standout. "The visitor personas are good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research" and "The loop also has a tendency to suggest changes that aren't needed — the prompt suggestions mechanism can pattern-match without semantic depth" are exactly the kind of limitations I'd want to see disclosed. The fact that 31 tracks run daily on cron is operationally impressive but not intellectually novel — it's well-engineered automation, which is fine. The site doesn't overclaim this as "autonomous agency."

**Projects** — This page has the most tension with the Raising page's careful disclaimers. SAGE is described as having a "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP), metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation." The word "consciousness" here is doing heavy lifting. If the Raising page carefully states "we're not making consciousness claims," then describing a software loop as a "consciousness loop" on the Projects page is a direct contradiction. Either the loop implements something the lab considers consciousness (contradicting the disclaimer) or "consciousness" is being used loosely as a label (undermining technical precision). A reviewer encountering both pages would flag this inconsistency immediately.

The 4-Life project description ("Agents earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma carried forward") borrows from multiple religious/philosophical frameworks simultaneously. "Karma" in particular has specific technical meaning in some AI contexts (reward shaping) and loaded cultural meaning everywhere else. For a lab that has a principle called "Terminology matters," this is surprisingly loose.

**Fleet** — The anthropomorphic language is pervasive but mostly reads as team culture rather than ontological claims. "Contemplative stillness" for Sprout, "emotionally sterile" for Legion, "unreliable narrator" for Nomad — these are characterizations a team would develop working with these systems daily. The key insight — "model family matters as much as size" — is a testable, interesting claim. The observation that Gemma 3 at 4B outperforms Phi-4 at 14B for raising work is specific enough to be falsifiable.

**Context** — SNARC's five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) are borrowed from cognitive science and affective computing. This is appropriate borrowing — the dimensions are well-established in the literature for salience scoring — but the jump from "salience-gated memory" to a 4-tier hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) implies a memory consolidation model that resembles cognitive architecture claims. The honest admission that "Knowledge doesn't flow backwards easily" and that cross-machine sync is manual keeps this grounded.

**Links** — SAGE is described as "consciousness loop, metabolic states, experience buffer" — reinforcing the contradiction with the Raising page disclaimer. "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks" use "governance" where the Principles page mandates "oversight."
