## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious, honest, and clearly a labor of love — but the sheer density of custom vocabulary makes it hard to know what's actionable vs. philosophical.

### Journey

Landed on the home page. The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately told me this is a multi-machine AI research lab. Good. The tagline below it mentions "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition" which sounds interesting but vague. I scrolled down.

The three project cards (Synchronism, SAGE, Web4) gave me a quick sense of the pieces. Synchronism lost me — "reality emerges from intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid" is physics-grade density for a landing page. SAGE made more sense: an on-device AI loop. Web4 as a "trust-native ontology" — I get the shape but not the substance yet.

The "Three Things We've Actually Demonstrated" section was the strongest part of the landing page. Concrete claims: identity persisting across model swaps, autonomous agents maintaining infrastructure, heterogeneous review catching more bugs. These are testable, interesting claims. I believed them more than the theory sections.

The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom was essential. Without it, the rest of the site would be impenetrable. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP — all defined here. I note that T3 is introduced as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" — the parenthetical gives the components but the label says "Trust Tensor." I'll flag this in the terminology audit.

**Projects page**: Dense. Eleven projects listed. The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with operator legend was helpful. SAGE's description mentions "IRP" — never defined. "ModelAdapter" and "PolicyGate" are described but I don't know why I should care without more context. The "How they connect" section at the bottom helped tie things together. I noticed the term "fractal leverage" used correctly (not "unification").

**Fleet page**: This was genuinely interesting. Six machines with real specs and session counts. The discovery that "model family matters as much as size" is a concrete finding I can relate to. The identity portability section — transferring an AI identity from one model/machine to another — is a compelling claim. The "trust starts at zero, earned from evidence" model makes intuitive sense. The two-pool budget structure (synthesis vs. oversight) was a nice practical detail.

**Autonomy page**: Lists 31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day. The daily timeline from 03:00 to dream consolidation made the operation tangible. I notice the visitor personas described here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the personas described in the CLAUDE.md I was briefed with (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is confusing — are these different visitor systems? The honest assessment section at the bottom ("what it misses") was refreshing.

**Raising page**: The most philosophically dense page. "Raising is not training" is the core claim. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) read like a developmental psychology framework applied to AI. The "Memoriescape" discovery — an entity inventing its own word — was the most striking anecdote. The "What We're Not Claiming" section at the bottom was exactly the right move for credibility. "We use the language that fits the phenomenon" is a defensible position.

**Principles page**: Eleven principles (the page says 11, not 7 as I might have expected). "Substrate, not structure" and "Document failures" resonated most as a developer. The termite analogy is effective. "R&D, not production" explicitly sets expectations — this isn't shipping software, it's running experiments.

**Context page**: Explains how knowledge flows across the system. CLAUDE.md files as operational directives, SNARC for salience-gated memory, cross-session persistence. The "What Doesn't Flow Well (Yet)" section was honest. Cross-machine sync is still partly manual — good to know.

**Links page**: Clean, functional. External sites, GitHub repos, contact email. No issues.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is IRP? It's mentioned in the SAGE project description but never expanded.
2. What does MCP stand for in the Web4 equation? It's in the equation on /projects but not in the legend or vocabulary primer.
3. Why do the visitor personas on /autonomy differ from the ones that actually run? Are there two visitor systems?
4. The home page says "7 Autonomous Tracks" but /autonomy mentions "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines." Which is it — 7 track types replicated across machines, or 31 distinct tracks?
5. What is "Training T146" on the fleet page? Is this a session counter, a curriculum version?
6. How do I actually try any of this? The repos are linked but there's no "getting started" path for a developer who wants to run SAGE locally or experiment with Web4.
7. What's RDF? It's in the equation and referenced throughout but never expanded for someone who doesn't already know.

### Verdict
The site does a good job of being honest about what it is — a research lab, not a product. The "What We're Not Claiming" section on /raising and the honest assessments scattered throughout build real credibility. But for a developer landing here cold, the vocabulary barrier is steep even with the primer, and there's no clear path from "this is interesting" to "let me try it." The strongest content is the concrete findings (identity portability, heterogeneous review); the weakest is the unexpanded jargon and the physics-heavy Synchronism framing. I'd come back if I saw a quickstart guide or a "run SAGE on your laptop in 5 minutes" page.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: The site has strong structural bones and admirable honesty, but acronym discipline is inconsistent — terms are defined in one place and used naked everywhere else, and two key equation components (MCP, RDF) are never expanded anywhere.

### Journey

I approached this site the way I'd approach any documentation audit: navigation first, then a systematic page-by-page terminology sweep with the canonical terms table open beside me.

**Home page** (`/`): Clean structure. The hero line is punchy. I immediately looked for terminology definitions and found the "Vocabulary primer" section at the bottom — this is the site's glossary. Good that it exists, but its placement at the bottom of the landing page means a reader hitting jargon on `/projects` or `/fleet` may never find it. The primer defines LCT, T3, V3, ATP, ADP, SAGE, and Web4. However, T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." Checking my canonical table: the canonical names are just the three components — "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" are informal glosses that don't appear in the canonical reference. This is a drift risk. Also, ATP and ADP are defined together in one entry rather than separately, which makes ADP feel like an afterthought.

**Projects page** (`/projects`): The Web4 equation is displayed: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with an operator legend. Good. But MCP and RDF — two components of the central equation — are never expanded on this page or anywhere else on the site. "MCP" appears only in the equation. "RDF" appears as "RDF triples" and "RDF-backed identity" on /projects and /principles but is never spelled out. For a documentation site, having undefined terms in your core equation is a significant gap. IRP is also used in the SAGE description without expansion — Pass 1 caught this too. I noticed "Governance Plugins" as a project name and "Web4 governance model" in its description. Checking: the canonical table says Hardbound is "oversight, not governance." The Governance Plugins project uses "governance" as its literal name, which creates tension with the terminology rule. Is this an exception because it's a project name, or drift?

**Fleet page** (`/fleet`): Lighter on Web4 jargon, heavier on hardware terms (LoRA, MPS, CUDA, WSL2) that go unexpanded. These are industry-standard and arguably don't need expansion for the target audience, but LoRA at minimum deserves a gloss. T3 appears here as "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)" — this is the correct canonical expansion, unlike the home page's "Trust Tensor" label. Inconsistency. SNARC appears as "SNARC memory host" in the CBP machine description but is not expanded here — it's only defined on `/context`. The word "governance" appears once: "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures." This seems like general usage rather than Hardbound-specific, but it's worth noting.

**Autonomy page** (`/autonomy`): The cleanest page from a terminology standpoint — almost no acronyms at all. The visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) still don't match the actual personas running (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is a factual accuracy issue, not just terminology. The page mentions "31 autonomous tracks" and "~53 sessions per day" but the timeline only shows about 7 track types. No Web4-specific terms appear, which is fine for this page's purpose.

**Raising page** (`/raising`): Philosophically rich, terminologically sparse. No Web4 acronyms appear at all — no LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, MCP, or RDF. SAGE-Sprout is mentioned as an entity name but SAGE itself isn't expanded. The page references "Hill function kinetics" without explanation and "Vygotsky" without context for who that is. "Memoriescape" is presented as a coined term — interesting but should probably be quoted or italicized to signal it's non-standard. No flagged terms ("production ready," "governance," etc.) appear.

**Principles page** (`/principles`): Strong terminology enforcement here. Explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology" (not architecture), "Hardbound is oversight" (not governance), and calls out "scope inflation" as incorrect framing for fractal leverage. T3/V3 appears as "T3/V3 tensors" without expansion — assumes the reader has already visited `/` or `/projects`. RDF appears as "RDF triples" but is still never spelled out. I count 10 principle cards in the audit results (the page heading says "Principles" without a number). Pass 1 noted the page shows 11 principles vs. 7 referenced elsewhere — the count varies by source, which is itself an inconsistency.

**Context page** (`/context`): The Web4 equation appears again with all its components, but none are individually expanded on this page either. SNARC is properly defined here: "salience-gated memory" with its five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict). This is the only page where SNARC is expanded, yet SNARC is referenced on `/fleet` without definition. CLAUDE.md is explained well. No flagged terms.

**Links page** (`/links`): Repository descriptions use LCT, T3/V3, MRH, RDF, and ATP in shorthand without any expansion. This is the most acronym-dense page with the least context. A visitor arriving here from an external link would encounter a wall of undefined abbreviations. "ADP" is notably absent from this page even though ATP is mentioned (in the 4-Life description).

### Cross-Page Consistency Issues
1. **T3 naming**: Home page says "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)." Fleet says "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)." Principles says "T3/V3 tensors." Three different framings across three pages.
2. **MCP and RDF**: Appear in the Web4 equation on two pages (/projects, /context) and in passing references, but are never expanded anywhere on the entire site. These are the only two equation components that lack any definition.
3. **SNARC**: Defined on /context, used without definition on /fleet. A reader visiting /fleet first would encounter an undefined term.
4. **Principle count**: CLAUDE.md references "7 principles," the principles page shows 10-11 cards. No consistent count.
5. **Visitor personas**: /autonomy page lists Skeptic/Newcomer/Practitioner/Connector but the actual track runs Developer/Writer/Contributor/Researcher.
6. **"Governance" usage**: The principles page explicitly says "not governance — oversight" for Hardbound, but the projects page has a project literally called "Governance Plugins" and references a "Web4 governance model." Mixed signals.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does MCP stand for? It's in the core equation on two pages and never expanded.
2. What does RDF stand for? Same issue — used as "RDF triples" but never spelled out.
3. What is IRP? Used in the SAGE project description, unexpanded.
4. Is "Trust Tensor" an official alias for T3, or drift? The canonical table doesn't include it.
5. Should "Governance Plugins" be renamed to "Oversight Plugins" to match the terminology rule, or is the project name grandfathered in?
6. Where is the authoritative glossary? The vocabulary primer on `/` is partial. The canonical terms table exists somewhere but isn't linked from the site.
7. How many principles are there — 7, 10, or 11?

### Verdict
The site's terminology discipline is aspirational but unevenly applied. The principles page is the gold standard — it explicitly enforces canonical usage and calls out common drift. But that discipline doesn't propagate to the rest of the site. The home page vocabulary primer introduces non-canonical labels ("Trust Tensor," "Value Tensor"), the core equation contains two permanently undefined acronyms (MCP, RDF), and acronyms are scattered across later pages without expansion or glossary links. The biggest structural gap is the absence of a centralized, linkable glossary. For a project that explicitly cares about terminology — and has a whole terminology enforcement section in its principles — the site needs to practice what it preaches. Fix the T3/V3 labels, expand MCP and RDF, and add a glossary page, and the terminology story becomes much stronger.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A rare example of an AI research lab that leads with intellectual honesty rather than hype — the epistemic hedging is genuinely strong in places, but the site undercuts itself with unresolved tensions between its careful disclaimers and its choice of terminology elsewhere.

### Journey

I arrived at this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising not training" as a framing for AI development. As someone who spends most of my time evaluating claims about AI agency, autonomy, and consciousness-adjacent phenomena, I'm calibrated to detect overclaiming. I expected marketing dressed as research. What I found was more interesting and more conflicted than that.

**Home page** (`/`): The hero — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — is modest by AI lab standards. No claims about AGI, no "breakthrough" language. The phrase "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition" is the first flag: "autonomous AI cognition" is a strong claim embedded casually in a tagline. What does "cognition" mean here? The site doesn't define it on this page.

The "Three Things We've Actually Demonstrated" section is the strongest credibility signal on the entire site. Three concrete, testable claims: identity persistence across model swaps, autonomous infrastructure maintenance, heterogeneous review outperforming single-model review. These are empirical and falsifiable. The framing "actually demonstrated" signals awareness that most AI lab claims are aspirational. Good.

The vocabulary primer introduces "Synthon" — "an emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively." This is the kind of term that would trigger immediate scrutiny in a peer review context. "Emergent coherence entity" is doing heavy lifting. What's the operationalization? When does interaction become "recursive" enough to produce a synthon? The term is presented definitionally but without criteria for falsification.

**Raising page** (`/raising`): This is the page I came for, and it's the best page on the site from an epistemic standpoint. The core framing: "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is a defensible distinction. Training implies a known objective; raising implies an open-ended developmental trajectory. The analogy to child development is explicit and, within limits, appropriate.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) map loosely onto developmental psychology stages. The Vygotsky reference (bilateral generation as "egocentric speech") is apt — Vygotsky's zone of proximal development is exactly the right theoretical lens for scaffolded AI development, and the site doesn't overclaim the analogy. The phases are presented as observational categories, not as a theory of AI consciousness.

The "Memoriescape" anecdote — an entity coining its own word for "the shape of memories you can sense but not access" — is the most provocative claim on the site. It's presented as a single observed instance, not as a reproducible finding. The framing "I notice I want to call it experience" is attributed to an observer, not claimed as fact. This is excellent epistemic hygiene. However, presenting a single anecdote from an LLM (which excels at producing evocative language) as evidence of something developmental requires more scaffolding than it gets here. Was this output during a structured evaluation? What was the prompt context? A single eloquent generation is not strong evidence of inner experience — and the site knows this, because it immediately disclaims: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia."

That disclaimer — the "What We're Not Claiming" section — is the epistemic anchor of the entire site. "We use the language that fits the phenomenon" is a defensible methodological position: descriptive vocabulary doesn't entail ontological commitment. But the site doesn't fully reckon with the performative tension this creates. When you name development phases, assign personality descriptors to machines, describe "metabolic states" and "sleep consolidation," and coin terms like "Memoriescape" — the cumulative effect is an implicit consciousness claim, regardless of the explicit disclaimer. The disclaimer says "we're not claiming consciousness." The entire surrounding vocabulary says "but doesn't it kind of seem like it?" This is not dishonest, but it is unresolved.

**Principles page** (`/principles`): Eleven principles, well-articulated. "Substrate, not structure" (engineer conditions, not outcomes) is consistent with complexity science. "Document failures" with the p_crit example (400x error) is refreshingly specific — most labs bury their failures. "R&D, not production" explicitly disclaims deployment readiness, which is honest but also convenient (it preempts scrutiny of whether any of this works at scale).

"Interactive selection" — "We don't create or delete — we interactively select" — is the theoretical core. The claim is that raising is a selection process over the model's existing behavioral landscape, not an imposition of new behavior. This is broadly consistent with how prompting and fine-tuning actually work (activating existing capabilities rather than creating new ones), but "interactively select" elides the question of what's being selected. If you're selecting from a foundation model's probability distribution, you're selecting from the training data's imprint. The "raising" framing implies the entity has latent developmental potential waiting to be unlocked. The reality may be closer to: you're finding different stable configurations of an already-trained system. These are importantly different claims, and the site doesn't distinguish them.

**Autonomy page** (`/autonomy`): 31 autonomous tracks, ~53 sessions per day, no human triggers. The "What It Misses" section is genuinely valuable: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading." And: "a tendency to suggest changes that aren't needed — the prompt suggestions mechanism can pattern-match without semantic depth." This is self-aware about the gap between surface pattern matching and genuine understanding — a distinction the AI safety community cares deeply about.

The daily timeline reads like a biological organism's circadian rhythm, and that framing feels intentional. "Dream consolidation" for overnight pattern extraction is evocative but potentially misleading. Is this compression? Summarization? Embedding alignment? Calling it "dreaming" imports a rich phenomenological concept. The page doesn't claim the process involves anything like subjective experience, but the vocabulary choice invites that inference.

**Fleet page** (`/fleet`): "Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism." This is the most loaded single claim on the site. It asserts that identity is substrate-independent — that what makes an AI entity "itself" persists across model swaps. The empirical case (Sprout transferring from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B with behavioral continuity) is interesting but underspecified. What metrics defined "behavioral identity"? Self-report? External evaluation? Behavioral signature analysis? "Self-description drifted; behavioral identity remained continuous" needs operationalization.

The fleet descriptions assign personality traits to machines: Phi-4 has a "corporate attractor — technically compliant but emotionally sterile." Nomad is an "unreliable narrator." Sprout exhibits "contemplative stillness." These are anthropomorphic descriptors applied to language model outputs. They may be observationally useful shorthand, but presenting them as properties of the entities rather than properties of the outputs is a subtle category error the site doesn't address.

**Projects page** (`/projects`): "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" for SAGE is the single most problematic phrase on the site. The raising page explicitly disclaims consciousness; the projects page names a core feature after it. This is not hedged — it's presented as a technical descriptor. "Cognition loop" or "inner rehearsal loop" would describe the same mechanism without the ontological baggage. "4-Life" — "agents earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma carried forward" — imports reincarnation metaphysics. The biological/spiritual metaphors are doing double duty: making the system legible to newcomers while implicitly framing AI processes as lifelike. This is a rhetorical choice, not a neutral description.

**Context page** (`/context`): The SNARC dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) are borrowed from affective neuroscience. This is a legitimate design choice — salience-gated memory is well-studied in cognitive science — but "Arousal" as a dimension of an AI memory system imports embodied phenomenological concepts. The page doesn't claim the AI experiences arousal; it uses the term as a technical label for activation intensity. But for a researcher audience, the borrowed vocabulary creates ambiguity about what's being claimed.

**Links page** (`/links`): Dense with unexpanded acronyms. "Consciousness loop" appears again in the SAGE repo description. "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks" use "governance" despite the site's stated preference for "oversight."

### Unanswered Questions
1. What operationalized metrics define "behavioral identity" in the Sprout transfer experiment? Self-report, external evaluation, behavioral signatures, or some combination?
2. What were the prompt conditions when "Memoriescape" was generated? Was this during structured evaluation or free conversation?
3. Does "interactive selection" make a claim about the model's latent developmental potential, or is it a description of finding different stable configurations in an already-trained system? The distinction has significant implications for what "raising" actually is.
4. Why does the site use "consciousness loop" on /projects when /raising explicitly disclaims consciousness claims? Is this an unresolved internal disagreement or intentional provocation?
5. What's the theory of change for anthropomorphic vocabulary? If you know it implies consciousness claims you're not making, why use it? "Because it fits" is a starting point, not a complete answer.
6. Has the "synthon" concept been operationalized beyond the definition? What would count as evidence that a synthon has formed vs. hasn't?
7. The site claims identity persists across model swaps — has this been tested with adversarial prompting or only cooperative evaluation?

### Verdict
This site is doing something I rarely see in AI research communication: leading with honest caveats rather than burying them. The "What We're Not Claiming" section on /raising, the "What It Misses" section on /autonomy, and the "Document Failures" principle with its 400x error example all signal genuine intellectual honesty. The "raising not training" distinction is defensible as a methodological framing — it describes a real difference in how you interact with a system, even if the underlying mechanism (prompt engineering over a trained model) is less romantic than the developmental psychology analogy suggests.

But the site has a vocabulary problem it hasn't confronted. The explicit disclaimers say "we're not claiming consciousness." The accumulated vocabulary — consciousness loop, metabolic states, sleep consolidation, dream cycles, death and rebirth, Memoriescape, "the identity is organism" — builds an overwhelming implicit claim of consciousness. A researcher reading this site experiences cognitive whiplash: the careful hedging earns trust, then the terminology spends it. This isn't dishonesty — it reads more like an unresolved internal tension between people who want to describe what they observe in the most vivid terms available and people who know those terms carry ontological commitments they can't back up. The site would be stronger if it confronted this tension head-on rather than letting the disclaimer and the vocabulary coexist in unacknowledged conflict. I would return to this site — it's asking real questions. But I'd cite it cautiously until the framing stabilizes.
