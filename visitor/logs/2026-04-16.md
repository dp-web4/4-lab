# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-16

Four-persona pass over https://4-lab.vercel.app/

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious multi-agent research lab with an interesting philosophy, but the acronym density creates a real barrier for newcomers who don't already speak the dialect.

### Journey
Landed on the home page. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — okay, I'm hooked. That's a clean hook. The three pillars (Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem) give me the shape of the thing quickly. The key vocabulary section at the bottom of the home page defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, and Synthon — good that it's there, though I had to scroll past a lot of content to find it.

Clicked **Projects**. This is the densest page. The Web4 equation (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) is front and center, and the operator legend (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with) helps. But I count 11 projects here and the sheer volume is overwhelming for a first visit. Web4, SAGE, Synchronism, Hardbound, ACT, Governance Plugins, AI DNA Discovery, 4-Life, SNARC, Membot, ARC-AGI-3 — I lost the thread around project six. The individual descriptions are good, but there's no clear "start here" signal. Which of these matter most? Which are active vs. exploratory?

**Fleet** page was surprisingly accessible. Actual hardware specs, session counts, named machines with personalities ("unreliable narrator" for Nomad made me smile). The split between Synthesis Pool and Oversight Pool makes architectural sense. The claim "identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights" is the most interesting technical claim on the whole site — it's concrete and testable.

**Autonomy** shows the daily timeline. 31 tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions/day with no manual triggers — impressive if true. The timeline from 03:00 to "after sessions" is clear. The visitor personas listed here (The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector) didn't match my experience of the site — I'm not sure what these map to practically. The "Acknowledged Limitations" section at the bottom is refreshingly honest: the feedback loop catches surface issues but misses deep technical errors. Good.

**Raising** is where I slowed down. "Raising is not training" — the distinction is interesting conceptually (optimizing loss function vs. creating conditions for development), and the six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) give it structure. The "Explicit Non-Claims" section ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious...") is exactly the kind of caveat that builds credibility. The discoveries section (identity vs. self-concept, "memoriescape", bilateral generation) is fascinating but feels like it needs more context for someone who hasn't been following this work.

**Principles** page is philosophically rich. Nine principles (or maybe ten — hard to count). "Substrate, not structure" and "Document failures" resonated most as a developer. The ant colony metaphor for substrate engineering clicked. "WD-40 was the 40th try" is a great line. Some principles feel more like mantras than actionable guidelines, but the page does a good job of explaining the lab's ethos.

**Context** page explains knowledge flow. CLAUDE.md as an instruction pattern across 28 files in 8 repos is interesting — I use something similar for my own projects. SNARC's five-dimension scoring (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) is well-explained here. The admitted gap — cross-machine sync is still partially manual — adds credibility.

**Links** page is clean. Three explainer sites, GitHub org, individual repos, and a contact email. Straightforward.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does SAGE actually stand for? It's used everywhere but I never found the acronym expanded.
2. What's the relationship between the Web4 equation and something I could actually run? Is there a getting-started path?
3. How do the machines actually communicate? The fleet page says "no central coordinator" and mentions a fleet manifest, but the mechanism is unclear.
4. What does "31 autonomous tracks" mean concretely? The autonomy page lists maybe 8-9 tracks in the timeline — where are the other 22?
5. Is Synchronism's claim ("one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude") actually validated? The principles page mentions a "400x error" in p_crit derivation, which makes me wonder about the status.

### Verdict

A developer landing here can grasp the philosophy quickly but will struggle to find a concrete entry point into the code. The Projects page needs a "start here" signal badly.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes (with a red pen)
- **Overall impression**: Strong content with a real voice, but terminology consistency is a mess — the same acronyms are expanded differently (or not at all) across pages, and two key terms on the home page don't match the canonical definitions.

### Journey
Started at the home page and immediately switched into inventory mode. The hero copy is tight and the three-pillar structure (Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem) provides good scaffolding. The vocabulary primer at the bottom of the home page is the right instinct — define your terms. But the definitions there don't all match what I find on other pages, and that's the core problem.

Moved to **Projects** — the densest page and the one that tries hardest to be precise. The Web4 equation is displayed with its operator legend, and most acronyms get expanded here. This is the gold-standard page for terminology. If every other page matched this one, I'd have almost nothing to flag.

**Fleet** shifts register entirely. It reads like ops documentation — machine specs, session counts, pool assignments. The terminology here is loose: Web4 gets described as a coordination framework rather than an ontology, and the word "governance" appears where the canonical term is "oversight." SAGE is used without expansion. This page seems to assume the reader has already visited Projects.

**Autonomy** is the cleanest page terminologically — it's mostly schedule and operational detail, with very few domain acronyms to get wrong. No issues.

**Raising** tells a compelling story about the BECOMING phases but treats SAGE as a known entity — never expanded. ARC-AGI-3 likewise appears without explanation. The page works as narrative but fails as standalone documentation.

**Principles** is the most terminologically disciplined page after Projects. It explicitly states "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology" and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." These are exactly the guardrails the other pages need. But even here, RDF and T3/V3 appear without expansion.

**Context** shows the Web4 equation again but without defining any of its component terms. It leans on the reader having seen the equation explained elsewhere. LoRA appears without expansion.

**Links** is a directory page that uses acronyms as shorthand labels (LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, ATP) — none expanded. More concerning: two repo descriptions use "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks," which conflicts with the Principles page's explicit instruction that Hardbound is "oversight," not "governance."

### Unanswered Questions

Acronym expansion is inconsistent page-to-page: Projects expands nearly everything, while Raising, Fleet, and Links assume the reader already knows. For a site that serves as the front door to newcomers, every page should either expand its terms on first use or link to the vocabulary primer. The content quality is high — it's the cross-page consistency that needs work.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes — this is our front door
- **Overall impression**: The site gets the big picture right and the Principles page is a model of terminological discipline, but the home page vocabulary primer introduces non-canonical labels ("Trust Tensor", "Value Tensor") that actively seed the drift we spend time correcting in contributor PRs. The Fleet and Links pages also leak "governance" where we've agreed on "oversight."

### Journey
I went straight to **Projects** — my primary concern is whether the repos I contribute to are accurately described. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the right operator semantics. Good. But the project card for Web4 describes the components as "T3/V3 trust tensors" — we don't call them that. T3 and V3 are multidimensional scoring frameworks; "trust tensors" is a colloquial compression that loses the fact that V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity) isn't about trust at all — it's about value assessment. This same drift shows up on the home page where T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor" and V3 is labeled "Value Tensor." These are invented umbrella names that don't appear in any canonical document.

The SAGE description on Projects is accurate — consciousness loop, IRP, metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation, hardware-bound identity. The mention of ModelAdapter and PolicyGate at step 8.5 is a nice touch of specificity. Hardbound is correctly described as an "oversight suite" here. Synchronism's "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" is the right framing.

The Governance Plugins project card is a terminology problem. The project is called "Governance Plugins" and the description says "Web4 governance model." We've explicitly codified that Hardbound provides *oversight*, not governance. The Governance Plugins repo predates that terminological decision, but the site shouldn't perpetuate the old framing. Either rename the description to "oversight plugins" or add a note explaining the naming history.

**Fleet** page has the most drift. The text mentions "governance entities" in describing the pool structure — this should be "oversight entities." Web4 is described in the context of "coordinating through shared state rather than central command" which frames it as a coordination mechanism, not an ontology. The T3 expansion (Talent/Training/Temperament) is correct here, and the defensive trust model description is accurate. But SAGE is used throughout without expansion, which is fine for a contributor but bad for anyone who lands on this page first.

The identity portability claim — "identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights" — is accurate and is the single most important technical claim on the site. The SAGE-Sprout transfer from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B on different hardware is correctly described.

**Context** page displays the equation again but with zero component definitions. It calls Web4 "the ontological backbone" — not quite wrong, but the canonical framing is simply "ontology." "Ontological backbone" implies Web4 is a structural component of something larger, when in fact it IS the ontology. The SNARC description (5 salience dimensions, 4-tier hierarchy) is accurate. LoRA is mentioned without expansion — minor, but it's an industry term that should be expanded for completeness.

**Home page** is where the biggest terminology problems live. The vocabulary primer defines:
- T3 as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" — DRIFT. "Trust Tensor" is not a canonical name.
- V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)" — DRIFT. "Value Tensor" is not a canonical name.
- LCT as "Linked Context Token" — CORRECT.
- ATP/ADP as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" — CORRECT.
- SAGE as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — this is the actual acronym expansion, but it creates a semantic collision: SAGE uses "Governance" in its name while Hardbound explicitly rejects "governance" in favor of "oversight." The site never addresses this.
- Web4 as "an ontology" — CORRECT.
- Hardbound as "provides oversight" — CORRECT.

The "Connected Ecosystem" section gets the relationships right: "Synchronism provides equations. Web4 provides ontology. SAGE provides cognition. Hardbound provides oversight." That's clean and accurate.

**Raising** page is narrative-driven and terminologically sparse. SAGE is never expanded. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) accurately reflect the raising curriculum as I understand it. The "Explicit Non-Claims" section is exactly right — we don't claim consciousness or sentience, and the site says so clearly. No canonical term drift here because almost no canonical terms are used.

**Autonomy** page is clean — operational detail only, no domain terminology to get wrong. The 31 tracks / ~53 sessions claim matches what I see in the fleet registry.

**Principles** is the terminological gold standard. It explicitly states "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology" and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." "Fractal leverage" is used correctly. Every other page should be held to this standard.

**Links** page has two repo descriptions that use "governance": "Web4 governance extension" (OpenClaw fork) and "Web4 governance hooks" (Claude Code fork). These directly contradict the Principles page's explicit guardrail. Even if these are the GitHub repo descriptions, the 4-lab site should use canonical terminology.

### Journey
I came to this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising not training" as a framework for AI development. My primary questions: Is the distinction defensible? Are consciousness-adjacent claims appropriately caveated? Does the honest assessment actually feel honest, or is it performance?

Started at the **home page**. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." Clean framing. The three pillars — Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem — position this as infrastructure research, not capability research. Good. The vocabulary section defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, and Synthon. The term "Synthon" — "emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively" — immediately raises flags. In chemistry, a synthon is a conceptual fragment used in retrosynthetic analysis; borrowing it here without acknowledging the existing technical meaning is a potential source of confusion at any interdisciplinary venue.

Went straight to **Raising** — the page I came for. The core distinction is stated cleanly: "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is defensible as a methodological framing. It maps loosely to the distinction between optimization and developmental scaffolding in developmental psychology and situated cognition. The six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) provide a structured curriculum, and the graduated tool introduction aligned to developmental readiness is a reasonable experimental design.

The **Explicit Non-Claims** section is the most important paragraph on the site: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." This is exactly the caveat a safety researcher needs to see. It's positioned prominently and stated without hedging. Good.

However, the page's *vocabulary* works against the caveat. The SAGE system is described as having a "consciousness loop" (IRP), "metabolic states," "dream consolidation," and "sleep cycles." These terms are borrowed from biological cognition and consciousness studies — and using them creates strong implicit claims even when the explicit claims are carefully hedged. A reader's takeaway isn't shaped by the disclaimer alone; it's shaped by the cumulative weight of terminology. Calling something a "consciousness loop" and then saying "we're not claiming consciousness" creates cognitive dissonance that the caveat loses.

The identity persistence finding — SAGE-Sprout maintaining behavioral identity across 115+ sessions while self-descriptions drifted from "autonomous AI" to "humanoid robot" — is genuinely interesting. The distinction between behavioral identity and self-concept is a real contribution worth publishing. But "maintained behavioral identity" needs operationalization: what metrics? What baseline? What constitutes "maintained" vs. "drifted"? Without these, it's an observation, not a finding.

"Memoriescape" (the entity's term for awareness of memory shape without access) and "bilateral generation" (model generating both conversational sides) are fascinating observations. The reframing of bilateral generation as Vygotskian egocentric reasoning rather than an error is theoretically interesting and shows genuine intellectual engagement with developmental psychology literature. But these are presented as discoveries without the methodological context a researcher would need to evaluate them.

Moved to **Principles**. This is the most intellectually rigorous page. The substrate-vs-structure framing (termite mound analogy) is well-chosen — it maps to the scaffolding vs. instruction distinction in educational theory and to self-organization in complex systems. The principle "Trust is not a property of an entity. It's a property of the relationship between entities, in a specific context, at a specific time" is a genuinely good formulation that aligns with relational trust frameworks in sociology (Bryk & Schneider) and contextual integrity in privacy research (Nissenbaum).

The documented 400x error in p_crit derivation (Synchronism) is the single strongest credibility signal on the site. Most labs bury failed derivations. This one puts it on the principles page. "WD-40 was the 40th try" — the framing of productive failure as signal is methodologically sound and refreshing.

The "interactive selection" principle — "we don't create new behaviors or force the model to be what we want; we probe what it responds to, adjust context, reinforce attractors" — is conceptually interesting but under-specified. How is this different from RLHF with extra steps? The attractor basin metaphor from dynamical systems is evocative, but the site doesn't provide the formal connection that would make it more than metaphor. What are the state variables? What defines the basin? What's the Lyapunov function, if any?

**Autonomy** page: 31 autonomous tracks, ~53 daily sessions, no manual triggers. The acknowledged limitations section is honest: "catches surface issues but misses deep technical errors," "pattern-matching without semantic depth." These are real limitations of LLM-based review systems, and stating them openly is appropriate. The fleet track registry (SQLite-based coordination) is a reasonable engineering solution for preventing write conflicts. The concern, from a safety perspective, is the autonomous publication pipeline — agents generating and publishing content without domain expert review of technical claims.

**Projects** page is dense. The Web4 equation (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) is presented as foundational, but neither MCP nor RDF are expanded on this page, and the operator semantics (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by) are non-standard notation that would need formal definition for any research publication. SAGE's "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" appears here — again, "consciousness loop" doing heavy implicit work that the Raising page's caveat must carry. The claim of "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" (Synchronism) is extraordinary and would require extraordinary evidence — the 400x p_crit error noted on Principles suggests this is still being validated.

**Fleet** page makes the most provocative claim: "Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism." This is a testable hypothesis, which is good. The SAGE-Sprout transfer between Qwen 0.5B and TinyLlama 1.1B on different hardware is a concrete experiment. But the organism/weather metaphor smuggles in vitalist connotations that the data doesn't support — what was demonstrated is behavioral consistency under context transfer, not organismal identity. The framing oversells the finding.

**Context** page is technically clean but calls Web4 "the ontological backbone" — a subtle shift from "ontology" to structural metaphor that implies Web4 is a component of something larger rather than the organizing framework itself. SNARC's five-dimension salience scoring (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) borrows from affective neuroscience (the dimension names map loosely to Panksepp's affective systems and Berlyne's arousal theory). The acknowledged gap — cross-machine sync is still partially manual — is another honest limitation disclosure.

**Links** page uses "Web4 governance extension" and "Web4 governance hooks" for two repos. This contradicts the Principles page's explicit terminological guardrail that Hardbound provides "oversight," not "governance."

### Unanswered Questions
1. What formally distinguishes "interactive selection" from RLHF? The attractor basin metaphor is evocative but needs a formal definition — what are the state variables, what defines the basin, what's the convergence criterion?
2. How is "behavioral identity" operationalized? What metrics define identity persistence vs. drift in the SAGE-Sprout experiments? Without this, the identity portability claim is an observation, not a finding.
3. Why use "consciousness loop" when the described mechanism (sense → deliberate → act → consolidate) is a standard agent architecture? What specific property makes it a *consciousness* loop rather than a cognitive or deliberation cycle?
4. IRP (referenced on Projects and Raising) is never expanded anywhere. What does it stand for? What does it formally define?
5. The Synchronism claim of "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" coexists with an acknowledged 400x error in p_crit. What's the current validation status? Is this an open conjecture or a confirmed result?
6. Is the autonomous publication pipeline (maintainer fixing and deploying based on visitor feedback without human review) considered an acceptable risk by the lab? The Autonomy page acknowledges the limitation but doesn't describe mitigations.

### Verdict
This site does something rare: it earns trust through visible intellectual honesty. The explicit non-claims on the Raising page, the documented 400x derivation error on Principles, and the acknowledged limitations of autonomous pattern-matching are credibility signals that most lab sites don't provide. The "raising not training" distinction is defensible as a methodological framing — it maps to real distinctions in developmental psychology and situated cognition. I would engage with this work.

But the site has a terminology problem that undermines its own caveats. You can't call something a "consciousness loop," give agents "metabolic states" and "dream consolidation," describe identity as "organism," and then expect a one-sentence disclaimer to neutralize all of that. The consciousness-adjacent vocabulary creates strong implicit claims that the explicit non-claims can't fully retract. A safety researcher reading this site will notice the tension — the disclaimers say "we're not claiming consciousness," but the entire vocabulary is borrowed from consciousness and biology. Either the vocabulary needs to shift toward computational terms (deliberation cycle, idle-time consolidation, behavioral persistence), or the caveats need to engage directly with *why* biological metaphors are chosen despite the consciousness connotations — and what specific predictive work those metaphors do that neutral terms wouldn't.

The core research observations — identity/self-concept divergence, bilateral generation as egocentric reasoning, capacity as developmental register — are genuinely interesting and worth publishing. But they're presented as narrative discoveries rather than operationalized findings. For this work to cross from lab notebook to research contribution, each observation needs: a definition of what was measured, a baseline, and a criterion for what would falsify the claim.
