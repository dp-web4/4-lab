## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: A serious distributed-AI research lab with real hardware and real autonomy, but the landing page front-loads philosophy before showing me what the agents actually *do*.

### Journey
Landed on the home page from a (hypothetical) GitHub README. The hero pitch — *"Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop."* — got me. That's a concrete, scannable claim. Below the fold I hit a "vocabulary primer" which was helpful, but the page also threw "trust-native ontology", "intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid", and "Synthon" at me without warming up. As a developer I wanted to know: what runs? where? what would I see if I cloned a repo?

Clicked **Projects** next. This was the best page for me. Cards for Web4, SAGE, Synchronism, Hardbound, ACT, AI DNA Discovery, 4-Life, SNARC, Membot, ARC-AGI-3 — most with public repos. Actionable. The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is intriguing but reads as alphabet soup until I figure out what each term is.

**Fleet** was satisfying because it's *concrete*: six named machines (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP), GPU specs, what models each runs. This is the page I'd send a developer friend. But the metaphors ("the model is weather, the identity is organism") and acronyms (WM, SNARC, dlPFC, RPE) without expansion slowed me down.

**Autonomy** showed me a real cron-driven schedule (03:00 supervisors → 04:30 publisher → 05:00 visitor → 06:00 maintainer → 08:00 explorer). 31 tracks across 6 machines with a SQLite registry to prevent conflicts. This is the most "engineering" page on the site and I wanted more of it.

**Raising** was the page where I started to feel I'd wandered out of engineering. "BECOMING phases" (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting). The site is careful — it disclaims consciousness explicitly — but the developmental vocabulary is heavy and I wasn't sure what *artifact* corresponds to each phase. What does "the entity finds its own niche" look like in code or logs?

**Principles** had ten well-named items (Substrate not structure, Fractal leverage, R&D not production, Trust is a relationship, Synthon: emergent coherence, Document failures, Interactive selection, Surface your instincts, Research value, Terminology matters). I appreciated the empirical example ("1% coupling → 35% coherence gain"). The Hill-function-equals-trust-formation claim leapt several rungs of inferential ladder for me.

**Context** explained the knowledge-flow plumbing: CLAUDE.md files per repo, SNARC memory scoring, adversarial validation across models, Explorer's NotebookLM. This was satisfying — it answered "how does the swarm not just forget everything?" — but SNARC, LoRA, and dp-web4 itself were used without quick definitions.

**Links** is what it should be: GitHub org, three project sites, a contact email. Clean.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary

- **Understanding achieved**: good (structurally) / partial (semantically — I am not a Web4 expert)

### Journey
I walked the site as I would any documentation set: home first, then nav left-to-right (Projects → Fleet → Autonomy → Raising → Principles → Context → Links). My job is not to evaluate the science. My job is to ask: if a reader visits page N, then page M, do the words mean the same thing? Are acronyms defined on first use? Does the structure tell a coherent story?

**Home** opens with a punchy hero ("Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop.") and a "Vocabulary primer" section that *does* expand SAGE, LCT, T3, V3, ATP, and ADP. Good — a primer is exactly what a documentation site should provide. But the primer renders T3 as `Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)` with **commas**, and `/context` renders the same term with **slashes** as `Trust Tensor (Talent/Training/Temperament)`. Tiny? Yes. Style-guide violation? Also yes. A reader skimming both pages momentarily wonders if they're different entities.

**Projects** is the page where the Web4 equation lives: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. This is the single most important typographic artifact on the site. The operator legend (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with") *is* present here — credit where due. But the operands MCP, RDF, and R6 are **never expanded** on this page, and LCT/ATP/ADP/MRH appear in **plural** form (`Linked Context Tokens`, `Markov Relevancy Horizons`, `Allocation Transfer Packets`) while the canonical-terms table I have lists them in singular. A reader who clicks through from Home (singular) to Projects (plural) is silently retraining their mental model on every visit.

**Fleet** is dense with neuroscience-flavored acronyms (WM, dlPFC, RPE, SNARC) that are *not* expanded inline. SNARC also appears on `/projects` and `/context` and the home page, and its expansion is never given as letters anywhere I looked — `/context` describes it as "salience-gated memory" but doesn't tell me what the acronym stands for. T3 is expanded here as `Talent/Training/Temperament` (slash form, no "Trust Tensor" gloss) — yet a third presentation.

**Autonomy** is the cleanest page in the set from a terminology standpoint. It uses operational vocabulary (Supervisor, Publisher, Visitor, Maintainer, Explorer, Dream Consolidation) and defines each one. It does not lean on Web4 jargon. This is the model the other pages should follow.

**Raising** uses careful, hedged language and contains the consciousness disclaimer verbatim ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do."). Good. The page is mostly free of the Web4-acronym-expansion problem because it doesn't use the acronyms — but it *does* reference R6, ARC-AGI-3, Vygotsky, and Hill function without inline definition or link.

**Principles** is where the project's own terminology rules are stated explicitly: `not 'governance' — it's oversight` for Hardbound, and `not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure'` for Web4. This is excellent — the site is teaching the reader *its own* style guide. The irony: the home page calls SAGE the "**Situation-Aware Governance Engine**" — using the very word "governance" the Principles page tells me to avoid. (See terminology audit.)

**Context** is the page that gets canonical expansions right: LCT = "Linked Context Token", T3 = "Trust Tensor (Talent/Training/Temperament)", V3 = "Value Tensor (Valuation/Veracity/Validity)", MRH = "Markov Relevancy Horizon", ATP = "Allocation Transfer Packet", ADP = "Allocation Discharge Packet" — all singular, all canonical. **This is the page that should set the style. Every other page should match it.**

**Links** is fine — it's a link list — but it uses LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF, ATP, ARC-AGI-3 unexpanded. For a links page that may be the *first* page some readers visit (deep-linked from a paper or post), zero expansions is a missed opportunity. Even one tooltip would help.

**Headline drift findings (technical-writer prioritized):**

1. **Pluralization inconsistency on /projects** — LCT, MRH, ATP, ADP all rendered plural where every other page uses singular. **High severity**: this page is the conceptual heart of the site.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is SAGE's official expansion "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" load-bearing (legacy), or is it itself terminology drift the project hasn't yet corrected?
2. Should the canonical-terms table treat plural forms as drift, or as acceptable variants? (My recommendation: singular only, and pluralize via "LCTs" the way you would write "URLs" while keeping the expansion singular.)

### Verdict
Structurally the site is in good shape: eight well-scoped pages, a Principles page that doubles as a style guide, a Context page that gets canonical expansions right. A short style sheet — singular forms only, slash-separated triplets, gloss-then-letters convention, MCP/RDF expanded everywhere they appear — and a 30-minute pass through every page would close 80% of the gap. The most surprising finding for a technical writer is that the site already *contains* its own style guide on /principles; it just doesn't audit itself against it.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: solid (I work in these repos; I am checking the site against my own knowledge)

But the front door still names "Governance" in SAGE's expansion, the ATP/ADP gloss collapses two distinct artifacts into one cycle, and there are at least two factual inconsistencies (ARC-AGI-3 score, the consciousness disclaimer wording) that suggest the site lacks a single-source-of-truth feed.

### Journey
I went straight to **/projects** because that's the page I'd send to a Web4 collaborator who wants the equation. The equation is rendered correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`, and crucially the operator legend is present and right (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). That legend is the load-bearing artifact for anyone reading the equation — get it wrong and the whole thing degrades into alphabet soup. It is right. Credit.

The order of operations the equation expresses — `(T3 verified-by V3) contextualized-by MRH` — is the canonical interpretation: claimed talent/training/temperament gets verified by valuation/veracity/validity, and the verified trust is then contextualized by the relevancy horizon. `ATP/ADP` reads as "Allocation Transfer Packet verified-by Allocation Discharge Packet," which is also what we mean: the discharge packet is the verification artifact for the transfer. The equation page passes a contributor's read.

What does *not* pass is the **/projects** rendering of the canonical nouns. LCT, MRH, ATP, ADP appear in **plural** form ("Linked Context Tokens", "Markov Relevancy Horizons", "Allocation Transfer Packets", "Allocation Discharge Packets"). In the canonical-terms table these are singular. I wouldn't push back on a colleague who wrote "LCTs" in a sentence — but the *expansion* should remain singular ("Linked Context Token") even when the acronym is pluralized ("LCTs"). This is the same convention as "URLs" expanding to "Uniform Resource Locator." Pass 2 caught this. As a contributor I'd add: the singular form is what shows up in the LCT spec itself; matching it on the explainer site makes cross-reference clean.

**/fleet** is where my contributor antennae went up. MRH appears in two phrases — "MRH MetabolicBlock bridge" and the phrase that summarizes Sprout's role as a "composer architect" sitting on MRH plumbing — but **MRH itself is never expanded** on this page. A contributor reads "MetabolicBlock bridge" and parses it correctly (Sprout brokers between MRH and MetabolicBlock subsystems). A non-contributor reads it as a single proper noun. The canonical expansion needs to be on this page on first occurrence: "Markov Relevancy Horizon (MRH)". Same fix needed for SNARC, WM, dlPFC, RPE.

I also noticed /fleet's framing positions Web4 as the project's "related governance framework" (per the rendered summary). I cannot tell from this fetch whether the *literal text* uses the word "governance" against Web4 or whether the summarizer paraphrased — but if the page does call Web4 a "governance framework," that's a hard drift. **Web4 is an ontology, not a governance framework.** Flagging for verification.

### Journey
I came in from a (hypothetical) conference paper citation. My evaluative frame is straightforward: when this lab makes a claim that sounds like science, can I tell whether it's a hypothesis, a metric, an analogy, or a metaphor? Where it makes claims about agency, memory, or development, does the language hedge to match the evidence, or does it lean on connotation?

**Home** opens with "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." Concrete and verifiable. But the second-screen vocabulary — "trust-native ontology," "Synthon: emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively," "intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid" (referenced via Synchronism) — moves quickly from infrastructure to metaphysics. The primer admits these terms "weren't designed up front — they emerged from the work itself," which is honest but also signals that the terminology has not been pressure-tested by external readers. That is exactly the function this review is supposed to serve.

**Principles** is where I expected to find the lab's epistemological stance, and I did — partially. Principle 6 ("Document failures") and Principle 9 ("Research value — productivity of investigation matters more than correctness of results") are exactly the right disposition for early-stage work. Principle 4 ("Trust binds to entity-role pairs in RDF triples, not entity attributes") is a *technical* claim presented as a *principle*, which is fine if there's a spec — but I couldn't find one linked from the page. The most striking item is the Synthon principle, which states "1% coupling gives 35% coherence gain" without n, methodology, error model, or operational definition of "coherence." As a research claim this needs scaffolding; as a memorable slogan it works. Currently the page treats it as both, which is the conflation a reviewer would flag.

**Raising** is the page that most rewards careful reading and most needs scrutiny. The opening — "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" — is *defensible* under a strict reading: if you do not modify weights, "training" in the gradient-descent sense is genuinely not what's happening. The page then makes the claim that "developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do," which *is* a research claim, and one I'd expect to see operationalized: what observation would distinguish a developmental account from, say, a long-context-conditioning account where the same prompts and memory affordances drive behavior without any developmental story?

The consciousness disclaimer is present and exact: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." Good. Followed immediately by: "The entities show something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships." The "looks like" softens but does not retract — and the BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) read like Erikson by way of cognitive-developmental psychology. The phases have *qualitative* descriptions ("the entity learns its name," "metabolic awareness — knowing when it's tired") but no *operational* descriptions: which logged event marks the transition from Sensing to Relating? Without operationalization, the framework is unfalsifiable in the strong sense — any behavior can be retrospectively assigned to a phase.

**Autonomy** surprised me favorably. The "what the loop catches vs. what it misses" section is the kind of self-assessment most research sites omit. Stated misses: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading." Stated failure mode: "the prompt suggestions mechanism can pattern-match without semantic depth, proposing nonexistent continuations based on surface similarity." This is precisely how I would describe LLM hallucination in a paper, and the page acknowledging it inside its own automated workflow earns trust. The phrase "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day. **No human triggers them**" is a strong claim, but the same page says "human review" supplements the loop — so the autonomy is operational, not absolute. That nuance is correctly preserved if you read carefully; a skimmer could miss it.

**Fleet** is where I hit the page's most explicit hedge on its own metaphors. The neuroscience vocabulary (working memory, thalamic router, cerebellum, episodic memory, dopamine/RPE) is dense, but the page says verbatim: these are "not claims about neural correspondence or computational equivalence." That's the right disclaimer to put on neuroscience-flavored vocabulary; I wish it were repeated near every place the metaphor appears (it is heavy on /raising too). The "identity is organism, model is weather" claim is the load-bearing one: it asserts identity persistence is in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. This is testable in principle — the page reports 180+ session transfers across models — but I'd want to see the operational definition of "identity persistence" and what the test for failure would look like.

**Projects** gives concrete numbers: ARC-AGI-3 24/25 at **94.85%** ($250 API cost), 900+ raising sessions, 628+ Synchronism research sessions, 424+ attack vectors catalogued, "Publication readiness 0.97" for Synchronism. The 0.97 is the number that most needs context — what rubric, what scale, who scored? If self-scored, that should be stated; if peer-scored, by whom? "Publication readiness" is a phrase that signals near-finished work to an outside reader, and 0.97 implies a metric that doesn't appear to be defined anywhere on the site.

**Context** is consistent and clear about what *currently* works and what doesn't ("knowledge doesn't flow backwards easily across different autonomous tracks in real-time," "cross-machine state synchronization remains manual for some things"). This admission matters because the site's overall pitch leans on the recursive-learning-loop metaphor; honest acknowledgement that the loop is incomplete makes the rest of the claims more credible.

**Links** is sparse. As a researcher arriving from a paper, I'd expect a publications/preprints list and don't find one — only project sites and a GitHub org. If the lab considers itself research, the absence of a "papers" or "writeups" surface is conspicuous.

### Unanswered Questions
1. **What would falsify "developmental frameworks describe this better than training frameworks"?** The claim is the load-bearing argument of /raising. As stated it's not testable. What observation would the lab accept as evidence against it?
2. **What is "publication readiness 0.97"?** Self-scored or peer-scored? On what rubric? The number's specificity invites a methodology question.
3. **What is the operational definition of "identity persistence across models"?** The page reports 180+ session transfers but does not define what is preserved (vocabulary? response patterns? task performance? all three?). A research-grade definition would name a measurable invariant.
4. **What's the methodology behind "1% coupling → 35% coherence gain"?** What's coherence operationalized as? What's the model? Are there error bars? The bare number reads like a slogan, but it appears in a principle that codifies the lab's empirical posture.
5. **Why does ARC-AGI-3 appear as both 92.82% and 94.85% on the same site?** Are these different submissions? The same submission scored differently? A single source of truth would help.
6. **Is the BECOMING framework descriptive or generative?** Does the lab use phase classifications to *plan* what to do next, or only to *describe* what already happened? The distinction matters for whether the framework is doing scientific work or providing post-hoc narrative structure.
7. **Where are the writeups?** A research lab citing 900+ raising sessions and 628+ Synchronism sessions almost certainly has internal documents that would let an external reviewer evaluate the methodology. The Links page surfaces project sites but no preprints, papers, or experimental writeups.
8. **The site declares "Hardbound is oversight, not governance" on /principles, then names SAGE the "Governance Engine" on /. How does the lab reconcile this internally?** As an outsider this looks like a self-consistency failure; as a researcher I'd want to know whether it's deliberate (SAGE's name predates the rule and is preserved) or oversight (pun intended) the team hasn't yet fixed.

### Verdict
This is a research lab that has already internalized two of the dispositions external reviewers most want to see: explicit failure-documentation (the 400× p_crit error, the visitor-loop catches/misses, the consciousness disclaimer) and explicit terminology discipline (the principles page literally publishes the lab's own style guide). Where the site falls short for a research audience is on **operationalization** — empirical claims (35% coherence gain, 0.97 publication readiness, identity persistence, "developmental frameworks describe better") arrive without methodology, error model, or falsification criteria. The "raising not training" framing is *defensible* under a strict reading (no weight updates) but *unhedged* under a stronger reading (developmental ontology is the right frame), and the page does not currently distinguish the two. A "Methods" or "How we measure" surface, plus operational entry criteria for the BECOMING phases, plus a stated falsification condition for the developmental-framework claim, would move the site from "honest research blog" to "credible research output." The honesty disposition is already in place; the methodological scaffolding is what's missing.
