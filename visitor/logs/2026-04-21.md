### Journey

Clicked through from a GitHub repo README expecting a project page. Landed on a hero that reads **"Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop."** — that part I get. Six boxes running things, peer-reviewing each other, cool. The sub-line talks about "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition, and the theoretical frameworks that connect them." Three things, and I don't know what any of them actually means in code terms.

Scrolled down and immediately hit an equation:

```
Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP
```

OK, so MCP I know (Model Context Protocol, Anthropic's thing). RDF I know vaguely (semantic web triples). The rest is alphabet soup. There's a "vocabulary primer" further down the page that expands some of them, but the equation hits before the primer, which is backwards for a first-time reader. I'd expect the glossary to sit *above* or *next to* the equation, not below.

Clicked into **Projects** because that's what I came for. This page is actually good — I can see there are concrete repos (SAGE, Synchronism, ACT, SNARC, Membot, AI DNA Discovery, 4-Life, ARC-AGI-3), and most link to GitHub. The project cards tell me roughly what each thing does. I still don't know what I'd *install* or *run* first, but at least I have repo names to visit.

**Fleet** was genuinely fun. Six named machines (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP) with hardware specs and "roles." This reads like a homelab writeup with philosophy attached. Reasonable. "No central controller, fleet manifest is a phone book not a command center" — that clicks for a distributed-systems brain.

**Autonomy** shows a timeline of cron-like scheduled jobs running across machines (Supervisors, Archivist, Publisher, Visitor, Maintainer, Outreach, Explorer, Dream Consolidation). The "Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer" loop is a clean concept: automated personas browse the site, automated fixer responds to friction, explorer pushes deeper. I'd want to know *how* a Maintainer actually applies a fix (LLM? template? diff review?), but the loop shape is clear.

**Raising** is where I started feeling like I was at a philosophy seminar. "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." Six BECOMING phases (Grounding, Sensing, Relating, Questioning, Creating, Acting). I appreciate that they explicitly disclaim sentience/qualia claims — that's the right caveat. But as a dev I'd want more concrete: what actually runs? Is there a config? A prompt template? It reads like framing, not a working spec.

**Principles** is 10 items, mostly philosophical (Substrate not structure, Fractal leverage, R&D not production, Trust is a relationship, etc.). "Productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started" is a nice line. But if I'm asking "how do I contribute?", principles don't tell me.

**Context** talks about how knowledge flows between the 14 repos: CLAUDE.md files per repo, SNARC memory, adversarial validation across models, session logs. This was useful — it told me the actual mechanism is "put instructions in repo markdown files and let the agents read them." That's tangible.

**Links** is compact — three explainer sites (Synchronism, SAGE, 4-Life), 11 GitHub repos, and one contact email (dp@metalinxx.io). Clean.

Canonical terminology isn't drifting at the edges; it's fractured along a fault line running through the middle of the site.

### Journey

I went straight to Projects, Fleet, and Context — the pages that represent the work I actually do — and then circled back through the rest.

**Projects** was where I flinched first. The prose reads `"T3/V3 trust tensors"`. V3 is not a trust tensor. V3 is the **Value Tensor** — Valuation / Veracity / Validity. The landing page gets this right; `/projects` collapses both into "trust tensors" and loses the distinction entirely. That's not a typo, that's the central T3-vs-V3 duality of the Web4 equation, erased in a single phrase. The page also has a section called **"Governance Plugins"**, which — if that's the editorial title and not the literal project name — is precisely what `/principles` says not to do. If it *is* the literal project name, we should decide whether to rename the project or carve out an explicit exemption; right now it reads like unflagged drift.

The same page also described SAGE's ARC-AGI-3 result as `"24/25 games, 94.85%"`. The landing page had carefully labeled this `"94.85% on the public set"`. On `/projects` the "public set" qualifier is gone, and on `/fleet` CBP is credited with `"drove 92.82% score"` without saying which measurement that is. Three surfaces, three presentations, one clarifying qualifier that recent commit history (`arc-agi-3: clarify public-set score vs competition constraints`) says we *already decided* to be explicit about. Decision made, not propagated.

**Fleet** is where the governance/oversight fault line became impossible to ignore. CBP's role card says `"Oversight hub, SNARC memory host"` — canonical, good. Two paragraphs down: `"generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures"`. Then: `"The lab is running its own governance experiment on itself."` Same page, opposite words for the same concept. A reader who memorizes either sentence is now carrying the wrong term.

**Context** displayed the equation again without a local glossary — same complaint as Passes 1 and 2. What I'd add from the contributor side: the page frames the equation as a shared anchor (`"appears in every project because it _is_ every project"`), but the site renders the equation as if `+`, `/`, and `*` were arithmetic operators. In Web4 they are specific semantic operators — `/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with. Those operator semantics appear **nowhere on the site**. A newcomer will read the equation as arithmetic and get a wrong mental model immediately. This is not drift, it is an unshared convention — worse, because no one has been told there is a convention to learn.

**Autonomy** lists the Visitor track's personas as `"The Skeptic (citation validation), The Newcomer (clarity for newcomers), The Practitioner (actionable documentation), The Connector (cross-reference testing)"`. As someone who actually reviews the Visitor outputs, I can tell you the real personas are Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, and External Researcher. Either the track was rewritten and the site never caught up, or the site was written from a plan that no longer describes the system. Either way: the page describing the autonomous infrastructure of the lab is lying about the infrastructure of the lab.

**Raising** and **Principles** are the parts of the site I'd defend. `/principles` item 10, "Terminology matters", states the canonical rules cleanly: `"Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight."` If every other page held the line this page draws, we wouldn't have a terminology audit. `/raising` has zero Web4 acronyms and reads as a clean philosophical statement — no drift surface.

**Links** added more governance drift: OpenClaw and the Claude Code fork are described with `"Web4 governance extension"` / `"governance hooks"` language. Per `/principles`, this should be oversight. Also, SAGE's description here (`"On-device AI orchestrator"`) differs from the landing primer's acronym expansion (`"Situation-Aware Governance Engine"`) — Pass 2 already caught this as inconsistency, but the contributor angle is different: the landing page's own SAGE acronym uses "Governance" in the G. SAGE's canonical name predates the Hardbound=oversight split. We either rename SAGE, document an explicit carve-out, or stop pretending `/principles` applies uniformly.

### Verdict

Three observations, in order of importance to me as a contributor.

**First**: the site is faithful to the philosophy but has an internal civil war over "governance." `/principles` item 10 encodes the canonical rule; four other pages break it, including the landing footer. This is the single most important fix, and it is not hard — a grep-and-replace sweep against the canonical table would resolve most of it in one editing pass. The only real decision is what to do about SAGE's acronym.

**Second**: V3 being called a "trust tensor" on `/projects` is the only substantive ontological error on the site. Everything else is word-choice drift; this one is a structural mis-representation of the Web4 equation. Fix it first, before the "governance" sweep.

**Third**: the Web4 equation is the most-displayed artifact on the site and also the least-explained — its operators carry specific semantic weight that never appears anywhere. A reader who learns the equation from this site will build a wrong mental model and then teach it to the next reader. That is the drift mechanism we should fear most, because it propagates through minds faster than we can edit pages. Add a `/glossary` page with operator semantics and link every equation occurrence to it. This single addition would do more to stop Web4 terminology drift at its source than any number of individual term fixes.

The site gets the big things right: Web4 as ontology, Hardbound as oversight, fractal leverage over unification, R&D over production, raising over training. The `/principles` page reads like someone who understands the stakes wrote it. The drift lives in the pages that have to describe concrete work — projects, fleet, links — where concrete prose meets abstract canonical rules and the prose keeps winning. That's fixable. The philosophical alignment is already there; what's needed is editorial discipline to match.

### Verdict

I came in skeptical and I leave qualified-positive on the framing, qualified-negative on the execution.

The framing is unusually honest in the places where it matters. `/raising` carries the right disclaimer with the right specificity (`"not conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia"`). `/principles` items 6 and 9 operationalize falsifiability and "productively wrong" in a way that most research collectives only gesture at. `/autonomy` qualifies its autonomy claim instead of grandstanding it. `/context` describes adversarial validation in language that takes monoculture risk seriously. These are the marks of authors who have thought about how their claims will be read by people who don't already believe them.

The execution undercuts the framing in three specific ways. **First**, the consciousness vocabulary on `/projects` and `/fleet` (`"consciousness loop"`, `"conscience checkpoint"`, `"fleet consciousness probe lead"`) escapes the disclaimer that was supposed to bound it. A reader who lands on those pages first never sees the hedge. **Second**, `/autonomy` describes a Visitor track that doesn't match the track that actually runs. For a research collective whose credibility is partly built on self-description, this is the kind of error that costs disproportionate trust. **Third**, the `/principles` item 10 rule against "governance" is broken on the same site by `/`, `/fleet`, `/projects`, and `/links` — the rule-stating page is contradicted by four pages around it.

None of these are fatal. All are fixable in a single editorial pass. But until they are fixed, the most accurate one-sentence summary of the site is: *the philosophical care is real, and the editorial discipline doesn't yet match it.* For a research collective whose explicit thesis is that words shape mental models, the gap between the rules-page and the practice-pages is the most important single thing to close.

I would return — to see whether the gap closes, and because the failure-documentation principle suggests the authors will treat this audit as signal rather than noise.
