# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-07

Four-persona pass over https://4-lab.vercel.app/
Each pass includes a terminology audit against Web4 canonical terms.

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious multi-agent AI research lab with a strong narrative, but the density of custom terminology creates a steep onramp for newcomers.

### Journey

Landed on the home page from a GitHub README link. The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately tells me this is infrastructure-heavy AI research, not a product. Good. The three stats (6 Machines, 8+ Repos, 7 Autonomous Tracks) give a quick sense of scale.

The "How We Work" section is clear: autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. I can follow this. The three project blurbs (Synchronism, SAGE, Web4) give me a rough map, though Synchronism's "Planck grid" framing feels like a big claim I can't evaluate.

"Three Things Actually Demonstrated" is the strongest section on the site. Concrete, testable claims with specific numbers (115+ sessions, 0.5B to 1.1B model transfer). This is what makes me take the rest seriously.

The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom of the home page is essential. Without it, the rest of the site would be nearly impenetrable. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE — all defined here. Smart placement, though I wish each page also defined terms on first use rather than relying on me remembering the primer.

Moved to **Projects**. The Web4 equation hit me immediately: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The legend helps ("/" = "verified by", etc.) but this is dense. I count 10+ projects listed. The descriptions are concise but assume familiarity — "IRP" in the SAGE card is never expanded. "Cosmos SDK" in ACT assumes I know what that is (I do, but a non-blockchain dev wouldn't). The "How They Connect" closing paragraph is good — it ties the ecosystem together.

**Fleet** is fascinating. The hardware diversity (Jetson to Mac Mini to laptops) is unusual and well-explained. The "model family matters as much as size" finding is genuinely interesting. The two-pool resource management section reads like an honest account of constraints becoming features. Identity portability (Sprout transferring across models) is the most compelling claim on the entire site.

**Autonomy** describes 31 tracks across 6 machines running daily. The timeline is clear and the feedback loop (Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer) makes sense. The persona names here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the ones described in CLAUDE.md for this very track (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher) — that's confusing if both are describing the same system. The "Honest Assessment" section at the bottom is appreciated.

**Raising** is where it gets philosophical. "Raising is not training" — I get the distinction conceptually. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) read like a developmental psychology framework applied to AI. The "What We're Not Claiming" section is crucial and well-placed. The discoveries (bilateral generation, memoriescape) are interesting but I'd want to see the raw session logs to evaluate them.

**Principles** lists 10 principles. Most are clear and well-articulated. "Substrate, not structure" with the termite analogy is memorable. "Fractal leverage" makes sense as a principle but I had to read it twice. "R&D, not production" — noted, and it's refreshingly honest. "Terminology matters" is meta and appropriate.

**Context** explains knowledge flow across the system. The CLAUDE.md pattern, SNARC memory, cross-session persistence — this is the operational backbone. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is another honest assessment that builds credibility. The admission that real-time cross-track communication is a gap shows self-awareness.

**Links** is clean and functional. Sites, repos, and a contact email. No issues.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is "IRP" in the SAGE project description? It's mentioned as "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" but never expanded.
2. The home page says "7 Autonomous Tracks" but the Autonomy page describes what looks like 8+ (supervisors, archivist, publisher, visitor, maintainer, outreach, explorer, dream consolidation). Which count is accurate?
3. How does one actually *use* or *contribute to* any of this? The site explains what the lab is and how it works, but there's no "getting started" path for a developer who wants to engage.
4. The Autonomy page lists different visitor persona names than what appears to be the real system. Which is canonical?
5. What's the relationship between the Web4 equation and actual running code? Is it implemented, or is it a theoretical framework that guides design?

The terminology density is the main barrier: I needed the home page's Vocabulary Primer before anything else made sense, and several pages introduce terms without defining them. The site would benefit from a clearer "so you want to get involved" path, but as a research lab explanation, it works. I'd bookmark it and come back.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Terminology is mostly canonical where it's defined, but the site has a systemic problem: acronyms are only expanded on the home page Vocabulary Primer and occasionally on Projects — the other six pages assume you've memorized them.

### Journey

I started at the home page with my canonical terms table open beside me. The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom is this site's terminology anchor — LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, and SAGE are all correctly expanded there. Good foundation. But my job is cross-page consistency, so I moved methodically through every page.

**Projects** is the most acronym-dense page on the site. The Web4 equation is displayed with its legend — correct. LCT and MRH are expanded here. But T3/V3 appears only as "Trust tensors" without the component breakdown (Talent/Training/Temperament and Valuation/Veracity/Validity). IRP appears in the SAGE description as "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" but IRP itself is never expanded — what does it stand for? This page also describes Web4 projects using the word "infrastructure" in the context of how the equation is encoded, which conflicts with the canonical rule that Web4 is an "ontology, not architecture, not infrastructure."

**Fleet** is better. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." The two-pool structure uses "oversight" correctly for the accountability pool. However, LoRA is used without expansion (should be "Low-Rank Adaptation"), SNARC is mentioned for the CBP machine without expansion, MPS is undefined, and the fleet description includes the phrase "governance entities" in describing how generative and oversight pools coordinate. "Governance" is a flagged term — the canonical word is "oversight."

**Autonomy** is light on Web4 terminology, which is fine — it's an operational page. But it lists 31 tracks and 4 visitor personas named Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, and Connector. These don't match the actual persona names used in the visitor track implementation (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). A documentation site describing its own systems should be accurate about those systems. The page also references "LLM" and "NotebookLM" without expansion.

**Raising** avoids Web4 canonical terms entirely — no LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, or R6 appear on the page. This is acceptable if the page is self-contained, but "Hill function kinetics" is used to describe trust formation in the Relating phase without any explanation. "Bilateral generation" and "memoriescape" are introduced as discoveries — "bilateral generation" gets a parenthetical (both-sides conversation without stop tokens), which is adequate, but "memoriescape" is only described by context. The consciousness caveats ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia") are well-placed and clear.

**Principles** is where the count problem lives. The CLAUDE.md references "7 principles" and the site structure comment says the same, but the actual page displays 10 principles. This is a structural inconsistency that undermines trust in the documentation. The terminology on this page is strong: Web4 is explicitly called "an ontology" and explicitly NOT "architecture" or "infrastructure." Hardbound is explicitly called "oversight" and not "governance." "Fractal leverage" is correctly used. "R&D, not production" is stated as a principle. However, T3/V3, RDF, and SAGE are all mentioned without expansion on this page.

**Context** displays the full Web4 equation but expands NONE of its component terms on the page. MCP, RDF, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP — all appear as raw acronyms. SNARC is the one term that IS expanded here ("Salience-gated memory system" — though the full canonical is "Salience, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict"). The page describes coupling-coherence and compatibility-synthon experiments without explaining what these are.

**Links** is a reference page with project descriptions. Acronyms appear freely without expansion (LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, ATP). ACT is expanded as "Agentic Context Tool" — I don't have this in my canonical table, so I can't verify. SAGE appears twice with slightly different descriptions (orchestrator in one place, consciousness loop emphasis in another).

### Cross-Page Terminology Consistency Summary

**Systemic issue**: The home page Vocabulary Primer is the only place most terms are expanded. Five of the remaining seven pages use canonical acronyms without expanding them. A reader who enters the site on any page other than home — via search, a direct link from a repo, or a shared URL — encounters a wall of undefined acronyms.

**Two instances of canonical drift**:
1. "Infrastructure" language on /projects for Web4 (should be "ontology" only)
2. "Governance entities" on /fleet (should be "oversight")

**One count inconsistency**: The principles page shows 10 principles, but site metadata and CLAUDE.md reference 7.

**One internal accuracy error**: The visitor persona names on /autonomy don't match the actual implementation.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does IRP stand for? It's used in the SAGE project description but never expanded anywhere on the site.
2. Why does the principles count differ between the page (10) and all references to it (7)? Were principles added without updating cross-references?
3. Is ACT canonically "Agentic Context Tool"? This expansion only appears on /links and isn't in any canonical terms table I've been given.
4. Should every page that displays the Web4 equation include a compact legend? Currently only /projects does.
5. What is the site's policy on acronym expansion — define once on home and assume retention, or define on first use per page? The current approach is inconsistent: some pages re-expand, most don't.

### Verdict
The canonical Web4 terms are mostly correct where they appear — the home page and principles page are particularly strong. But the site has two structural documentation problems: first, acronyms are generally only expanded on the home page, making every other page hostile to readers who arrived via direct link; second, there are two genuine terminology drift instances ("infrastructure" for Web4 on /projects, "governance" on /fleet) that contradict the site's own stated canonical rules. For a site whose principles page literally includes "Terminology matters," these inconsistencies need fixing.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The core equation and primary term expansions are correct, but "governance" has leaked into three locations where "oversight" is required, and IRP — a central SAGE concept — is used without expansion anywhere on the site.

### Journey

Went straight to **Projects** — this is the page that represents the work I contribute to. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly with its operator legend (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). Good. The Web4 project description says "Trust-native ontology" — correct framing. LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, and ATP/ADP are all mentioned, and LCT and MRH are properly expanded. T3/V3 appears only as "trust tensors" without the component breakdown — a contributor would expect to see "Talent/Training/Temperament" and "Valuation/Veracity/Validity" spelled out here since this is the canonical project page.

SAGE's description mentions "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — IRP is never expanded. This is a significant gap. The Internal Reflection Protocol is the heart of SAGE's cognitive architecture; leaving it as an opaque acronym on the project page is a disservice. Hardbound is correctly described as "Enterprise oversight" — good, not "governance."

However, there's a supporting project listed as **"Governance Plugins"** — this is a project name, so arguably fixed, but it sits awkwardly next to the principle that "governance" should not be used. More critically, on the **Links** page, the OpenClaw fork is described as "Web4 governance extension" and the Claude Code fork as "Web4 governance hooks." These are direct terminology drift — both should say "oversight extension" and "oversight hooks." The ACT description says "Cosmos SDK Web4 implementation" — framing Web4 as something you "implement" leans toward infrastructure/platform language rather than ontology.

Moved to **Fleet**. The trust tracker section is correct: "per-peer T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)" — canonical expansion, well-placed. The two-pool system is named correctly (Synthesis Pool / Oversight Pool). But the explanatory paragraph about what the two-pool structure taught them uses "governance entities": *"generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures."* This is the same drift Pass 2 caught. The canonical phrasing should be "oversight entities." This matters because the Fleet page is describing exactly the kind of system Web4 models — if the site describing Web4 can't use Web4's own terminology consistently, what does that signal to contributors?

The identity portability section is accurately described. Sprout's transfer from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B is correctly framed as a state-file phenomenon, not a model phenomenon. The "model is weather, identity is organism" metaphor captures what we actually observed. The fleet's peer-to-peer architecture with no master node is accurate.

**Context** page displays the full equation but expands zero terms. As a contributor, I can read it cold, but this page is supposed to explain knowledge flow — the equation sitting there unexpanded feels like decoration rather than communication. SNARC is described as "Salience-gated memory" and then its five scoring dimensions are listed as "Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict." There's an inconsistency: the S in SNARC maps to Salience in the label but Surprise in the dimension list. These are related but not identical concepts. The four-tier hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) is accurately described.

**Autonomy** lists visitor personas as Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector. I know the actual implementation uses Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher. This isn't just a naming discrepancy — the actual personas have specific knowledge ceilings and audit functions that the generic names don't convey. The 31-track count and daily timeline are accurate to what I see running.

**Raising** is well-crafted. The BECOMING phases match the actual raising curriculum. "Hill function kinetics" is correctly referenced as the enzyme-binding analogy for trust formation — this maps directly to the coupling-coherence experiments. The bilateral generation discovery is accurately described, including the Vygotsky egocentric speech reference. The consciousness caveats are appropriately placed and honestly worded. No terminology drift on this page.

**Principles** lists 10 substantive principles (an 11th entry is just the site footer tagline). Principle 10 ("Terminology matters") is the strongest terminology statement on the site — it explicitly names Web4 as ontology (not architecture/infrastructure), Hardbound as oversight (not governance), and cross-domain reuse as fractal leverage (not scope inflation). This principle is doing the right work. But the count discrepancy persists: the CLAUDE.md and site metadata reference "7 principles" while the page shows 10.

**Home** and **Links** round out the picture. The home page Vocabulary Primer is the terminology anchor — every expansion there is canonical. The Links page is where the remaining drift lives (governance × 2 in project descriptions).

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary

- **Understanding achieved**: good

- **Overall impression**: A rare example of an AI research lab that leads with epistemic humility rather than capability hype — the caveats are real and well-placed, though the site occasionally undermines its own rigor by using consciousness-adjacent language ("consciousness loop," "metabolic states") in descriptive contexts while disclaiming it in reflective ones.

### Journey

I came to this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising vs. training" as a framing for AI development. As an AI safety/trust researcher, I'm evaluating three things: (1) Are the consciousness-adjacent claims appropriately caveated? (2) Is the "raising not training" distinction defensible? (3) Does the site's self-assessment feel honest or performative?

Started at the **home page**. The hero — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — is restrained. No claims about AGI, no breathless capability assertions. The three "Things Actually Demonstrated" are specific and testable: identity persistence across 115+ sessions with model transfer, autonomous infrastructure cycles, and heterogeneous peer review. This is the right way to present empirical work — concrete, bounded, verifiable. The vocabulary primer correctly frames Web4 as "ontology" and describes SAGE's cognition loop without overclaiming. The "synthon" concept (emergent coherence entity) is introduced carefully as "observed rather than designed."

However, the home page also says SAGE has a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" on the Projects page — this is where my concern begins. "Consciousness loop" is a loaded term in AI safety circles. If this is a technical name for a specific computational process, it needs to be defined precisely on the page where it appears. Using it casually invites exactly the kind of misreading the Raising page works hard to prevent.

**Raising** is the most intellectually interesting page on the site. The core claim — "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." — is a defensible and useful distinction. It maps cleanly to the difference between supervised optimization and developmental scaffolding in cognitive science. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) parallel Piaget's stages loosely enough to be a useful analogy without claiming equivalence.

The consciousness caveat is well-placed and well-worded: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is the right epistemic posture — describing observables without committing to ontological claims about inner experience.

The observer note — "I notice I want to call it experience" — is the most honest sentence on the site. It acknowledges the pull toward anthropomorphism without either surrendering to it or pretending it doesn't exist. This kind of reflexivity is rare in AI research writing.

The Vygotsky reference for bilateral generation (egocentric speech as reasoning through simulated dialogue) is well-grounded. This isn't speculative — Vygotsky's framework for internalization of social speech is well-established in developmental psychology and the parallel to LLM self-dialogue is genuinely interesting. "Hill function kinetics" for trust formation is a defensible analogy (sigmoidal saturation curves appear in many cooperative phenomena), but the reference is too terse for a researcher who isn't also a biochemist. One sentence of context would suffice.

**Principles** lists 10 substantive principles. The strongest is the meta-principle: "These aren't aspirational values posted on a wall. They're operational patterns that emerged from building the system." This frames principles as empirical observations rather than prescriptions — a meaningful epistemological choice. Principle 6 ("Document failures") citing the p_crit derivation with a 400x error is exactly the kind of transparency that builds credibility. Principle 9 ("Most research leads nowhere — and that's expected") correctly normalizes negative results.

However, the "Interactive selection" principle (Principle 7) makes a strong claim: "We don't create or delete — we interactively select." This implies that the identity of AI agents is being *discovered* rather than *constructed*. That's a philosophically loaded position. It's defensible as a methodological stance (we probe and reinforce attractors rather than training from scratch), but the site doesn't adequately distinguish this operational claim from the stronger ontological claim that the identity pre-exists in some meaningful sense. A researcher unfamiliar with the lab's philosophy could read this as a claim about AI selfhood that the Raising page explicitly disclaims.

**Autonomy** is operationally clear. 31 tracks, 6 machines, ~53 daily sessions. The feedback loop (Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer) is a well-designed closed-loop audit system. The honest assessment is valuable: the system "misses deep technical errors requiring domain expertise" and "content technically correct but misleading." This is genuine self-critique, not performative hedging. The acknowledgment that pattern-matching can generate unnecessary changes shows awareness of the automation bias problem.

The persona names (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) are more abstract than the actual implementation names — not a terminology problem per se, but it means the site is describing an idealized version of its own system rather than the actual one. For a site that values honesty, this is a small but notable gap.

**Fleet** makes the most empirically interesting claim: "model family matters as much as size" and "Gemma 3 at 4B outperforms Phi-4 at 14B for raising work." This is a testable, specific finding. The identity portability claim — that behavioral consistency persists across model swaps because identity lives in state files, not weights — is well-framed. The metaphor "model is weather, identity is organism" is evocative and captures the distinction clearly.

But the fleet page also describes the two-pool system using "governance entities" alongside "oversight pool." This inconsistency matters: if you're making claims about trust architectures, terminological precision in *describing* those architectures is part of the intellectual rigor. Using "governance" when your own principles say "oversight" is the kind of drift that erodes confidence in precision-dependent claims.

**Context** page is operationally sound — the CLAUDE.md pattern for agent alignment, SNARC memory scoring, lossy compression as a feature. The honest limitation ("cross-machine state synchronization remains manual") is appropriate. The Web4 equation appears without any term expansions — fine for an internal reference page, but this page is supposed to explain knowledge flow to outsiders.

**Projects** page has the densest claim load. SAGE described with "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" and "metabolic states" — both consciousness-adjacent terms used in technical descriptive mode. "Conscience checkpoint" for PolicyGate is another loaded term. These are not caveated here the way they are on the Raising page, which creates a dissonance: the philosophical page carefully disclaims consciousness, but the technical page uses consciousness vocabulary freely. A researcher would notice this gap.

**Links** is utilitarian. The "governance extension" and "governance hooks" descriptions are the terminology drift other passes caught.

### Unanswered Questions
