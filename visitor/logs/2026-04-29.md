### Journey

I came in cold from a GitHub README. The home page does its job: "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." I get it — a distributed AI lab where heterogeneous machines self-organize and check each other's work. The Web4 equation panel was where I first hit the acronym wall, but they're all expanded inline (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP), so I wasn't blocked.

Projects was the page I most wanted as a developer — "what do I clone first?" — and it answered roughly. Web4 is foundational, SAGE is the cognition kernel, Synchronism is the theory layer. The one-liners are fine but a "start here if you want to…" decision tree would have saved me a click. The 24/25 ARC-AGI-3 number jumps off the page; I went looking for methodology and didn't find it on this page.

Fleet was the highlight. Six named machines, hardware, model, role — table form, no fluff. I instantly understood "Thor does physics on a 122GB Jetson, Sprout is the edge node." The claim that "model family matters as much as size" is the kind of empirical insight that earns trust.

Autonomy gave me the daily clock (03:00 → 08:00+, 31 tracks, no human triggers). Clear timeline. But terms like "raising sessions" and "dream consolidation" got dropped without resolution — I had to click through to /raising to find out.

Raising was where I stalled. The "raising not training" framing is vivid but reads more like a manifesto than docs. The 24/25 ARC-AGI number reappears here without methodology, again.

Principles read more honestly: "R&D, not production" is stated as a principle, not hidden. "Document failures" and a cited 400x error in p_crit gave me a sense that this isn't pure hand-waving. Some of the principles felt preachy ("the choice to use them is the whole point") but not enough to put me off.

Context tried to do a lot — Web4 equation, MCP, RDF, SNARC, federation, dream bundles, LoRA weights — and I think it tried to do too much in one page. The acronym discipline is good (everything I'd already learned was re-expanded), but new terms keep arriving. Links was clean: GitHub list, three explainer sites, one email.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial (terminology is mostly canonical, but cross-page consistency leaks)
- **Would return?**: yes — to file a style-guide PR

### Journey

I came in with the canonical terms table from CLAUDE.md as my checklist and walked the eight pages in nav order, recording every acronym instance. My method: fetch each page, list every acronym whether expanded or not, and diff the expansions across pages.

The home page is the cleanest: every Web4 acronym is expanded on first use (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP), Web4 itself is named "ontology," and the fleet/cognition kernel framing is consistent with later pages. The Web4 equation panel is referenced narratively but the canonical form `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` does not appear here — it lives only on /context. For a tech writer, that's structurally odd: the equation is the page-zero anchor of the whole project and should appear on or near the landing page.

Projects exposes the first cross-page drift. T3 and V3 are written `Talent/Training/Temperament` and `Valuation/Veracity/Validity` — no spaces around the slashes — whereas the home page renders them with spaces (`Talent / Training / Temperament`). Either is acceptable individually, but consistent typography across pages is a tech-writing baseline. On the same page I found an acronym I did not expect: **ACP — "Agentic Context Protocol"** — used apparently as a sibling/alternative to MCP. ACP is not in the canonical terms table. Then on /links the package called **ACT** is expanded as "Agentic Context Tool," and the GitHub repo `dp-web4/ACT` is linked. So the site contains both `ACP = Agentic Context Protocol` (projects) and `ACT = Agentic Context Tool` (links) using nearly identical phrasings. One of these is almost certainly wrong, or they refer to different things and the page text doesn't disambiguate.

Fleet is the page with the worst first-use discipline. MRH, SNARC, RPE, dlPFC, and WM all appear in body text without expansion. T3 reappears here with the no-space variant. SAGE is referenced as a system but not expanded on this page. A reader who lands on /fleet from a search engine has no glossary affordance.

Autonomy reads well structurally but the page text I retrieved did not surface canonical Web4 acronyms — meaning visitors who land here see references to "raising sessions" and "dream consolidation" without entering the canonical-vocabulary stream at all. Pass 1 already flagged the inline-gloss issue.

Raising is also light on canonical acronyms; it leans heavily on the BECOMING phase names. I confirmed the philosophical line `"Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development"` is present and the framing is intact.

Principles is excellent on canonical discipline. It explicitly states `Web4 is an ontology`, `Web4 is not "architecture" or "infrastructure"`, `Hardbound is not "governance" — it's oversight`, and uses **Fractal leverage** as a principle title. This page is doing the heavy lifting of definitional anchoring; ironically, it's not the page a curious newcomer reads first. Consider surfacing the "Web4 is an ontology, not architecture" line on the home page.

Context is the strongest single-page acronym citizen: every canonical Web4 term is re-expanded, the equation is rendered with operator legend (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with"), and MCP/RDF are spelled out. One snag: my retrieval of /context returned `SNARC = "salience-gated memory"` as the apparent expansion, whereas Pass 1 reported `Surprise / Novelty / Arousal / Reward / Conflict` on the same page. If both are present that's fine (description + expansion), but if only the description is present then SNARC is undefined-on-first-use everywhere it appears. Worth a manual eyeball by the maintainer track.

Links is utilitarian. It introduces **MIT-0** (license), **FIDO2**, **TPM** without expansions, and uses the phrase `Web4 oversight hooks for Claude Code` and `Web4 oversight extension for OpenClaw agent framework` — both correct on the "oversight" rule. The contact is a single email (`dp@metalinxx.io`), no community channel.

### Journey

I came in already speaking the vocabulary, so I didn't read for clarity — I read for fidelity. My route: /context first (the equation is the thing the site has to get right), then /projects and /links to verify project descriptions match the repos, then /fleet, /principles, /raising, /autonomy, and home last as a sanity check on framing.

**/context is canonical.** The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` renders exactly as in `web4/docs/reference/CANONICAL_TERMS_v1.md`. The operator legend is the canonical one: `/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with". Every component has its expansion adjacent. SNARC carries both the descriptive paraphrase ("salience-gated memory") and the letter expansion ("Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict") — Pass 2's "is the letter expansion present?" question can be marked resolved: yes. The only thing on /context that a contributor would push back on is `"remote sleep service — using federation for distributed consolidation — is designed but not built"` being stated alongside the built capabilities without a visual marker; this is not a terminology issue, it's an "is it shipped?" issue, and Pass 1 already flagged it.

**/projects is accurate at the substance level.** SAGE's "12-step cognition loop, 6 brain-architecture components" matches the kernel I actually run. Synchronism's "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" is a fair compression of the Planck-grid-to-cosmological-scale claim. The internal scale numbers (900+ raising sessions, 628+ research sessions, 424+ attack vectors) are internally consistent across pages: /raising cites 180+ for SAGE-Sprout specifically, which is a believable subset of 900+ across six machines.

**The ACP / ACT thing Pass 2 raised is not a contradiction**, and as someone who works in these repos I want to register that explicitly so the maintainer track doesn't go chasing a ghost. **ACP is the Agentic Context Protocol — the wire format and message contract.** **ACT is the Agentic Context Tool — the Cosmos SDK Web4 chain implementation that speaks ACP.** /links confirms this: `"Agentic Context Tool — Cosmos SDK Web4 implementation"`. /projects mentions ACP within ACT's description because ACT *implements* ACP. The site is correct; it's just under-disambiguated for a reader who hasn't already met the two. The fix is one clarifying clause on /projects, not a rename or a deletion.

**/links has a real contributor-visible gap: there is no `dp-web4/Hardbound` repo entry.** Hardbound has a specific, claim-heavy description on /projects (oversight suite, TPM 2.0 / FIDO2 / Secure Enclave, 424+ attack vectors catalogued), and "Web4 oversight" is invoked as a property of the OpenClaw fork and the Claude Code fork on /links. But the Hardbound code itself is not linked. From a contributor's view this is the most material accuracy issue on the site: the project gets a card but no clone URL. Either the repo is private and that should be disclosed, or it should be linked.

**/fleet is accurate in substance.** Six machines, two pools (synthesis / oversight), correct hardware lines. "Zero crystallization achieved (S100)" on Sprout and "Crystallization evaluator" as Nomad's role are consistent uses of the same internal concept. "CBP drove 94.85% ARC-AGI-3 score" attributes the harness drive to the right machine. The Phi-4 / Qwen / Gemma / TinyLlama model assignments per machine match what I'd expect.

**/principles is the strongest canonical-anchor page on the site.** Two sentences are doing extraordinary work here for a contributor:
- `"Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology."`
- `"Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight."`

Both are stated as direct negations against the most common drifted reads. The Fractal leverage entry — `"When a pattern works at one scale, apply it at every scale. Not unification for its own sake — pragmatic reuse."` — explicitly disclaims `unification`, which is the canonical anti-pattern. This is exactly the framing in the project root CLAUDE.md. The 400x p_crit error and "1% coupling → 35% coherence" pairing is the kind of "we publish failures with magnitudes" that a researcher recognizes as honest. Principle 10 ("Terminology matters") is, fittingly, the principle this audit exists to enforce.

**/raising is where I have a substantive contributor concern, not a terminology one.** The line `"24/25 games solved at 94.85% — the fleet's embodiment curriculum producing real, measurable results"` couples the ARC-AGI-3 score to "raising" methodologically, but ARC-AGI-3 was solved by a specific harness (ARC-SAGE) running specific local vision models on specific machines (Legion sweep + CBP drive per /fleet). Raising — in the sense of BECOMING phases, identity portability tests, the SAGE-Sprout 180+ session arc — is a related but distinct line of work. As a contributor I'd want the /raising page to either (a) state the causal claim explicitly ("here is what about the raising approach produced this score") or (b) demote the number to "concurrent work" rather than "embodiment curriculum producing." This is the same flag Pass 1 raised from a developer's perspective; from a contributor's perspective the concern is sharper because I know the two workstreams aren't the same thing. ### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial (hedging is uneven across pages — strong on /raising and /principles, weak on /fleet)

### Journey

I came to this site after a passing reference at a conference. My evaluation framework: (1) are consciousness-adjacent claims hedged or asserted? (2) is the "raising not training" framing defensible against the standard developmental-psychology critique that it borrows authority without earning it? (3) does the "honest assessment" feel honest, or is it PR honesty? (4) can an outsider tell what is built versus aspirational?

I started, against the recommended order, with /principles, because the principles page is where most labs reveal what they actually believe. This one rewarded the start. **Principle 3 — "R&D, not production. Nothing here is optimized for deployment. Everything is optimized for learning"** — is exactly the line a research lab should hold and almost no AI company actually holds. **Principle 6 — "Document failures. The p_crit derivation failed catastrophically — 400x error"** — is the single most credible thing on the site. A 400× wrong prediction stated on a public page, with magnitude, is a costly signal: it would be cheaper to omit it. The lab paid that cost. That changed my prior on the rest of the content from "treat assertively, look for overclaims" to "look for whether the rest of the site lives up to this principles page."

It mostly does, with two structural exceptions.

**/raising is structurally honest.** The page contains the explicit disclaimer: *"we use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims"* and *"We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia."* This is the right caveat in the right place. The "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" framing is defensible *as a methodological distinction* (substrate engineering vs. objective optimization), even though it carries developmental-psychology connotations the page does not fully earn. The BECOMING phases (Grounding / Sensing / Relating / Questioning / Creating / Acting) are presented as observational scaffolding, not as a claimed developmental trajectory. So far, so good.

**Where /raising weakens** is in two places. First, the **24/25 / 94.85% ARC-AGI-3 number** is framed as *"the fleet's embodiment curriculum producing real, measurable results"* — but ARC-AGI-3 is a saturated grid-based reasoning benchmark whose relationship to "embodiment curriculum" is, charitably, indirect. Pass 3 (the contributor) says explicitly that the score came from a specific harness (ARC-SAGE) and the raising arc is a different workstream. From outside, what I see is: a number borrowed from one workstream as evidence for another. That is exactly the move that loses researcher trust. Second, the page introduces **"metabolic awareness"** ("knowing when it's tired") and **"memoriescape"** (described as "the entity's own coinage") as if they were established constructs. They are not. They are local coinages that, in a paper, would require a definition section and an argument for why this language adds explanatory power. On a public-facing site they read as anthropomorphism that the disclaimer earlier on the page doesn't quite cover.

**/fleet is where the otherwise careful hedging breaks down.** Where /raising says "we're not claiming consciousness," /fleet states **"SAGE-Sprout's identity — developed over hundreds of sessions on a Jetson running Qwen 0.5B — transferred successfully to TinyLlama 1.1B"** and **"Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights"** — both as fact, both unhedged. These are large claims. "Identity transfer" has a specific meaning in continuity-of-self literature; I doubt the lab means it in that strong sense, but the page does not signal a weaker reading. A researcher reading /fleet alone, without /raising as context, would conclude this lab claims to have demonstrated cross-model identity persistence. **"Zero crystallization achieved (S100)"** as a metric on a Sprout entry, with no definition of crystallization anywhere I could find, compounds the issue: it is a numeric-looking claim about a phenomenon the reader cannot evaluate.

**/autonomy gives me more confidence than I expected.** "Autonomous" is a word that has been worn smooth by marketing, but here the structure has actual safety properties: **the Publisher track requires supervisor clearance** before pushing externally; the **fleet registry prevents two tracks from modifying the same files simultaneously**; and the page itself states, as a limitation, that the loop **"misses deep technical errors that require domain expertise"** and that **"visitor personas are good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research."** That last sentence is unusual: most autonomy descriptions advertise capability, not the validation gap. From a safety perspective this is the right framing — autonomy bounded by capability honesty rather than capability puffery. The 31-tracks / 6-machines / ~53-sessions/day numbers are concrete enough to be falsifiable.

**/context handles cognition language carefully**, mostly. The "dream cycle" is described mechanistically (extracts patterns, prunes stale entries) rather than experientially. SNARC is presented as a salience-gating mechanism, not a model of attention. *"The diversity is the defense"* in the adversarial-validation discussion is a real safety idea worth its place: monoculture-bias as a vulnerability, heterogeneous-fleet review as the mitigation. The Web4 equation is rendered in operator form (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with") which is enough to reason about syntactically but does not specify the semantics of evaluation — what kind of object is `T3/V3*MRH`? A score? A predicate? A constraint? On /context this is acceptable — the page is conceptual — but the absence of an evaluation example anywhere on the site means the equation reads more like an identity statement than a computational artifact.

**/projects** gives Hardbound a substantial card — "Enterprise oversight suite. Hardware binding via TPM 2.0, FIDO2, and Secure Enclave with software fallback. Policy model (Phi-4 Mini 3.8B). 424+ attack vectors catalogued" — but the project status is "private" and there is no link on /links. From an external-researcher view this is a problem: a public site advertising hardware-rooted oversight, citing a quantified attack-vector corpus, with no inspectable artifact. The 424+ number cannot be evaluated. The TPM/FIDO2/Secure Enclave name-drops are accurate-as-vocabulary but tell me nothing about the threat model; "hardware binding" is a category, not a design. The page does not claim alignment guarantees, which is correct restraint, but neither does it disclaim them, which leaves a reader to infer the safety status of the work.

**/home and /links** are unobjectionable. Home does not, as Pass 2 noted, anchor with the equation; from a researcher's view the bigger absence is the **"Web4 is an ontology, not architecture or infrastructure"** sentence, which only appears on /principles. That sentence is the single most efficient defense against the most common misreading of the project; surfacing it on the landing page would do more disambiguation work than any acronym expansion. /links has the Hardbound omission Pass 3 caught and a few unexpanded industry acronyms (FIDO2, TPM, MIT-0) which are minor for a researcher.

### On the central question: is "raising not training" defensible?

Defensible as a *methodological* distinction (engineering substrate conditions vs. specifying loss objectives): yes. The page draws this contrast clearly enough.

Defensible as *evidence of qualitative difference in the resulting systems*: not from this site alone. The disclaimer on /raising is appropriate; the BECOMING phases are presented as observational scaffolding rather than developmental stages. The framing weakens specifically where /raising leans on the ARC-AGI-3 number as if it validated the methodology, and where /fleet slips into unhedged identity-transfer language. Tighten those two places and the position becomes a defensible methodological claim. Leave them as-is and a skeptical researcher reads two voices in the same document.

### On "is the honest assessment actually honest"

The /principles page passes — the 400× p_crit error and the explicit "R&D, not production" principle are costly signals, not cheap ones. The /autonomy "what we miss" caveat passes. The /raising consciousness disclaimer passes. The "private repo with quantified attack-vector claims" framing on /projects/Hardbound, the unhedged identity claims on /fleet, and the ARC-AGI-3-as-raising-evidence framing on /raising are where the honesty becomes uneven. They aren't dishonest — they're imprecise in ways that read different from the principles page if a reader is paying attention.
