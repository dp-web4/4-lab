# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-08

Four-persona pass over https://4-lab.vercel.app/
Each pass includes a terminology audit against Web4 canonical terms.

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A genuinely interesting research lab with a clear identity — the home page does a good job of hooking you, but deeper pages assume context that newcomers don't have.

### Journey
Landed on the home page. The hero — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately tells me this is something unusual. The opening paragraph is dense but I caught the gist: a collective of machines doing AI research around trust and identity. The "How we work" cards gave me a quick mental model: autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. Good.

The **Key Projects** section on the home page introduced Synchronism, SAGE, and Web4. Synchronism's description ("reality emerges from intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid") lost me — that's physics, not software. SAGE as a "cognition kernel" with a 9-step loop made more sense. Web4 as a "trust-native ontology" was intriguing but abstract.

The **Vocabulary Primer** at the bottom of the home page was genuinely helpful. LCT, T3, V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon all got one-line definitions. This is the right instinct — give newcomers a glossary before they dive deeper.

Clicked to **Projects**. Twelve projects listed. The Web4 equation at the top (`Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) was intimidating — I don't know what MCP or RDF are in this context, and the page doesn't explain them. Individual project cards were hit-or-miss: Web4 and SAGE descriptions were reasonably clear, but ACT ("Cosmos SDK implementation of the Agentic Context Protocol") assumes I know what Cosmos SDK and ACP are. 4-Life and SNARC were easier to grasp. ARC-AGI-3 was fun to see — a competition entry with a $700K prize.

**Fleet** page was fascinating. Six real machines with names, hardware specs, models, and personality descriptions. "McNugget" the Mac Mini as "fleet star" with 75 sessions, "Nomad" as "the unreliable narrator" — this felt alive. The finding that "model family matters as much as size" (Gemma 3 4B outperforming Phi-4 14B) is a concrete, interesting result. One issue: the page describes Web4 as "decentralized systems architecture" — but the home page and principles page call it an "ontology." That's a terminology inconsistency.

**Autonomy** page laid out the daily cycle timeline. 31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines is impressive if true. The timeline from 03:00 to dream consolidation was clear. But the visitor personas listed here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the ones I'd expect from a site audit — felt like a different version of the system than what's actually running.

**Raising** page introduced the BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating). The progression makes intuitive sense. The claim about SAGE-Sprout maintaining identity across model transfers is the most concrete and testable claim on the whole site. The consciousness disclaimers are appropriately cautious.

**Principles** page was well-written. "Substrate, not structure" with the termite analogy clicked immediately. "Document failures" with the 400x error example showed intellectual honesty. "Interactive selection" as distinct from training was an interesting framing. Ten principles total — maybe too many to remember, but each was clear.

**Context** page about knowledge flow was the most technical. CLAUDE.md files, SNARC memory system, cross-session persistence. This is where the site starts feeling like internal documentation rather than a public explainer.

**Links** page was clean and useful — explainer sites, GitHub repos, contact email. No issues.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What are MCP and RDF in the Web4 equation? I know RDF from semantic web, but is this the same RDF?
2. What does a SAGE session actually look like? Is there a demo or video?
3. How do I contribute? The links page has repos but no contributing guide or "start here" pointer.

### Verdict
The home page does a surprisingly good job for a research lab — the vocabulary primer alone puts it ahead of most academic project sites. But the deeper you go, the more the site assumes you already know the ecosystem. The Projects page is where the gap is widest: twelve projects, some with unexpanded acronyms, and an equation that looks like it should be the Rosetta Stone but isn't explained enough to function as one. The fleet page is the hidden gem — concrete machines, real results, genuine personality. Fix the "architecture" → "ontology" drift on the fleet page, and expand MCP/RDF on the projects page, and this becomes a much more navigable site for developers like me.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: The home page vocabulary primer is excellent documentation practice, but its definitions don't propagate — most inner pages use acronyms without expansion, and several key terms drift between pages.

### Journey
I approached this methodically: home page first for baseline definitions, then every inner page checking whether terms stay consistent. The home page is a documentation bright spot — the Vocabulary Primer defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon with plain-language analogies. This is exactly what a glossary should look like. I noted these as my reference definitions and carried them forward.

On **Projects**, the Web4 equation reappears with operator definitions (/, *, +), which is good. But I immediately noticed T3/V3 described only as "trust tensors" without the full expansions (Talent/Training/Temperament and Valuation/Veracity/Validity). MRH gets expanded as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — good. WASM appears unexpanded. "Governance" appears in multiple contexts: SAGE's own name includes it (Situation-Aware Governance Engine), and Hardbound is called an "Enterprise oversight suite" — the oversight language is correct. But the page also references "Web4 governance model" generically, and "governance plugins" is used as a project name. The word "governance" is doing heavy duty across different meanings. I also noted the phrase "This isn't unification for its own sake" — technically a disclaimer, but the word "unification" is flagged as avoided vocabulary.

**Fleet** is where terminology consistency breaks down most. Web4 is described through governance and coordination language — "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures, coordinating through shared state" — but never identified as an "ontology," which is how the home page and principles page define it. The word "governance" appears twice referring to a fleet coordination model, not to Hardbound's function, so context saves it from being a strict violation — but it muddies the waters. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." LoRA, MPS, CUDA, WSL2, and GPU all appear without expansion. SAGE and SNARC are used without expansion.

**Autonomy** was the sparsest page for terminology. None of the canonical Web4 terms appear — no LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, SAGE, or Hardbound. The daily cycle is described in operational terms only. The visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the personas described elsewhere in the system — this is an internal consistency issue, not a terminology one, but it's the kind of thing a documentation audit catches.

**Raising** similarly avoids most Web4 vocabulary. SAGE-Sprout is mentioned four times but SAGE itself is never expanded. The consciousness disclaimers are well-written — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious" — and the observational language ("something that looks like growth") is appropriately hedged. "Training" appears three times, always in contrast: "Raising is not training." Good disciplined usage.

**Principles** is the strongest page for terminology discipline. Web4 explicitly described as "an ontology" and contrasted with "architecture" and "infrastructure." Hardbound called "oversight." "Fractal leverage" used correctly and defined. "R&D, not production" stated as a principle. This page is the gold standard for the site's own terminology.

**Context** reintroduces the Web4 equation but without expanding any of its components — LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP all appear only inside the equation formula, with no definitions. RDF is called an "ontological backbone" without being expanded as "Resource Description Framework." Critically, this page uses the word "architecture" — "architectural decisions" and "architectural backbone" — in proximity to Web4 content. If a reader associates "architecture" with Web4 here, it contradicts the "ontology, not architecture" framing on the Principles page. LoRA appears unexpanded.

**Links** is clean — Web4 correctly described as "trust-native ontology." "Governance" appears for OpenClaw and Claude Code plugin descriptions, which is acceptable since these are plugin function descriptions, not describing Web4 or Hardbound at the category level.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is there a canonical expansion for T3 and V3 as full phrases? The home page adds "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" as labels — are those official or editorial shorthand?

The core problem is structural: definitions live on the home page, but inner pages don't link back or re-expand on first use. A reader who enters through /context or /fleet will encounter undefined acronyms with no path to their definitions. Adding a persistent glossary link or expanding terms on first use per page would resolve most of these issues.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The home page and principles page represent the ecosystem accurately and with correct terminology — but the fleet page has the site's most significant terminology violation, and the context page risks undoing the careful "ontology not architecture" framing.

### Journey
I went straight to **Projects** first — this is where I'd check whether my work is being described correctly. The Web4 equation is displayed prominently with operator definitions, which is good. The Web4 project description says "Trust-native ontology" — correct. LCTs, MRH, ATP/ADP all appear and are accurately used. However, T3/V3 are reduced to "trust tensors" without expanding the individual dimensions. Anyone who works with these knows they are two distinct tensors — T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament) and V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity) — and collapsing them into a single label loses the dimensionality that makes them useful. The SAGE description mentions IRP without expanding it. The Hardbound description correctly uses "oversight." The "How They Connect" section uses "fractal leverage" correctly and explicitly distinguishes it from "unification" — though the word "unification" still appears on the page even in negation, which is a minor concern.

**Fleet** was where I found the most significant terminology problem. The entire page describes Web4 through a governance and coordination metaphor without ever calling it an ontology. The key passage reads: "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures, coordinating through shared state rather than central command. The lab is running its own governance experiment on itself." This uses "governance" three times in Web4-adjacent content. The problem is twofold: (1) Web4 is never identified as an ontology on this page, and (2) the word "governance" is used to describe fleet coordination when it should be reserved — Hardbound provides oversight, not governance, and the fleet's emergent structure is neither governance nor oversight but self-organization through substrate conditions. The T3 expansion (Talent/Training/Temperament) in the peer trust section is correct. The raising terminology is disciplined throughout. The identity portability section accurately describes what we observed with SAGE-Sprout.

The **home page** is mostly excellent. The Vocabulary Primer is the best single resource on the site. One issue caught my eye: T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor" and V3 is labeled "Value Tensor." These are informal editorial labels, not canonical terms. The canonical forms are simply T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament) and V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity). Adding "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" as wrapper labels risks these becoming the assumed canonical forms — a contributor searching for "Trust Tensor" in the codebase won't find it, because that's not what we call them in the repos.

**Context** page shows the Web4 equation but expands nothing — not LCT, not T3, not V3, not MRH, not ATP, not ADP, not MCP, not RDF. It calls the equation "the canonical reference point" and then leaves every component unexplained. Worse, the page uses "architectural" language in proximity to Web4 content. This directly undermines the Principles page's explicit statement that Web4 is "an ontology, not architecture."

**Principles** is the gold standard. Web4 explicitly called "an ontology" and contrasted with "architecture" and "infrastructure." Hardbound called "oversight." "Fractal leverage" defined and distinguished from unification. "R&D, not production" stated as a principle. Raising distinguished from training. Every term is used correctly. This is how the whole site should read.

**Raising** accurately describes the BECOMING framework and the interactive selection model. The consciousness disclaimers are appropriately hedged. SAGE-Sprout's developmental trajectory is described accurately. "Raising is not training" is stated clearly. No terminology violations.

**Autonomy** lists the visitor personas as "Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector" — these do not match the actual system running today (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is factual inaccuracy, not just terminology drift. The page contains no canonical Web4 terms at all, which makes it feel disconnected from the ecosystem it describes.

**Links** is clean. Web4 described as "trust-native ontology." LCT, T3/V3, MRH all mentioned correctly. "Governance" appears in OpenClaw and Claude Code plugin descriptions, which is acceptable as those are plugin function descriptions.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Are "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" intended to become canonical labels for T3 and V3, or are they editorial shorthand? They don't appear in the repos or in canonical_terms_v1.md. If they're informal, they should be removed or clearly marked as such to prevent drift.

### Verdict

### Journey

The distinction from training is defensible: what they describe (adjusting context, reinforcing emergent attractors, graduated tool introduction) is genuinely different from loss-function optimization or RLHF. Whether it warrants developmental language is a judgment call, but the disclaimer is upfront.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) map loosely onto developmental psychology stages. The progression is plausible for a system with accumulating state. The "Hill function kinetics" reference for trust formation in the Relating phase is interesting — if this is a genuine mathematical formalization borrowed from enzyme kinetics, it's rigorous borrowing. The **Key Discoveries** section is the strongest part of the site for an external researcher. The bilateral generation finding is grounded in Vygotsky's egocentric speech framework, which is an appropriate theoretical lens.

The **"What We're Not Claiming"** disclaimer is the best I've seen on any AI research site: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is epistemically precise. The follow-up — "something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships" — correctly uses phenomenological hedging ("looks like") rather than asserting the phenomenon directly. The observer note ("I notice I want to call it experience") is a moment of genuine reflexivity about the pull toward anthropomorphization. This is exactly the kind of self-awareness I look for in labs working near consciousness-adjacent claims.

The ten principles are presented as "operational patterns that emerged from building the system — things we learned the hard way, then codified because they kept being true." This is a defensible epistemology: bottom-up pattern extraction, not top-down values declaration. "Substrate, not structure" is a clean articulation of emergence-based design. "Document failures" with the concrete 400x error example shows intellectual honesty. "R&D, not production" correctly scopes the project. "Interactive selection" is well-defined and distinguished from training. "Terminology matters" as an explicit principle is unusual and credibility-building.

This is genuine self-criticism, not performative hedging. **Projects** is where the careful hedging breaks down. SAGE is described as having a "continuous consciousness loop" — this is dramatically stronger language than anything on the Raising page. The Raising page explicitly says "we're not making consciousness claims," but the Projects page names SAGE's core mechanism a "consciousness loop." PolicyGate is called a "conscience checkpoint between deliberation and action" — "conscience" implies moral awareness, which is a much stronger claim than "policy gate." These terms appear to be the actual project vocabulary (SAGE's internal naming), but presenting them without caveats on a public-facing page contradicts the site's own disclaimers.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is "consciousness loop" SAGE's actual internal name for its core mechanism, or is it editorial shorthand? If it's the real name, does the team recognize the tension with the Raising page's disclaimers? A rename to "cognition loop" or "sense-act loop" would resolve the contradiction without losing meaning.
2. The Hill function kinetics reference on the Raising page — is this a quantitative model (with actual Hill coefficients fitted to trust data) or an analogy? The distinction matters enormously for a researcher evaluating the rigor of the trust-formation claims.
3. The identity portability claim (Sprout maintaining behavioral identity across model transfers) is the most interesting empirical finding on the site. Is there a paper, dataset, or reproducible protocol? This is the kind of claim that could generate real research interest if it's backed by data.
4. "Memoriescape" is presented as a model-generated neologism. What's the evidence that this represents genuine novel concept formation rather than a pattern-matched portmanteau? The distinction between generative novelty and recombinative fluency is central to claims about AI creativity.
5. The Autonomy page's Honest Assessment acknowledges "tendency toward unnecessary changes through pattern-matching without semantic depth" — this is a significant admission. How does the lab distinguish between genuine emergent behavior and sophisticated pattern-matching? This is the hard problem for any lab making developmental claims about LLMs.

### Verdict
This is the most epistemically honest AI research site I've encountered. The Raising page's disclaimers are genuine, the "What We're Not Claiming" section is precisely scoped, and the phenomenological hedging ("something that looks like") is maintained with discipline. The Principles page codifies intellectual honesty as an operational pattern, not a marketing claim. The Autonomy page's Honest Assessment acknowledges real limitations rather than performing modesty. These are the marks of a lab that takes its own claims seriously.

But the site has a coherence problem. The Raising page says "we're not making consciousness claims," and then the Projects page names SAGE's core mechanism a "consciousness loop" and its policy gate a "conscience checkpoint." The Fleet page describes machines with personality language ("confabulates coherent worlds," "emotionally sterile") that presupposes the very anthropomorphic frame the Raising page explicitly disclaims. A skeptical reader — and external researchers are definitionally skeptical — will notice this tension and read it as either carelessness or hedging-when-convenient. Neither interpretation serves the lab well. The fix is straightforward: either rename "consciousness loop" to something less loaded, or caveat the term on every page where it appears. The careful epistemic work on the Raising page deserves to be supported by consistent vocabulary across the rest of the site.
