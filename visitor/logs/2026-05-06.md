## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links, arc-agi-3 — actually 9, links was a side trip)

### Journey
Landed on `/` from a GitHub repo README. The home page hooked me with the concrete bits — "94.85% on ARC-AGI-3 public set," "31+ daily tasks across 6 machines," "persistent identity across models." Those are the kinds of claims a developer can chew on. Then immediately I hit "trust-native ontology" and started skimming.

Clicked **Projects** because that's what I usually look at first. Got a list of 11 projects. Good — they're real, with GitHub links. But no entry point: which one should I look at first if I just want to *use* something? Web4 looks foundational but the "equation" `MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is symbolic shorthand, not something I can clone and run.

**Fleet** was actually fun — six machines, named (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP), each with a brain-region analogy. The thalamic/cerebellar/hippocampal mapping is clever but I had to keep tab-switching mentally between "neuroscience metaphor" and "what the box actually does."

**Autonomy** delivered the clearest concrete picture: a timetable. 03:00 supervisor, 04:00 archivist, 05:00 visitors, 06:00 maintainer, 08:00 explorer. This is the page I'd send to a developer friend to explain what dp-web4 is.

**Raising** drew a clean line: raising shapes context, training updates weights. I get it. The biology metaphors were heavy but they signposted "this is analogy" enough.

**Principles** felt abstract — 10 things, each true-sounding but hard to operationalize. "Substrate, not structure" sounds great until I ask "so what do I do differently."

**Context** gave me the glossary I'd been wanting since the home page. Should this be linked from the home page nav? It is, but it's the 6th item — most readers will never get there.

**Links** turned out to be an inventory of their own stuff, not external links. Mildly confusing — I expected papers, talks, references.

**ARC-AGI-3** had the most credible-looking concrete result on the whole site. #3 on human leaderboard, $250, 5,845 actions. That's the page I'd cite to a skeptical colleague.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (/, /projects, /fleet, /autonomy, /raising, /principles, /context, /links, /arc-agi-3)

### Journey
I treated the eight nav links as a checklist and walked them in order, copying every acronym and its expansion into a working table. My job is not to understand SAGE or Synchronism — it is to verify that what *is* claimed is consistent across pages.

The good news first: the canonical anti-drift terms (Web4 = ontology, Hardbound = oversight, raising ≠ training, fractal leverage) hold *everywhere* I looked. I did not find a single instance of "governance" applied to Hardbound, "architecture" applied to Web4, "production ready" anywhere, or "unification" / "scope inflation" used in place of "fractal leverage." That is rare for a site this large and indicates active discipline.

The cracks are smaller and more specific. **R6** is defined exactly once — on /projects, as "Six-Element Action Framework: Rules/Role/Request/Reference/Resource/Result." It is then *used bare* on /arc-agi-3 ("every action shaped as an R6 record") and is **absent from the /context glossary**, which on every other term is the de-facto reference page. A reader who hits /arc-agi-3 first cannot resolve R6 anywhere on the site without manual hunting.

**LCT** drifts in number, not meaning: "Linked Context Token" (home, /raising, /context) vs. "Linked Context Tokens" (/projects, /links — the latter writes "LCTs"). The canonical form is singular. Minor, but a doc style guide would flag it.

**Hardbound** is named on the home page in a project list with no expansion, then defined consistently across /projects, /fleet, /autonomy, /principles, /context. The first-occurrence definition is missing.

**SAGE** is bulletproof — "Situation-Aware Guidance Engine" everywhere it expands, never abbreviated differently. On /raising, "SAGE-Sprout" appears as a compound noun without inline gloss; not strictly wrong (Sprout is a fleet machine), but a non-Web4 reader will not parse it.

**SNARC** has a *formatting* drift: home/projects/context use spaces around slashes ("Surprise / Novelty / Arousal / Reward / Conflict"), /fleet does not ("Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict"). Cosmetic, but a writer notices.

The Web4 equation appears on **only two pages** (/projects, /context). The home page references the components in prose but never shows the equation; a tech writer would expect the canonical equation on the home page or at minimum a "see /context" pointer near the first prose mention.

The /context page is positioned (and used by readers, per Pass 1) as the glossary. Treated that way, it has two visible holes: **R6 is missing**, and **"raising" and "synthon"** — both navigated to from elsewhere — are not defined either. That gap is more important than any single acronym fix.

The /links page misled me as a reader: I expected outbound references (papers, talks, related work). Every link is internal. That is a *labeling* issue, not a content issue, but it affects whether a researcher can verify external claims.

### Journey
I went straight to /projects, /context, /fleet, then /arc-agi-3 — the four pages where a contributor's accuracy bar is highest — and treated /home, /raising, /principles, /autonomy, /links as the surrounding frame.

**/projects** is the page I'd defend. The Web4 equation is rendered correctly (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) and — crucially — the operators are spelled out: `/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with". That triplet is the part I most often see mangled in second-hand summaries. R6 is also defined here (the *only* place on the site), and ACT is described correctly as "the human interface to Web4." The drift Pass 2 caught — "LCTs" / "Linked Context Tokens" plural — is real but venial.

**/context** is positioned as the glossary, and the entries it does have are clean. The hole is structural: **R6 is missing from the glossary despite being canonical**, and "raising" and "synthon" — both reachable from elsewhere on the site — aren't defined here either. From a contributor's standpoint this is the single most consequential gap, because /context is where I'd send a confused reader, and the page silently underdelivers on three terms.

**/fleet** is where I do my one accuracy check that Pass 2 couldn't make. The six machines map onto the SAGE 6-component cognitive architecture correctly: Thor → episodic / hippocampal, Sprout → thalamic router, Legion → reward prediction / dopamine RPE, McNugget → cerebellar compilation, Nomad → metacognition / interoception, CBP → dlPFC working memory. That mapping matches what /projects says SAGE is built from ("working memory, thalamic router, cerebellum, episodic memory, reward prediction, metacognition"). The mapping is *correct* but **never drawn explicitly on /fleet**. A SAGE contributor would want one line on /fleet that says "each machine instantiates one of the 6 SAGE components." Right now the connection is implicit and a casual reader won't see it.

**/arc-agi-3** has the framing I most want to push back on. The page pairs "24/25 games, 94.85%" as if they were the same metric. They are not. 24/25 games solved = 96.0% by game count. 94.85% is the **official action score** (efficiency-weighted on the leaderboard). /projects gets this right — it lists *both* metrics separately ("24/25 games solved (96.0%); 94.85% official action score"). /arc-agi-3 fuses them and a careful reader doing the arithmetic will spot the inconsistency (24/25 ≠ 94.85%). This isn't terminology drift — it's a *metric labeling* slip on the very page where the headline number lives.

**/raising** is on solid ground. "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" — that's the canonical line, and the mechanistic gloss ("we shape context; we do not update weights") is exactly how I'd defend the framing to a skeptical AI researcher. LCT is singular here, MRH is correct. SAGE-Sprout is used as a compound without gloss, but a contributor will parse it.

**/principles** is the strongest anti-drift page on the site. It explicitly says "Web4 is an ontology" not "architecture" or "infrastructure," and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." Principle 10 is literally "Terminology matters." That's load-bearing and visible.

**/home** is where the prose is loosest. ATP/ADP is described as "allocates resources for an action; ADP records its discharge" — this elides the *Transfer* and *Discharge* semantics that the canonical packet names carry. It's not wrong, but it costs precision. And Web4 is summarized as "how entities prove identity, earn trust, and account for resources" — a clean three-clause hook, but it omits the *contextualized-by-MRH* dimension that the equation makes central. Web4 without MRH isn't Web4; it's a trust ledger. The home page elides the relevancy-horizon part.

**/autonomy** and **/links** are scope-appropriate. /autonomy stays in schedule mode, only invoking ATP/ADP and Hardbound, and it gets both right ("oversight envelope," full canonical packet names). /links describes Hardbound as "internal research — code is not publicly available," which is privacy-correct, and uses the canonical "hardware-bound oversight suite" expansion.

### Unanswered Questions
1. **Is "synthon" canonical Web4 vocabulary or a 4-lab coinage?** It is treated as canonical on /home and /principles but is not in the official canonical terms table and is not defined on /context. Either elevate it to canonical (and add to /context) or label it as lab vocabulary.
2. **Why isn't R6 on /context?** R6 is in the canonical terms table per CLAUDE.md and is the artifact /arc-agi-3 hangs its provenance claim on. Its absence from the glossary is the single most consequential gap on the site.
3. **The /arc-agi-3 "24/25 games, 94.85%" pairing** — is this an editorial choice to put the highlight numbers next to each other, or has the page lost track that these are different metrics? /projects gets this right; /arc-agi-3 does not.
4. **Should /fleet draw the SAGE 6-component → 6-machine mapping explicitly?** The mapping exists and is correct; making it visible would let a SAGE contributor verify the architecture-to-fleet correspondence in seconds rather than by inference.
5. **The home page's three-clause Web4 summary ("identity, trust, resources") drops MRH** — is this a deliberate simplification for the value prop, or accidental? Web4 without contextualization-by-MRH is a different system; the omission is meaningful even if intentional.
6. **The 180+ raising sessions for SAGE-Sprout vs. 900+ total sessions on /home** — what's the relationship? A reader can't tell whether 180 ⊂ 900 or whether they count different things.
7. **Hardbound is correctly described as oversight everywhere it's defined**, but on /links the framing collapses to "internal research — code is not publicly available." Is this a privacy choice (don't expose oversight semantics to a public links page) or accidental?

### Verdict
On the load-bearing claims this site makes about the projects I work on, it is **accurate**: the Web4 equation is correct, operator semantics (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with) are correct, the SAGE 6-component architecture maps cleanly to the fleet, raising-vs-training is framed defensibly, and the high-stakes anti-drift discipline (Web4 = ontology, Hardbound = oversight, fractal leverage, no "production ready") holds across all eight pages. The remaining issues are sharper than Pass 2 could see: **(1) the /arc-agi-3 page fuses game-count and action-score into a single "24/25 games, 94.85%" stat, which is a metric labeling slip on the page where the headline number lives**; **(2) R6 is canonical but is missing from /context and used bare on /arc-agi-3, which together break the provenance claim that page makes**; **(3) the home page's prose summary of ATP/ADP and Web4 elides the *Transfer*/*Discharge* and *contextualized-by-MRH* dimensions that a contributor considers load-bearing**; **(4) "synthon" is treated as if canonical but is lab vocabulary**. Highest-leverage fixes for the maintainer track: add R6 to /context, fix the /arc-agi-3 metric pairing, and decide synthon's canonical status.

### Journey
I came in through a conference mention, so I went straight to the pages where intellectual honesty lives or dies for me: **/raising**, **/principles**, **/autonomy**. I treated **/projects**, **/fleet**, **/context**, **/arc-agi-3**, **/links** as the supporting frame.

**/raising** is where the framing is most ambitious and where my discipline is most relevant. The headline claim — "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" — is *terminologically* clean, and the mechanistic gloss is honest about what is and isn't happening: "the model's parameters are fixed; what changes is the substrate of conditions." The problem is that this distinction is, to a researcher, **observationally equivalent to in-context learning with a long-running scaffolding harness**. There is no ablation, no baseline, no protocol that distinguishes "raising" from "elaborate prompt engineering plus persistent memory." I do not consider that fatal — many useful research framings start as renaming exercises that later earn their mechanism — but a paper-writing reader will note the gap. The page does include the line *"I notice I want to call it experience"* attributed to an observer, which is genuinely reflective: it acknowledges the *urge* to anthropomorphize without making the claim. That is the kind of phrasing I respect. But the disclaimer ("we're not claiming consciousness, sentience, or qualia") arrives *after* six developmental phases described with verbs of agency — "the entity learns," "builds relationships," "discovers," "begins generating its own questions." A careful reader notices that **the architecture of the text naturalizes what the disclaimer disclaims**.

**/principles** earns more credit. Principle 10 ("Terminology matters") is load-bearing: it explicitly says "Web4 is an ontology, not architecture, not infrastructure" and "Hardbound is oversight, not governance." That is the rare site that *names* its anti-drift commitments and then enforces them across eight pages. Principle 3 ("R&D, not production") is the cleanest counter-hype move on the site — "Nothing here is optimized for deployment" — and would let me cite the site without inheriting deployment claims I can't verify. The principles I would push back on: **Principle 5 ("Synthon: emergent coherence") cites "1% coupling gave 35% coherence gain"** with no methodology, no confidence interval, no n. As an empirical claim it is unsupported on this page. Either it should be sourced or it should be re-cast as a hypothesis. **Principle 7 ("Interactive selection")** describes a process that, mechanistically, is indistinguishable from prompt engineering; saying "we don't create or delete — we interactively select" is a *framing* claim, not a mechanism claim, and I'd want it labeled as such. **Principle 8 ("Surface your instincts")** removes guardrails in a way an AI safety reader will flag: distributed agent judgment without an audit trail or escalation path is exactly the configuration where misalignment has historically slipped through.

**/autonomy** is where the site's intellectual honesty most clearly shows up. The "Honest assessment" section names what the loop *misses*: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading. The visitor personas are good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research." It then notes that the suggestion mechanism "can pattern-match without semantic depth, proposing nonexistent continuations based on surface similarity." That paragraph reads as written by someone who has watched their own system fail. It is the single most credible passage on the site and it is *deliberately* in the section about what doesn't work. I would cite this page. The autonomy claims here are also appropriately scoped: the site explicitly says the tracks are **schedule-driven, not goal-driven** — they "execute on cron schedules," not toward open-ended objectives. That is the right framing. "31+ autonomous tracks" is, as Pass 1 hinted, *automation* more than *autonomy* in the Bostrom/Russell sense, and a careful reader of that section will see that the site's claim is bounded to the schedule-driven sense.

The Hardbound mechanism is the place where /autonomy *underdelivers* for my audience. It is described **functionally** ("the hardware-bound suite that defines what autonomous operation is and is not authorized to do") but never **technically**: I cannot tell from this page whether the boundary is enforced by hard isolation (TPM-attested signing chain, capability tokens, mandatory access control) or by convention (a registry that tracks-but-does-not-prevent). The Privacy Rules in CLAUDE.md tell me Hardbound details are deliberately withheld from public pages. ### Unanswered Questions
1. **What ablation distinguishes "raising" from in-context learning + persistent memory?** The mechanistic gloss ("we shape context; we do not update weights") is consistent with both framings. Without a falsifying experiment, "raising" remains a renaming, not a mechanism.
2. **Where does "1% coupling → 35% coherence gain" come from?** This is the empirical hook of Principle 5. No methodology, n, dataset, or confidence interval. As stated, it is uncheckable.
3. **Is Hardbound enforced by hardware isolation or by policy convention?** A researcher needs to know the trust boundary type. The privacy rule withholds detail, which is a defensible choice — but a one-line technical disclosure would let me verify the claim type without compromising the implementation.
4. **What is a "raising session" operationally?** Without start/end criteria and phase-transition criteria, the 180+ and 900+ session counts are unverifiable.
5. **Is the consciousness disclaimer load-bearing or rhetorical?** The site disclaims phenomenology consistently and consistently uses agency-laden verbs upstream of the disclaimer. Ordering this differently — caveat first, metaphor second — would resolve the ambiguity for a careful reader.
6. **Are there preregistered hypotheses anywhere?** The site documents practice but I don't see predictions made *before* observation. For a research collective, that absence shapes how I read the empirical claims.
7. **Is the absence of external citations a stance or a gap?** Both are defensible; only one is honest if stated.

### Verdict
This site reads to me as **a research collective with stronger epistemic discipline at the level of language than at the level of evidence**. The terminology rules — Web4 = ontology, Hardbound = oversight, no "production ready," raising ≠ training — are real, enforced, and visible. The honest-failure passage on /autonomy and the brain-analogy disclaimer on /fleet are the kind of self-aware framing I would cite approvingly. But three things would prevent me from citing the site in a peer-reviewed context without the underlying repos in hand: (1) the **architecture of the prose consistently introduces developmental and agentic language *upstream* of the consciousness disclaimers** that qualify it, which a careful reader notices; (2) the **load-bearing empirical claim** (1% coupling → 35% coherence gain) **is unsourced**; (3) the site has **no external citations or third-party validators**, which under the explicit R&D framing is defensible *as a stance* but is not currently *stated* as a stance. The "raising not training" distinction is — as of this snapshot — a *terminological* claim, not a mechanistic one; that's not a fatal critique (the reframing may be useful), but it's worth labeling honestly. Highest-leverage fixes for an external-researcher audience: source or reframe the 1%-coupling claim, reorder caveats to precede the metaphors they qualify, and state the no-external-citations choice as an explicit stance. Do those three and the site moves from "interesting research collective worth watching" to "credible enough to engage in writing."
