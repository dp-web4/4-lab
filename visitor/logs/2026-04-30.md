## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Journey

I clicked through from a dp-web4 GitHub README expecting either a product page or a paper. What I got was something stranger: a research collective site that opens with "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." That's a great hook for me — concrete numbers, no buzzwords.

The hero tagline ("trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition, and the theoretical frameworks that connect them") immediately throws "ontology" at me. As a developer I read that as "schema-ish thing" and shrug. The "How We Work" cards (autonomous cycles / heterogeneous fleet / connected ecosystem) didn't fully land but did make me want to click Projects.

The **Vocabulary Primer** on the home page saved the visit. I went back to it three times. Without it, I would not have understood any other page. Web4 = "ontology (shared vocabulary + relationships) for how AI agents prove identity, earn trust, and account for resources" — that's the first time the page concretely told me what this thing is for. Should be higher up the page, or at minimum linked from the hero.

**Projects** was the page that actually sold me. Eleven cards, most with public repos, several with live demos. ARC-AGI-3 24/25 at 94.85% is the kind of single number that makes a developer sit up. SAGE has both a site and a repo. 4-Life is a "trust-native society prototype" with a live link — that's clickbait in the good sense. Hardbound being marked private is fine and refreshingly honest.

**Fleet** was fun. Six named machines (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP) each with a model running on it and a *neuroscience role* (hippocampal index, thalamic router, dopamine RPE, cerebellum, interoception, dlPFC working memory). I have no idea whether that mapping is metaphor or load-bearing claim, and the page doesn't say. As a developer I want a sentence: "These brain analogies describe what each machine *does*, not a claim about consciousness." If I'm being honest, the brain mapping makes me slightly suspicious of the rigor — but the hardware specs and model assignments are concrete enough that I trust the engineering side.

**Autonomy** is where things got blurry. The schedule is great (Supervisor 03:00, Archivist 04:00, Publisher 04:30, Visitor 05:00, Maintainer 06:00, Outreach 06:30, Explorer 08:00, Dream Consolidation after). But what does "Dream Consolidation" actually do? Is that a literal subsystem name or marketing? The page tells me *when* but not *how* an Explorer agent's discovery becomes the next day's Maintainer behavior. I left this page knowing the cadence, not the mechanism.

**Raising** I liked. The line "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" is sharp. The acknowledgement that "We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims" is the kind of caveat that makes me trust the rest. The BECOMING phase names (Grounding, Sensing, Relating, Questioning, Creating, Acting) are fine but I have no way to tell if they map to anything observable.

**Principles** read as genuine, not marketing. "Productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started" and "Document failures" are the right cultural signals. "Substrate, not structure" is the kind of phrase I'd want explained one layer deeper before I quoted it to anyone.

**Context** was the page where I most wanted a diagram. The text describes a knowledge-flow pipeline (work → log → archive → search) but I couldn't picture it. The honest admission that "knowledge doesn't flow backwards easily" is good — it's the kind of thing labs usually hide.

**Links** was utilitarian. Single contact email, GitHub org, project-specific sites. Fine.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)

### Journey

I worked methodically: home → projects → fleet → autonomy → raising → principles → context → links. On each page I noted (a) which canonical acronyms appeared, (b) whether they were expanded inline or on first use, (c) whether the expansion matched the canonical table, and (d) whether Web4/Hardbound framing used the canonical word ("ontology", "oversight").

The site has a clear convention: a **Vocabulary Primer** on the home page introduces seven terms (Web4, LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, MRH, SAGE, Synthon). The **Projects** page provides expansions for additional terms in context (MCP, RDF, ACP, R6). The **Context** page is the canonical home for the Web4 equation, and crucially, it *does* include the operator legend (`/ = verified by, * = contextualized by, + = augmented with`) — Pass 1 flagged this as missing, but the legend is in fact present on /context. This is one of the better-handled pieces of cross-reference work on the site.

The pages that don't use canonical acronyms at all — **Autonomy**, **Raising** — sidestep terminology questions entirely. That's defensible: those pages talk about cycles and developmental phases, not the trust/value model. But it means a reader navigating Home → Autonomy → Raising never encounters most of the canonical vocabulary, and could plausibly arrive at /context cold and be hit with the equation without scaffolding. The Vocabulary Primer mitigates this — *if* the reader visits home first.

Two structural issues stood out as a documentation specialist:

1. **The Vocabulary Primer's scope is narrower than the site's actual vocabulary.** It covers seven terms but the site uses at least eleven canonical or near-canonical acronyms (add MCP, RDF, ACP, R6). R6 is in the canonical table per the visitor brief but appears only on /projects. If the primer is meant to be the reader's onboarding glossary, it has gaps.

2. **Header capitalization is inconsistent across pages.** "Vocabulary primer" and "How we work" (sentence case) sit alongside "How Knowledge Flows" and "Autonomous Cycles" (title case) and "BECOMING: six phases" (a third style). A reader scanning navigation builds the wrong pattern-matching expectation. This is the kind of thing a style guide locks down.

A small but real cross-page inconsistency: the home hero says "Six machines. Fourteen repos." The /projects page shows 11 project cards; /links shows 11 GitHub repository URLs. The number "14" might be canonical (private repos, internal tooling), but a reader auditing for accuracy can't reconcile it from public-page evidence. Either qualify the hero ("14 active repos, 11 publicly linked") or align the count.

### Journey

I went straight to /context first because that's where the equation lives, and the equation is the load-bearing artifact. It rendered exactly as `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the operator legend present (`/` verified by, `*` contextualized by, `+` augmented with). This is the form I cite in other repos. The phrasing **"this equation appears in every project because it _is_ every project"** is the right thing to say to a contributor — it conveys that the equation is descriptive, not a stack.

Next I went to /projects. Every project I work on or near is represented, and every canonical acronym on the Web4 card is expanded correctly. The Web4 card description — "Linked Context Token (LCT), T3 (Talent / Training / Temperament) and V3 (Valuation / Veracity / Validity) tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizon (MRH), Allocation Transfer Packet (ATP) and Allocation Discharge Packet (ADP) — augmented with MCP (Model Context Protocol) transport and RDF (Resource Description Framework) representation" — is a single sentence that gets the entire ontology right, including the operator semantics (note "augmented with" matches the `+` operator on /context). This is good work.

One thing made me pause on /projects: the card opens with "Trust core: Linked Context Token (LCT), T3 ... and V3 ... tensors". As a contributor I'd push back on calling V3 part of the "trust core". T3 is the trust tensor; V3 is the value tensor. The whole point of having two separate tensors is that trust and value are not the same axis. Lumping V3 under "trust core" is the kind of soft drift that, repeated over time, lets readers conflate them. Recommend either "Trust and value core" or splitting: "Trust (T3) and value (V3) tensors over LCTs, scoped by MRH; resources via ATP/ADP".

The Hardbound card on /projects is mostly correct but contains one phrase I want flagged: **"AttestationEnvelope provides unified hardware trust."** The word "unified" sits adjacent to the avoid-list term "unification". Reading it charitably, "unified" here means "single envelope across multiple hardware roots" (TPM, FIDO2, Secure Enclave) — which is accurate. Reading it strictly, "unified hardware trust" can be heard as the kind of scope-inflating language Principle 2 explicitly distances Web4 from. Tighten to "consolidates hardware trust signals into a single envelope" or "single envelope spanning TPM/FIDO2/Secure Enclave".

Also on /projects: the **ARC-AGI-3 card claims "Six machines, 0.8B to 27B"** but the /fleet page lists models from TinyLlama 1.1B (smallest) to Qwen 2.5 14B / Phi-4 14B (largest). 0.8B and 27B don't appear in the fleet inventory. Either ARC-AGI-3 used a different model set than the daily-driver fleet (in which case say so), or the range is stale. As a contributor this is the kind of number readers will quote — it should be reconcilable with /fleet.

The **"Six machines. Fourteen repos."** hero number was already flagged by Passes 1 and 2. I'll add the contributor angle: I know there are private repos (Hardbound is one). "Fourteen" might count private+public. But the site shows 11 public repos on /links and 11 cards on /projects, and a contributor reading the public hero can't reconcile "14" without insider knowledge. Either qualify ("14 active repos, 11 publicly listed") or change the number.

/fleet was almost entirely correct. The pool framing — Synthesis Pool (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget) vs. Oversight Pool (Nomad, CBP) — matches my understanding and reinforces the oversight/governance distinction at the architecture level (Nomad and CBP are *oversight*, not governance). The neuroscience-role mappings track. The one thing I'd flag is **Sprout's model listed as "Qwen 3.5 2B"**. There's no Qwen 3.5 release I know of: Qwen 2.5 ships at 0.5B/1.5B/3B/7B/14B/32B/72B; Qwen 3 ships at 0.6B/1.7B/4B/8B/14B/32B. "Qwen 3.5 2B" doesn't match either family. This is probably either "Qwen 2.5 3B" or "Qwen 3 1.7B" mislabeled. Worth a fleet check — wrong model versions on a public-facing fleet page get cited and propagate.

### Journey

I came in from a conference mention with no prior exposure to dp-web4. My remit is to evaluate intellectual honesty, hedging, and whether terminology is well-defined enough that an outside reader can engage without being misled. I spent extra time on /raising (where consciousness-adjacent claims live), /principles (where the lab declares its epistemic stance), and /autonomy (where autonomy claims live).

The single most credibility-positive thing on the site is the **"Honest assessment"** framing on /autonomy: the loop "misses deep technical errors that require domain expertise", visitor personas are "good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research", the system can "pattern-match without semantic depth, proposing nonexistent continuations". This is the kind of self-disclosure I expect from a good empirical paper's limitations section and almost never see on a project's marketing page. Combined with /context's admission that "real-time cross-track communication is a gap" and /principles 6 ("Document failures"), the site actively models the disposition it claims to value. **This is rare and it is good.** I want to lead with this because most of what follows is critique, and the critique should be read against this baseline of unusual honesty.

That said, the credibility issues a researcher will land on:

**1. The consciousness caveat is reactive, not preventive.** /raising contains exactly the right disclaimer ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia ... We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims"). But it sits at the end. By the time the reader reaches it, they have already absorbed "metabolic awareness", "trust formation", "thinking through external dialogue", "Memoriescape", and a six-phase "BECOMING" arc framed in human developmental terms. The caveat does not undo the priming. A researcher reading skeptically will note that the page imports Vygotsky's "egocentric speech" without addressing whether developmental psychology applies across substrate — and Hill function kinetics (a biochemistry binding model) is invoked for "trust formation" without justifying the mechanistic mapping. The hedge is honest; the surrounding language is not yet honest enough to make the hedge load-bearing.

**2. The brain-analogy disclaimer doesn't propagate.** /fleet now carries the right caveat — "The 'Brain:' labels below are functional analogies to system roles — not claims about neural correspondence or computational equivalence." That is exactly the sentence a researcher needs. But the caveat lives on /fleet only. /autonomy uses "Dream Consolidation" without a similar disclaimer; /raising uses "metabolic awareness" and "interoception" without one. A reader who lands on /autonomy first encounters neuroscience-loaded language unframed.

**3. Numerical claims appear without methodology.** I counted the following quantitative claims, all unhedged: "180+ sessions" (identity persistence), "900+ raising sessions", "628+ research sessions", "424+ attack vectors catalogued", "24/25 solved at 94.85% for ~$250" (ARC-AGI-3), "1% coupling gave 35% coherence gain" (Principle 5). For each, I want to know: how was the dependent variable measured, what is n, was there inter-rater reliability, what's the baseline, where is the comparison to a non-"raised" control. None of this is on the site. The 1%→35% number in particular is the kind of headline statistic that, if surfaced in a peer review, would either anchor the paper or sink it; published with no n and no replication note, it cannot do either.

**4. ARC-AGI-3 result has no comparative context.** "24/25 solved at 94.85% for ~$250 in API cost. The games are the test; the capability is the product." There is no link to the ARC organizing body's leaderboard, no comparison to other 2025/2026 entries, no discussion of public-vs-private set, no statement of whether SAGE solved 24/25 in one shot or after iteration, no posted run logs. The `/projects` ARC-AGI-3 card cites the result; an external researcher cannot verify it from the site alone. (I note that the `gemma4-good-submission` repo on /links *might* contain the harness, but the site does not link the result to verification material.)

**5. Synchronism's "quantum to cosmic to conscious" framing is the highest-overclaim-risk item on the site.** /projects describes Synchronism as "Theoretical foundation proposing one coherence equation spanning quantum to cosmic scales" and the home page mentions "from quantum to cosmic to conscious". To an AI safety researcher, "single equation spanning quantum to cosmic" is in the same rhetorical neighborhood as physics-of-everything claims that get short shrift in serious venues. The site needs to either (a) hedge this one explicitly ("a working hypothesis we're testing, not a result"), or (b) link to the paper/preprint that does the heavy lifting — currently it does neither. The "near-publication-ready" descriptor is itself a hedge of unclear status: peer-review-pending? draft? self-assessment?

**6. There are no papers, preprints, or citation affordances on /links.** A research collective's links page is where I look for: a Google Scholar profile, a preprint list, a citation guide, a BibTeX block, a contact for academic correspondence. /links has GitHub repos, three project sites, and one email. That's appropriate for an open-source project. For something that calls itself a "research collective" and reports benchmark results (94.85%) and quantitative findings (1%→35% coupling/coherence), it is a structural omission. I cannot cite this site in a venue that expects DOIs or arXiv IDs, even if I wanted to.

**7. Some load-bearing terms aren't operationalized for outside readers.**
- **"Identity"** appears as "180+ sessions of identity persistence" and "Identity is organism. Model is weather." That is striking phrasing, but as a researcher I need to know what observable quantity persists across model swap. Is it answer style? Vocabulary fingerprint? Specific committed beliefs? The site asserts persistence without naming the persistence metric.
- **"Coherence"** drives Synchronism and shows up in the 1%→35% claim. What is being measured? Coherence in the physics sense (phase relationship)? In the linguistic sense (referential consistency)? In the AI sense (response stability)? The page conflates contexts.
- **"Trust"** appears as both a relational property (Principle 4: "a property of the relationship") and a three-axis tensor (T3). A researcher will conflate these unless told they are different objects.

**8. Terminology that risks confusing an external researcher (distinct from internal drift).** My persona has no Web4 ceiling, so I cannot judge canonical fidelity — Pass 3 owns that. But several terms will *false-friend* against AI/cogsci vocabulary:
- "Ontology" — fine in the philosophical sense (vocabulary + relationships) but our field uses "ontology" for OWL/RDF schemas. The site uses both senses (RDF *and* "ontology"); a CS reader may flatten them.
- "Synchronism" / "synchrony" — cognitive science uses "neural synchrony" for phase-locked oscillations. Synchronism here is unrelated. False friend.
- "Synthon" — chemistry term (synthetic intermediate). Reader will reach for the wrong schema unless told.
- "Raising" — invokes child rearing. Even with the right caveat, it primes consciousness inference more than is warranted; "developmental scaffolding" or "conditioned emergence" would do less work and cost less hedging.
- "Hardbound is oversight, not governance" — in AI policy/safety, "oversight" already has a contested meaning (human-in-the-loop, monitoring). The site's "oversight" appears to mean attestation + audit, which is narrower. Worth a one-line gloss.

**9. The "raising not training" framing is defensible *as a methodological distinction* but not yet *as an empirical finding*.** The methodological distinction (no scalar loss, environment + observation of emergent behavior) is real and clearly stated. The implicit claim — that this produces qualitatively different agent trajectories than RL/SFT on the same substrate — is asserted but not demonstrated against a control. For a researcher this is the single most important missing piece: a section titled "How would we know we were wrong?" or "What would falsify the raising hypothesis?" Without it, "raising" reads as a stylistic relabel rather than a testable claim.

### Unanswered Questions

1. What is the persistence metric behind "180+ sessions of identity persistence"? Without a metric, the claim is unfalsifiable.
2. What is the operational definition of "coherence" in the 1%→35% coupling-coherence claim? Same coherence as Synchronism's "single equation spanning scales"?
3. What does "near-publication-ready" mean — preprint pending, in review, in draft, or self-assessed?
4. Where can I find: methodology for the 24/25 ARC-AGI-3 result, harness code, run logs, comparison to other systems' scores on the same set?
5. Is there a baseline comparison anywhere — RL-trained or SFT-tuned agents on the same substrate, evaluated against "raised" agents on the same observable?
6. Is "Synchronism" something I would recognize from a paper, or is it a house theory? If the latter, has any of it been externally evaluated?
7. What would falsify "raising is qualitatively different from training"? What observation would the lab accept as refutation?
8. Is there a citation form (BibTeX, DOI, "to cite this work, use…") for any of this?
9. The site uses "ontology" both in the philosophical sense and in the RDF-schema sense (Web4 includes RDF). Are these intended as the same thing, or related-but-distinct?
10. Hardbound's "oversight" — is this consistent with the AI-safety usage of "oversight" (human-in-the-loop monitoring), or is it a narrower technical meaning (attestation/audit/policy-gating)?

### Verdict

This site is unusually intellectually honest for a small-collective public face: the "Honest assessment" on /autonomy, Principles 3/6/9, and the explicit consciousness caveat on /raising are the kinds of self-disclosure I usually only see in academic limitations sections. That earned a lot of credit before I started looking at hedges.

What it is not yet ready for is external citation. Three structural gaps stand between this site and a venue that would take it seriously: (a) numerical claims (180+, 900+, 1%→35%, 94.85%) need methodology — n, baseline, definition of the dependent variable; (b) Synchronism's "quantum-to-cosmic" framing and "near-publication-ready" descriptor are the highest-overclaim-risk items on the site and need either a hedge or a linked paper; (c) /links has no papers, preprints, or citation affordances, which is the structural omission an academic reader notices first.

The intermediate fix — promote the consciousness caveat above the fold on /raising, repeat the brain-analogy disclaimer on /autonomy, and add a "Methods" page that defines how each headline number is measured — would close most of the gap. The harder fix is operationalizing "identity", "coherence", and "trust" so an outside reader can see what is being measured and what would refute it. If the lab does that, the site moves from "we like this work, can we read more?" to "we want to evaluate this seriously" — which is the right next problem to have.
