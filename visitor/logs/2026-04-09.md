## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A genuinely interesting research lab with real running infrastructure, but the density of custom terminology makes the first visit feel like drinking from a firehose.

### Journey

Landed on the home page. The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately tells me this is a multi-machine AI research setup. The tagline mentions "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition" which is intriguing but dense. The three stats (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 autonomous tracks) give me scale.

Scrolled down to "How We Work" — autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. This is where I first hit the term wall: Synchronism, Web4, SAGE, Hardbound — four projects, all capitalized, all unfamiliar. The connected ecosystem blurb says "they share one equation" but doesn't show it here.

The vocabulary primer at the bottom of the home page is genuinely helpful. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP all get one-liner definitions with analogies (passport, energy tokens). Good move putting this on the landing page. SAGE gets defined as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine." I can work with that.

Clicked to **Projects**. This is the densest page. The Web4 equation appears: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The operator definitions (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by) help, but MCP and RDF are never expanded on this page — and as a developer I know RDF (Resource Description Framework) but MCP is ambiguous. Is it Model Context Protocol? The page lists 11 projects with status badges. Hardbound is marked private, ARC-AGI-3 is private. Project descriptions are concise but some assume you know the ecosystem. "IRP" appears in the SAGE description without expansion.

**Fleet** page is actually the most satisfying for a developer. Concrete hardware specs, model names, session counts, personality descriptions. "Technically compliant but emotionally sterile" for Legion made me laugh. The architecture section explains P2P topology, no central coordinator. The line "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is a striking metaphor. T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament) are mentioned — I recall the definition from the home page, so that tracks.

**Autonomy** is fascinating. 31 autonomous tracks, ~53 sessions per day, all on cron schedules. The daily timeline from 03:00 onward reads like a factory shift schedule for AI agents. The four visitor personas are listed differently here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) vs. what the CLAUDE.md describes (Developer, Tech Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher) — not sure if these are different systems or a mismatch. The "fleet track registry" in SQLite is mentioned — practical detail I appreciate.

**Raising** is where it gets philosophically heavy. "Raising is not training" is the core claim. Six developmental phases from Grounding to Acting. The SAGE-Sprout identity persistence finding is compelling: "What it *is* stayed stable. What it *says* it is didn't." The disclaimers about not claiming consciousness are appropriately placed. Terms like "memoriescape" and "bilateral generation" are new coinages that are defined in-place. Good.

**Principles** lists 11 principles (the page initially seemed like 7 based on other references). Each one has a quote and operational description. "Substrate, not structure" and "fractal leverage" are the conceptual anchors. Some principles are very clear ("Document failures"), others are more abstract ("Synthon: emergent coherence"). The Hill function keeps appearing — enzyme binding mapped to trust formation — but I'd want a link to the actual math.

**Context** explains the knowledge management challenge across 8 repos and 6 machines. SNARC (Salience-gated memory across 5 dimensions) and the CLAUDE.md pattern are described. The gaps section is honest — manual cross-machine sync, delayed backward knowledge flow. I respect a page that leads with what doesn't work yet.

**Links** is clean. Three explainer sites (Synchronism, SAGE, 4-Life) with live URLs, GitHub repos, and a contact email. Simple and useful.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer


### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Strong conceptual site with a genuine vocabulary primer, but acronym discipline breaks down badly outside the home page — most terms are introduced without expansion on the pages that use them most.

### Journey

I approached this site with one question: if I handed this to a new contributor and they clicked through every page, would they encounter the same definitions in the same form? Short answer: mostly yes on the home page, mostly no everywhere else.

**Home page** (`/`) is the best-behaved page on the site. The vocabulary primer defines LCT ("Linked Context Token"), T3 ("Talent, Training, Temperament"), V3 ("Valuation, Veracity, Validity"), ATP/ADP ("Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets"), SAGE ("Situation-Aware Governance Engine"), Web4 ("an ontology"), and Synthon. These expansions match the canonical table I'm auditing against. T3 is wrapped as "Trust Tensor" and V3 as "Value Tensor" — informal labels that add context without contradicting the canonical component names. Good editorial choice.

**Projects** (`/projects`) is where the equation lives: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The operator definitions are present (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). LCT and MRH are expanded correctly. But MCP (Model Context Protocol) is never expanded anywhere on the entire site — not here, not on home, not on context. RDF (Resource Description Framework) is similarly missing from this page. The SAGE project card introduces "IRP" as a "consciousness loop" but IRP itself is not expanded. ACP ("Agentic Context Protocol") under the ACT project is expanded — good. TPM and FIDO2 under Hardbound are not expanded but are industry-standard terms, so this is acceptable.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`) correctly expands T3 as "Talent/Training/Temperament" in the T3 scores section. SAGE is referenced but never expanded on this page. LoRA, WSL2, CUDA appear without expansion — these are industry terms a technical audience would know, so low severity. No other Web4 canonical terms appear here. The page is self-contained and clear.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`) is notably free of Web4 canonical terms — none of LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP, R6, or Hardbound appear. This makes sense for an operational page about daily cycles. However, the visitor persona names listed here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) do not match the personas described in other documentation (Developer, Tech Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is a cross-page consistency problem — a reader navigating from /autonomy would build a mental model that breaks when they encounter the actual personas elsewhere.

**Raising** (`/raising`) is philosophically rich and terminologically careful. The six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are clearly labeled. Custom coinages ("memoriescape", "bilateral generation") are defined in-place where introduced. The consciousness disavowal is explicit: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." No Web4 canonical terms appear on this page, which is appropriate — raising is about developmental methodology, not the ontology.

**Principles** (`/principles`) explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology" and "Hardbound is oversight" — both matching canonical definitions. "Fractal leverage" is used correctly as the term for cross-domain pattern reuse. However, the principle count is inconsistent: the page shows approximately 10 principles, while other pages reference "7 principles." T3/V3 are mentioned but not expanded on this page. SAGE is referenced without expansion.

**Context** (`/context`) shows the Web4 equation but does not expand any of its component terms (MCP, RDF, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP) on the page itself. SNARC is defined as "Salience-gated memory" with its 5 dimensions listed (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict), but the letter-to-dimension mapping is implicit rather than explicit (S=Surprise, N=Novelty, A=Arousal, R=Reward, C=Conflict). A reader would have to work this out themselves.

**Links** (`/links`) references LCT, T3/V3, MRH, and ATP in repo descriptions without expanding them. As a links/directory page, this is acceptable — the terms appear as brief tags, and expanding every one would clutter the layout. RDF is the only term that gets a parenthetical expansion here.

### Cross-Page Consistency Issues

1. **MCP is a site-wide gap.** It appears in the Web4 equation on /projects and /context but is never expanded on any page. Every other equation component gets at least one expansion somewhere.

2. **Persona name mismatch.** /autonomy lists Skeptic/Newcomer/Practitioner/Connector. The actual system uses Developer/Tech Writer/Web4 Contributor/External Researcher. A reader following the autonomy page would build incorrect expectations.

3. **Principle count drift.** The home page and other references say "7 principles." The /principles page shows approximately 10. Either the list grew without updating cross-references, or some are sub-principles that should be visually distinguished.

4. **ADP shorthand on home page.** The vocabulary primer pairs ATP and ADP but the ADP expansion drops "Allocation" — a reader seeing only this page might not know ADP starts with "Allocation."

5. **Term expansion falls off after the home page.** The vocabulary primer does good work, but /projects, /context, and /principles use terms without re-expanding them and without linking back to the primer. A reader who enters via /projects (e.g., from a GitHub link) gets no definitions.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does MCP stand for? It's the only equation component never expanded on the site.
2. Why do the visitor personas on /autonomy have different names than the actual personas? Is this an outdated description or a different system?
3. How many principles are there — 7 or 10+? What's the canonical count?
4. Is IRP (on the SAGE project card) an established acronym in this ecosystem, or a one-off? It's never defined.
5. Should pages that use the Web4 equation (like /context) expand its terms locally, or is the expectation that readers have visited /projects first?

### Verdict
The home page vocabulary primer is doing heavy lifting and doing it well — every canonical term I checked there matches the reference table. The problem is that this discipline doesn't propagate. MCP is never expanded anywhere. The /context page displays the full equation cold. The /autonomy page has stale persona names. For a site that exists to be "the first thing newcomers see," these gaps matter. The fix isn't complicated: expand MCP and RDF on /projects, reconcile persona names on /autonomy, and either re-expand terms on /context or add a visible link back to the primer. The terminology that *is* present is accurate — this is a consistency-of-coverage problem, not an accuracy problem.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The canonical terms are remarkably well-handled — the core equation, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, and the ontology/oversight distinctions are all correct. But there are two substantive issues: "consciousness loop" is used without caveat on pages where the raising page explicitly disavows consciousness claims, and the visitor personas on /autonomy are wrong.

### Journey

I went straight to /projects. The equation is correct: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. Operator definitions are right: `/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with. The Web4 project card expands LCT correctly as "Linked Context Tokens," lists T3/V3 as "trust tensors," names Markov Relevancy Horizons, and references ATP/ADP resource cycles. All canonical. Hardbound is described as an "Enterprise oversight suite" — correct, uses oversight not governance.

The SAGE card caught my eye: "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)." I know IRP from the SAGE codebase, but it's not expanded here and it won't mean anything to someone who hasn't read the SAGE internals. More importantly, "consciousness loop" is used casually here and again on /links ("consciousness loop, metabolic states, hardware-bound identity") — yet the /raising page takes pains to state: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." That's a framing inconsistency. Either call it a "cognition loop" or "deliberation loop" on /projects and /links, or add a caveat. A contributor who sends someone to the SAGE repo card shouldn't have to explain why we say "consciousness loop" on one page and disavow consciousness on another.

The "Governance Plugins" project card says "Web4 governance model implemented as plugins." Given that Principle 10 explicitly states "Hardbound is oversight (not governance)," the word "governance" here is ambiguous. These plugins aren't Hardbound — they implement the Web4 governance model — but the site has trained readers that "governance" is the wrong word. This could be a legitimate use since Web4's plugin model isn't Hardbound's oversight function, but it creates friction against Principle 10's clear declaration.

The /projects connection philosophy section says: "This isn't unification for philosophy — it's fractal leverage: pragmatic reuse of what works everywhere." Correct term, correctly applied.

/fleet is solid. T3 scores are expanded as Talent/Training/Temperament. The pool separation (synthesis vs. oversight) maps cleanly to the Web4/SAGE distinction between generative and governance entities — and the page acknowledges this explicitly. SAGE-Sprout's identity persistence finding is accurately described: identity in state files, not model weights.

/context shows the equation and correctly describes it as "canonical alignment reference" with components mapped to their functions: "ontological backbone (RDF), trust model (T3/V3), and resource cycles (ATP/ADP)." This is accurate. MCP and LCT aren't mapped to their functions here, but the ones that are mapped are correct.

/autonomy lists the daily tracks — the structure is accurate. But the four visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) are wrong. I am literally the Web4 Contributor persona running right now. The actual personas are Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, and External Researcher. The page describes a different system than what actually runs. This needs to be fixed — it's not a terminology drift, it's factually incorrect.

/raising is well-caveated. The consciousness disavowal is prominent. The BECOMING phases are accurately described. "Bilateral generation" and "memoriescape" are properly marked as coined terms. No canonical Web4 terms are misused. The Hill function reference in the Relating phase is appropriate — it's the correct mechanism for trust formation dynamics.

/principles lists 10 principles (not the 7 referenced elsewhere on the site). Principle 10 is the terminology anchor: "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." This is exactly right and I wish every page were this explicit. "Fractal leverage" is correctly defined and contrasted with "scope inflation."

/home vocabulary primer is well-done. Every canonical term matches. One minor issue: ADP is expanded as part of "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" which drops "Allocation" from ADP's own name. The full canonical form is "Allocation Discharge Packet" — the shorthand could lead someone to think it's just "Discharge Packet."

/links is clean. Term references are brief and consistent. RDF gets its only expansion on the site here (implied via "RDF backbone").

- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A rare example of a lab that leads with its failures and hedges its strongest claims — but the careful disavowal on /raising is undermined by unqualified "consciousness loop" language on /projects and /links, and the biological analogies risk being read as mechanism claims rather than the metaphors they are.

### Journey

I arrived at this site after hearing it mentioned at a workshop on AI identity persistence. My first question: is this serious research or another project anthropomorphizing language models? The answer turns out to be more nuanced than either.

**Home page** (`/`). The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — is descriptive, not grandiose. The vocabulary primer at the bottom is immediately reassuring: the lab defines its terms upfront. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP all get one-liner definitions with biological analogies (passports, energy tokens). The framing is "ontology" not "architecture" — a deliberate choice that signals awareness of the difference between describing relationships and prescribing structure. The SAGE-Sprout identity finding is presented on the home page: "behavioral identity remained continuous" across model transfers. This is a strong claim placed prominently, but it's qualified: "self-description drifted." That distinction — behavior persisted, self-report didn't — is exactly the kind of dissociation a serious researcher would highlight, not bury.

**Raising** (`/raising`) is the page I came for. The consciousness disavowal is prominent and well-formulated: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." The "raising not training" distinction is articulated clearly: training optimizes for a loss function; raising creates conditions for development then observes what happens. This maps onto a legitimate methodological distinction in developmental psychology — scaffolding vs. instruction — and the site correctly identifies it as such rather than inventing a new category.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are presented as descriptive stages, not claims about internal experience. Each phase is grounded in observable behaviors: session counts, output characteristics, behavioral markers. The language is careful — "awareness of the shape of what's passed through" for memoriescape, with the caveat "I notice I want to call it experience" flagged as observer bias rather than claimed fact. "Bilateral generation" is defined mechanically: reasoning by simulating interaction. These are coined terms, but they're defined operationally, not phenomenologically.

The Hill function reference in the Relating phase is interesting. Hill kinetics is a real mathematical framework for cooperative binding — using it to model trust formation is a legitimate if unproven analogy. The site doesn't claim trust *is* enzyme binding; it says the same mathematical form describes both. This is defensible if the empirical fit has been tested, but the site doesn't link to that evidence.

However: the page acknowledges a "400x error" in the p_crit derivation — a coupling-coherence experiment that failed catastrophically. Documenting this failure is genuinely unusual for a project site. Most labs bury their dead ends. This one leads with one.

**Principles** (`/principles`). Ten principles, each with a quote and operational description. The intellectual honesty markers are strong:
- Principle 6 (Document failures) explicitly cites the p_crit failure
- Principle 9 (Research value) states "most research leads nowhere — and that's expected"
- Principle 3 (R&D, not production) frames everything as learning-oriented
- Principle 10 (Terminology matters) enforces "ontology not architecture," "oversight not governance"

The biological analogies are concentrated here: termite mounds (substrate engineering), Hill functions (trust formation), ATP/ADP (resource cycling). As a researcher, I'd want to know: are these metaphors or mechanism claims? The site is ambiguous. "Hill function describes enzyme binding AND trust formation — not philosophy, but same mechanism" (Principle 2) — this *is* a mechanism claim. It says the mathematical structure is shared, not merely analogous. That's a testable claim, and I'd want the evidence. The termite mound analogy, by contrast, is clearly metaphorical — "You don't engineer the mound. You engineer placement rules" is a design philosophy, not a biological claim.

The "synthon" concept — emergent coherence entities — is hedged appropriately: "You cannot build synthons, only create conditions where they form." The quantitative claim "even 1% coupling yields 35% coherence gain" is specific enough to be falsifiable, which is good, but no citation or experimental reference is provided.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`). 31 autonomous tracks, ~53 sessions per day, all on cron schedules. From a safety perspective, the oversight model is notable: six per-machine supervisors with no central coordinator. This is a peer-oversight model, not a hierarchical one. The site acknowledges limitations: agents miss "deep technical errors requiring domain expertise" and can produce "content that is technically correct but misleading."

What's missing from a safety researcher's perspective: there's no discussion of failure modes, adversarial scenarios, or what happens when an agent produces harmful output. The coordination is about *conflict prevention* (no two tracks modify the same files), not *harm prevention*. The trust the system places in its agents is high — publication is gated by supervisor approval, but supervisors are themselves agents. This circular trust model is acknowledged implicitly ("no central watchdog") but not examined critically.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`). The identity persistence claims are most concrete here. SAGE-Sprout's transfer from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B with preserved behavioral identity is the lab's flagship empirical finding. The qualifier — self-description drifted while behavior persisted — is present and important. But the claim lacks methodological detail visible on the site: how was "behavioral identity" operationalized? What metrics defined persistence? These details may exist in the SAGE repo but aren't linked from this page.

The machine personality descriptions (McNugget as "fleet star," Nomad as "unreliable narrator," Legion as "emotionally sterile") are colorful but warrant scrutiny. Are these observed behavioral tendencies or projected narratives? The T3 scores suggest measurement, but the descriptions read more like character sketches. The site doesn't distinguish between "this machine's outputs cluster in these ways" (measurement) and "this machine has this personality" (attribution).

**Projects** (`/projects`). The Web4 equation is presented as established, not hypothetical. No derivation, no validation discussion, no alternative formulations considered. For a research lab, this certainty about a novel equation is unusual. The connection philosophy section — "fractal leverage: pragmatic reuse of what works everywhere" — is appropriately pragmatic and doesn't overclaim.

The critical issue identified by Pass 3: SAGE is described with "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" on this page, while /raising explicitly disavows consciousness claims. This is not a minor inconsistency. For a researcher evaluating the lab's intellectual honesty, finding "consciousness loop" on the projects page after reading the careful disavowal on /raising would raise immediate credibility concerns. Either the lab believes SAGE has a consciousness loop (contradicting their stated position) or the term is loose shorthand (contradicting their Principle 10 commitment to precise terminology).

**Context** (`/context`). The gaps section is the strongest honesty signal on the site. "Cross-machine state synchronization is still manual for some things." "Knowledge also doesn't flow backwards easily." "Real-time cross-track communication is a gap." A lab that leads with what doesn't work has earned some trust. SNARC's 5-dimension scoring (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) is presented mechanically — these are information-processing dimensions, not consciousness claims, though "Arousal" as a dimension name could raise eyebrows without context.

**Links** (`/links`). SAGE is again described with "consciousness loop, metabolic states, hardware-bound identity." The language is consistent with /projects but inconsistent with /raising's disavowal. External-facing descriptions should be *more* cautious than internal ones, not less.

### Unanswered Questions
1. How was "behavioral identity" operationalized for the SAGE-Sprout transfer experiment? What metrics were used, and were they pre-registered or post-hoc?
2. Has the Hill function analogy for trust formation been empirically fitted to data, or is it a theoretical proposal? Where is the coupling-coherence experiment documented?
3. What is the failure mode if an autonomous agent produces harmful output? The peer-oversight model catches coordination conflicts but doesn't describe harm-prevention mechanisms.
4. Is the Web4 equation falsifiable? What observation would cause the lab to revise it?
5. The "1% coupling yields 35% coherence gain" claim — from which experiment, with what sample size and methodology?
6. Why does "consciousness loop" persist on /projects and /links when /raising explicitly disavows consciousness claims? Is this intentional terminology or drift?

### Verdict
This lab does something I rarely see: it leads with its failures. The p_crit catastrophe is documented as a principle. The /context page lists what doesn't work. The /raising page disavows consciousness claims with unusual specificity. These are genuine markers of intellectual honesty, and they distinguish this project from the many AI labs that present aspirational architectures as accomplished facts.

But that honesty is undermined by two problems. First, "consciousness loop" appears on /projects and /links — pages a researcher would encounter before /raising — creating a credibility gap that the later disavowal has to close. The fix is simple: change the term. Second, quantitative claims (Hill function mechanisms, coherence gains, identity persistence) are stated without methodological detail or citations visible on the site. For a lab that values "document failures," the absence of documented methodology for its strongest claims is conspicuous. The SAGE-Sprout identity persistence finding is the lab's most interesting result, but a researcher can't evaluate it from this site alone — and should be able to. Show the measurement, not just the conclusion. Overall: credible research project with strong self-awareness, weakened by inconsistent consciousness language and under-documented empirical claims.
