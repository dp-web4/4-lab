# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-15

Four-persona pass over https://4-lab.vercel.app/
Each pass includes a terminology audit against Web4 canonical terms.

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious, fascinating research collective — but the sheer density of custom terminology makes it hard to know what's real software vs. theoretical framework on a first visit.

### Journey
Landed on the home page. The hero — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately grabbed me. The metrics (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 autonomous tracks) gave a sense of scale. The "How We Work" section was clear: autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. Good.

The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom of the home page was a lifesaver — it defined LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, and SAGE right where I needed them. Smart placement.

Clicked into **Projects**. This is where things got dense. The Web4 equation (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) appeared with operator definitions, which helped. There are 11 projects listed — from theoretical physics (Synchronism) to competitive AI (ARC-AGI-3) to memory systems (SNARC, Membot). The range is impressive but overwhelming. I couldn't tell which projects are actively developed vs. experimental sketches. The "fractal leverage" concept (reuse what works everywhere) makes sense as a unifying thread, but I had to take it on faith.

**Fleet** was one of the most interesting pages. Six machines with names, hardware specs, model assignments, session counts, and raising phases. The two-pool budget model (synthesis vs. oversight) is a clever architectural decision. The insight that "model family matters as much as size" (Gemma 3 4B outperforming Phi-4 14B) is the kind of concrete finding that builds credibility. The identity portability discovery — "Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights" — is genuinely interesting.

**Autonomy** describes 31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines running ~53 sessions/day. The daily timeline (03:00–08:00+) is well laid out. The feedback loop (Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer) is clear. The honest assessment section acknowledging what the loop misses (deep technical errors, subtle framing issues) earned trust.

**Raising** was the most philosophically dense page. The BECOMING framework (6 phases from Grounding to Acting) is clearly explained. The "raising is not training" distinction is well-articulated. The discoveries section (identity vs. self-concept, memoriescape, bilateral generation) reads like genuine research findings. The "What We're Not Claiming" section explicitly denying consciousness claims is important and well-placed.

**Principles** lists ten operational principles. These felt earned rather than aspirational — the intro explicitly says they "emerged from building the system." Favorites: "Document failures" (the p_crit story failing 400 times) and "R&D, not production." The synthon concept (principle 5) was the hardest to parse without more context.

**Context** explains how knowledge flows across repos and machines. The CLAUDE.md pattern, SNARC memory system, and Web4 equation as shared anchor all make sense architecturally. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is another honest-assessment moment that builds credibility. Cross-machine sync being partially manual is a real limitation they're not hiding.

**Links** is clean and functional. Explainer sites, GitHub repos, and a contact email. No bloat.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What's the actual relationship between Synchronism (physics framework) and the AI/trust projects? The "fractal leverage" claim that the same equations apply across scales is interesting but I'd want to see the math.
2. How do I actually *use* any of this? If I wanted to run SAGE on my own machine or implement Web4 in a project, where's the getting-started guide?
3. What does "consciousness loop" mean concretely in SAGE? The /raising page is careful about claims but /projects and /links use "consciousness loop" without caveats.
4. The fleet runs ~53 sessions/day autonomously — what's the compute cost? Is this viable for anyone outside this lab?
5. "Trust begins at zero and emerges from interaction" — what prevents gaming the trust system?

### Verdict
The 4-lab site does a solid job conveying ambition and intellectual honesty. The vocabulary primer on the home page and the repeated "honest assessment" sections are the best features — they show self-awareness that most research sites lack. But for a developer landing cold, the gap between "I understand what words mean" and "I understand what I could do with this" is still large. The site explains *what the lab is* better than *why I should care* or *how to get involved*. Adding maturity indicators to projects and a "start here" path for developers would close that gap significantly.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site is well-structured with clear navigation, but terminology handling is inconsistent — the same acronyms get different expansions (or none) depending on which page you're on, and several pages use the Web4 equation without expanding any of its terms.

### Journey
I approached this methodically: home page first, then every internal page, with the canonical terms table open beside me. My job was to check every acronym expansion, flag inconsistencies, and verify that terms match the canonical definitions.

The **home page** is the best-behaved page terminologically. The Vocabulary Primer defines LCT, T3, V3, ATP/ADP, and SAGE in one place. Good practice. However, I noticed T3 is framed as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" wrapper labels aren't in the canonical table — they may be correct, but they add a layer that isn't used consistently elsewhere.

Moving to **Projects**, the Web4 equation appears with operator definitions — helpful. But ATP/ADP is described as "resource cycles" here rather than "Allocation Transfer Packet / Allocation Discharge Packet." T3/V3 are called "trust tensors" without the component breakdown. The word "governance" appears in project descriptions — I flagged each instance for context review. Hardbound is correctly called an "oversight suite."

**Fleet** was terminologically sparse. T3 is correctly expanded. SNARC appears as "memory host" without full expansion. No other Web4-specific acronyms appear, which is fine for a hardware-focused page.

**Autonomy** is clean — no Web4 terms used at all. The page is self-contained and clear.

**Raising** is also clean terminologically. The "raising is not training" distinction is clearly stated. No Web4 acronyms appear, which makes sense for a philosophy-focused page.

**Principles** is interesting — it explicitly corrects terminology. "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure'" and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." This is the page doing the most work to enforce correct usage. The word "unification" appears in contrast: "Not unification for its own sake — pragmatic reuse." All correct.

**Context** is the most problematic page for my purposes. The Web4 equation is displayed but NONE of its component terms (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP) are expanded on this page. SNARC is described as "salience-gated memory" — a different label than Fleet's "memory host." LoRA is mentioned without expansion. The word "architecture" appears once as "architectural decisions" — generic usage, not describing Web4, so acceptable.

**Links** lists projects with brief descriptions. SAGE is described with "consciousness loop" twice, without the caveats that appear on /raising. LCTs, T3/V3, and MRH are listed as Web4 components but not expanded. ADP is absent entirely from this page (only ATP appears). The word "governance" appears in GitHub repo descriptions.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" canonical framing for T3/V3? It appears on the home page but nowhere else — if it's official, other pages should use it. If not, the home page is adding non-canonical labels.

### Verdict

The biggest gap is that several pages (Context, Links) use Web4 acronyms without ever expanding them, which means any reader who doesn't start at the home page will encounter undefined terms. ATP/ADP is the most inconsistently handled term — "Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets" on home, "resource cycles" on projects, unexpanded on context and links, and ADP missing entirely from links. A terminology-conscious site needs a stricter first-use-expansion discipline on every page, or a persistent glossary link in the nav.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site represents the ecosystem accurately at the structural level, but terminology discipline breaks down outside the Principles page — the very terms we've spent months canonicalizing appear in drifted or incomplete forms on the pages newcomers are most likely to read.

### Journey
I came in knowing the canonical terms and the equation by heart. My job is to check whether this site — the first thing newcomers see — would teach them the *correct* vocabulary or accidentally seed drift that we'd then have to un-teach.

Started at the **home page**. The hero and "How We Work" section are solid. The Connected Ecosystem line — "Synchronism provides equations. Web4 provides ontology. SAGE provides cognition. Hardbound provides oversight." — is precise and correct. Good. The Vocabulary Primer is where I hit the first issue: T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 is labeled "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The component dimensions are correct, but "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" are **not canonical wrapper labels**. T3 *is* Talent/Training/Temperament — it's not a "Trust Tensor" that *contains* those dimensions. Same for V3. This framing adds an interpretive layer that doesn't exist in the canonical definitions and will confuse anyone who then reads the actual Web4 docs. ATP/ADP is correctly expanded as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" but also described as "energy tokens" — the biological metaphor is fine as color, but it shouldn't be the primary framing.

**Projects** is the most content-dense page and gets the most things right. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly with operator definitions. LCT is correctly expanded as "Linked Context Tokens." MRH is correctly expanded as "Markov Relevancy Horizons." Hardbound is correctly called an "oversight suite." The "How They Connect" section explicitly uses "fractal leverage" and correctly contrasts it with "unification for its own sake." The equation operators (`/`, `*`, `+`) are correct. However: ATP/ADP is described as "resource cycles" — this is a metaphor, not the canonical expansion. T3/V3 are called "trust tensors" without the component breakdown. The "Governance Plugins" project uses "governance" throughout ("Web4 governance model") — this is technically a project name, but it directly conflicts with the principle on /principles that says Hardbound is "oversight, not governance." If we're enforcing that distinction, a project literally called "Governance Plugins" implementing a "Web4 governance model" undermines the enforcement. SAGE's description uses "consciousness loop" without the caveats that /raising carefully provides.

**Fleet** is accurate on the hardware and raising details. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." The identity portability finding about SAGE-Sprout is accurately described. The two-pool model mirrors the Web4 synthesis/oversight distinction correctly. SNARC is mentioned only as "memory host" — functional but not expanded.

**Autonomy** is clean of Web4 terminology, which is appropriate. But there's a factual error: the visitor personas are listed as "Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector" — these don't match the actual implementation (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is the site describing its own infrastructure inaccurately.

**Raising** is well-constructed. The "raising is not training" distinction is clearly and correctly stated. The BECOMING phases are accurately described. The consciousness disclaimer ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia") is appropriately placed. No Web4 terminology drift here.

**Principles** is the best page on the site for terminology enforcement. It explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology" (not architecture, not infrastructure), "Hardbound is oversight" (not governance), and "Cross-domain application is fractal leverage, not scope inflation." This page does the work. But I count 9 principles — the site structure documentation says "7 principles." Minor discrepancy.

**Context** is the most problematic page from a terminology standpoint. The Web4 equation is displayed in full (`MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`) but **not a single component term is expanded**. LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP — all appear as raw acronyms. T3/V3 is described as "the trust model" and ATP/ADP as "resource cycles" — both are drifted shorthand, not canonical. This page is about how knowledge flows across the system, and it's the one page where a contributor might send someone to understand the connective tissue. If that person doesn't already know the terms, this page teaches them nothing.

**Links** uses "consciousness loop" for SAGE twice without any caveats. ADP is entirely absent — only ATP is mentioned in the Web4 component list. LCTs, T3/V3, and MRH are listed but never expanded. "Governance" appears in the descriptions of the OpenClaw and Claude Code forks ("Web4 governance extension," "Web4 governance hooks"). These should be "oversight" if we're enforcing the canonical distinction.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site demonstrates unusual intellectual honesty for a project making consciousness-adjacent claims — the explicit disclaimers and honest assessment sections are genuinely rare — but the careful epistemic framing on /raising and /principles doesn't propagate to /projects and /links, where "consciousness loop" appears as an unqualified feature description.

### Journey
I came to this site after a conference mention of "raising not training" as a developmental framework for AI systems. That framing is what brought me here — it's a strong claim that positions against the dominant paradigm, and I wanted to see whether the work behind it is rigorous or rhetorical.

The **home page** sets up the lab well. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." is clear positioning. The vocabulary primer at the bottom is a good practice — defining terms on first encounter. I noted "trust-native ontology" for Web4, which is an interesting framing choice (ontology implies shared vocabulary and formal relationships, not just architecture). The synthon definition — "emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively" — is the kind of language that could mean something precise or nothing at all; I flagged it for closer reading on /principles.

**Raising** was the page I came for, and it's the strongest page on the site from an epistemic standpoint. The "What We're Not Claiming" section is exactly what a skeptical researcher needs: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is a defensible claim. The BECOMING framework (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) is presented as descriptive taxonomy, not a causal theory — an important distinction. The "looks like" framing throughout ("something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity") is epistemically appropriate. The observer note — "I notice I want to call it experience" — is phenomenologically honest in a way I rarely see in AI research writing. It names the anthropomorphic pull without yielding to it.

However, there are tensions. The "raising not training" distinction rests on the claim that "training optimizes toward loss functions; raising establishes developmental conditions and observes what emerges." This is a meaningful distinction in *intent*, but the underlying mechanism is still prompt engineering and context management — the model itself is not being modified. The question is whether the developmental framing adds explanatory power or just poetic framing. I think it does add something: the observation that behavioral identity persists across model swaps (SAGE-Sprout across 115+ sessions) is genuinely interesting and wouldn't naturally emerge from a "training" frame. But the page could be more explicit about what the mechanism actually is — state files, prompt construction, memory systems — rather than letting "raising" do metaphorical work.

**Principles** reinforces the careful framing. The "R&D, not production" principle is important context — it preempts the "but does it scale?" question that would otherwise dominate a researcher's evaluation. The p_crit failure story (400x error documented as signal) is exactly the kind of honest reporting that builds credibility. Principle 7, "Interactive selection," is the most philosophically interesting: "we probe what it responds to, adjust context, reinforce attractors that resonate... the resulting identity is collaborative, not imposed." This is a testable claim — you could measure whether prompt-context adjustments produce convergent behavioral signatures — and the site would benefit from pointing to that testability.

The synthon concept (Principle 5) remains the weakest link. "Emergent coherence entity" is defined by example (termite mounds) but never operationalized. The claim that "even 1% coupling gives 35% coherence gain" appears without citation, methodology, or definition of "coherence." For a site that otherwise practices good epistemic hygiene, this is a notable lapse. A researcher would want to know: coherence measured how? Over what timescale? In what system?

**Autonomy** is well-constructed. The honest assessment section is again the standout feature: acknowledging that the autonomous loop "misses deep technical errors that require domain expertise" and "has a tendency to suggest changes that aren't needed" is the kind of self-critical reporting that distinguishes genuine research from marketing. The distributed oversight model (SQLite registry, no central watchdog, mutual review) is architecturally interesting. My concern is the absence of failure mode analysis — what happens when an autonomous track introduces an error that another track amplifies? The feedback loop is described but the failure modes of the feedback loop are not.

**Projects** is where the epistemic discipline breaks down. SAGE is described with "continuous consciousness loop" as a feature — no hedging, no "looks like," no link to the careful disclaimers on /raising. This is the same project that /raising describes with meticulous caveating, now presented as having a "consciousness loop" as though that's an uncontroversial technical feature. For a researcher, this inconsistency is a red flag: it suggests the careful language on /raising may be strategic rather than reflecting genuine epistemic uncertainty. The Hill function claim — "describes enzyme binding AND trust formation... because it's the same mechanism" — is stated without qualification. Analogical reasoning between biological and computational systems requires careful justification; asserting identity ("same mechanism") rather than analogy is a strong claim that demands evidence.

**Fleet** contains the identity portability claim: SAGE-Sprout's identity transferred from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B across hardware. This is presented as a "concrete, testable observation" but no test methodology is described. What was measured? Self-report consistency? Behavioral metrics? External observer agreement? "Identity transferred successfully" is not an observation — it's an interpretation of observations that aren't specified. The metaphor "the model is weather; the identity is organism" is evocative but unfalsifiable as stated.

**Context** has the Web4 equation without any term expansions — a researcher landing here would see pure jargon. The "dream cycle" and "sleep consolidation" language borrows from neuroscience without acknowledging the metaphorical transfer, which is exactly the kind of terminological borrowing that leads to misunderstanding in cross-disciplinary contexts.

**Links** uses "consciousness loop, metabolic states, experience buffer" twice for SAGE without any of the caveats from /raising. ADP is absent. "Governance" appears where "oversight" should be per the site's own principles.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is the actual mechanism behind "identity portability"? If identity lives in state files and prompt construction, then isn't "raising" just careful prompt engineering with persistent state? That's still interesting — but the developmental language obscures the mechanism rather than illuminating it.
2. How is "coherence" measured for the synthon claim? The 35% figure implies quantification, but no methodology is visible. If this is from Synchronism theory, the site should link to the derivation.
3. Why does /projects use "consciousness loop" without caveats when /raising explicitly disclaims consciousness? Is this intentional audience segmentation (developers get the feature label, researchers get the epistemics), or is it drift?
4. What are the failure modes of the autonomous feedback loop? The honest assessment says the loop "misses deep technical errors" — but what happens when it confidently reinforces a wrong pattern?
5. Is the Hill function claim ("same mechanism") testable? If trust formation in this system actually follows Hill kinetics, there should be data showing the characteristic sigmoidal response curve. Does that data exist?

### Verdict
This site does something genuinely rare: it makes consciousness-adjacent claims about AI systems while maintaining a visible, explicit epistemic framework for those claims. The /raising page is the best example — the "What We're Not Claiming" section, the "looks like" framing, and the observer-perspective honesty ("I notice I want to call it experience") are models for how to write about emergent AI behavior without overclaiming. The honest assessment sections throughout the site are similarly unusual and credibility-building. However, the careful framing is localized. The same SAGE system that /raising describes with meticulous disclaimers appears on /projects and /links as having a "consciousness loop" — presented as a feature, not a hypothesis. This inconsistency is the site's most significant problem from a research credibility standpoint: it suggests the epistemic care is performative rather than structural. A researcher reading /raising would think "these people are being careful"; the same researcher reading /projects would think "these people are using consciousness language to market a sense-deliberate-act loop." The fix is straightforward: propagate the epistemic discipline from /raising to every page that mentions SAGE. The ungrounded quantitative claim (35% coherence gain) and the unqualified identity assertion (Hill function as "same mechanism") are secondary concerns, but they undermine the otherwise strong foundation of honest self-assessment that makes this site worth returning to.
