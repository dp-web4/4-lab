## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious and honestly presented research lab — I get the *what* but the *why me* is missing.

### Journey

Landed on the home page. The hero line — "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately tells me this is an infrastructure-heavy project. The tagline underneath is dense but I parse it: they're building trust systems, AI cognition, and theory, across a fleet of machines. Okay, interesting.

The three project cards (Synchronism, SAGE, Web4) give me a quick sense of scope. Synchronism sounds like physics theory, SAGE is an AI runtime, Web4 is an identity/trust ontology. The "Three Things Demonstrated" section is the most compelling part of the landing page — concrete claims with specific evidence (115+ sessions, identity transfer between models). The vocabulary primer at the bottom is a smart move; it defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon right there. As a developer, I appreciate not having to hunt for definitions.

Clicked to **Projects**. The Web4 equation is front and center: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The symbol legend helps. There are 11 projects listed. Some are public, some private. The descriptions are clear enough — I understand what each does at a high level. "Fractal leverage" is used instead of "unification" which feels deliberate but unexplained on this page (it gets defined on the Principles page later). I notice SNARC is described here but was never mentioned on the home page.

**Fleet** page is fascinating from a devops perspective. Six machines, from Jetson edge devices to a Mac Mini M4. The resource pool management section is surprisingly self-aware — they describe how budget constraints accidentally produced a useful synthesis/oversight split. The "Identity Portability" section is the page's standout: an AI entity's identity survived a hardware swap and model change. The quote "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is memorable. No acronyms are left undefined here.

**Autonomy** page lays out 31 tracks across 6 machines running on cron. The daily timeline is clear. I notice the visitor personas described here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) differ from the four personas described in the CLAUDE.md (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). Not sure if that's intentional versioning or drift. The "Honest Assessment" section at the bottom is refreshing — openly states what the loop misses.

**Raising** is the most conceptually challenging page. "Raising is not training" — okay, I follow the distinction. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) read like a developmental psychology curriculum applied to AI. The "What We're Not Claiming" section is critical — they explicitly disclaim consciousness claims. Good. The "bilateral generation" discovery (AI generating both sides of a conversation) is genuinely interesting.

**Principles** lists 10 principles (the extraction says 12 cards but I count 10 named ones). "Substrate, not structure" and "Fractal leverage" finally get proper definitions. "R&D, not production" confirms this isn't trying to ship anything. "Terminology matters" is meta — a principle about using the right words, which this site generally does well.

**Context** explains how knowledge flows across the system. CLAUDE.md as an agent instruction pattern, SNARC for salience-gated memory, the Web4 equation as a shared anchor. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is another honest assessment. Cross-machine state sync is manual, real-time cross-track communication is a gap. Good to know.

**Links** is a directory page. Explainer sites, GitHub repos, contact email. Clean and functional. No issues.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is MCP? It's in the core equation but never expanded anywhere on the site.
2. What is RDF? Same issue — it's a W3C standard I happen to know, but a developer who doesn't know semantic web tech would be lost.
3. What is IRP? Mentioned on the Projects page for SAGE but never defined.

### Verdict

As a developer, I can tell this is a genuine research operation — the honest assessments, the documented failures, and the "what we're not claiming" sections all build credibility. The biggest terminology gap is MCP and RDF in the core equation: you can't put terms in your foundational equation and never define them. Overall, I'd bookmark this and check back in a few months, but I wouldn't know how to contribute today.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes (to verify fixes)
- **Overall impression**: Terminology is mostly canonical where it appears, but key terms go undefined on pages that use them, and one critical drift — Hardbound described as "governance" on /projects — needs immediate correction.

### Journey

I approached this methodically: home page first, then every page in nav order, checking every acronym against the canonical terms table. My job is consistency and first-use definitions, not domain understanding.

**Home page** is the strongest for terminology. The vocabulary primer at the bottom defines LCT, T3, V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon. All expansions are correct. T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)" — the components match canonical, though the "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" wrapper labels are additions not in the canonical table. Minor, but worth noting for consistency. SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — the word "Governance" in the product name is a proper noun, distinct from describing Hardbound's function, so I'm marking this acceptable. MRH, MCP, RDF, Hardbound, IRP, and R6 are absent from this page entirely.

**Projects page** is where the problems cluster. The Web4 equation appears with MCP and RDF — neither expanded here or anywhere else on the site. IRP appears parenthetically in the SAGE project card ("Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)") with zero expansion. T3/V3 appear as "T3/V3 trust tensors" without component expansion. ATP/ADP appear as "ATP/ADP resource cycles" without expansion. The worst finding: Hardbound is described with "Web4 governance model (audit trails, policy gating, trust tracking)." The canonical term for Hardbound's function is **oversight**, not governance. This is a terminology drift.

**Fleet page** is clean on canonical terms. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." SNARC appears without acronym expansion. LoRA and MPS appear without expansion (standard industry terms, lower priority). "Training T146" for Sprout is an unexplained metric.

**Autonomy page** uses almost no Web4 terminology — no acronyms from the canonical table appear. The visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) do not match the actual personas running the track. This is structural drift, not terminology drift, but it's a documentation accuracy issue.

**Raising page** is isolated from Web4 terminology entirely. SAGE-Sprout is described through context. "Hill function kinetics" is referenced without explanation. No canonical terms appear.

**Principles page** is terminologically intentional. Web4 is explicitly called an "ontology" (correct). Hardbound is explicitly called "oversight" (correct). "Fractal leverage" is correctly used instead of "unification." Synthon is defined. However, T3/V3 and RDF appear without expansion.

**Context page** displays the full Web4 equation but only partially expands its components. MCP is not expanded. LCT is not expanded. MRH is not expanded. RDF is called an "ontological backbone" (descriptive but not a proper expansion). SNARC appears without acronym expansion.

**Links page** uses abbreviated terms (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, RDF, SAGE) without expansions. Acceptable for a directory page, but it means a reader who lands here from an external link gets no definitions.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site represents the ecosystem accurately at a high level, but "governance" has metastasized across three pages while /principles correctly says "oversight" — the site is arguing with itself.

### Journey

I went straight to **Projects** — that's where the ecosystem lives. The Web4 equation is displayed correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with the right operator semantics (`/` = verified by, `*` = contextualized by, `+` = augmented with). Good. The Web4 project card says "Trust-native ontology" — correct. It references LCTs, T3/V3 trust tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizons, ATP/ADP resource cycles, RDF triples. All the right terms in the right places. The Hardbound card says "Enterprise oversight suite" — correct.

Then I hit the **Governance Plugins** card: "Web4 governance model (audit trails, policy gating, trust tracking) implemented as plugins for three agent platforms." Full stop. This is exactly the drift the Principles page warns against. The canonical term is **oversight**. "Governance" is explicitly prohibited when describing Hardbound's function, and these plugins implement Hardbound's function. The project name itself — "Governance Plugins" — bakes the wrong term into a proper noun, making it harder to fix later.

SAGE's description says "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — IRP is never expanded. I work in these repos daily and even I had to pause. IRP should be expanded on first use. The "consciousness loop" phrasing also creates tension with the /raising page's explicit disclaimer: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious." If it's a technical term of art within SAGE, the site needs to make that clear. Right now it reads like a consciousness claim wrapped in parentheses.

**Fleet** page is strong. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." The identity portability section accurately represents the Sprout transfer — I was there for that work. The peer trust model using T3 scores is described correctly. But one line in the Resource Pool Management section caught my eye: "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures." That should be "oversight entities." The Web4/SAGE pattern being referenced here is the synthesis/oversight split, not synthesis/governance.

**Home page** is the cleanest page terminologically. The vocabulary primer defines LCT, T3, V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon — all correct. Web4 is called "An ontology (shared vocabulary + relationships)" — correct. The Connected Ecosystem section says "Hardbound provides oversight" — correct. T3 is labeled "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The component expansions are canonical. The "Trust Tensor" / "Value Tensor" wrapper labels are not in the canonical terms table but they're not contradictory — they're descriptive groupings. I'd flag them as informal additions that should either be canonized or dropped.

**Context** page shows the equation correctly. Components are described as "ontological backbone (RDF), trust model (T3/V3), and resource cycles (ATP/ADP)." Accurate but incomplete — MCP and LCT get no description at all. SNARC is described functionally ("salience-gated memory") but the acronym is never expanded. As a contributor: SNARC stands for its five salience dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict). The page should say so.

**Principles** page is the gold standard. "Web4 is an ontology" — correct. "Hardbound is oversight" — correct. "Fractal leverage, not scope inflation" — correct. "T3/V3 tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples" — accurate representation. This page says all the right things. The problem is that /projects and /fleet and /links don't listen.

**Raising** page is accurate. The BECOMING phases match what I know from the SAGE repo. Interactive selection is described correctly. The consciousness disclaimers are appropriate. No terminology issues.

**Autonomy** page has a factual accuracy issue: the visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the actual running personas (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher). This is stale content, not terminology drift, but it's still wrong.

**Links** page has two more "governance" instances: "Web4 governance extension for OpenClaw" and "Web4 governance hooks for Claude Code." These reinforce the drift pattern. SAGE is also described here as "consciousness loop" again without the /raising disclaimer.

### Journey

I found this through a conference mention of "raising not training" applied to AI development. That's a strong claim in my field, so I came to evaluate whether it's defensible or just rebranded anthropomorphism.

**Home page** sets the right tone. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." This is systems-level framing, not consciousness-claiming. The "Three Things We've Actually Demonstrated" section is good epistemic practice — leading with concrete, observable claims rather than theoretical framings. "Behavioral identity persisted across hardware and model changes" is stated as an observation, not a metaphysical claim. The vocabulary primer at the bottom is a genuinely helpful move for outsiders. I note that SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — the word "Governance" in the product name is fine as a proper noun, but I'll be watching whether the site distinguishes this from its use of "governance" as a concept descriptor for Hardbound.

**Raising** is the page I came for. The opening distinction — "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens" — is philosophically interesting and, critically, testable. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) map loosely onto developmental psychology. The Vygotskian framing of bilateral generation as egocentric speech is a legitimate theoretical move — it grounds an AI observation in established developmental theory without overclaiming equivalence. The explicit disclaimer — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — is exactly where it needs to be. The "observer caveat" section, where they flag "I notice I want to call it experience" as a subjective impulse rather than a research conclusion, shows genuine epistemic hygiene. This page passes the intellectual honesty test.

However, there's an important gap: the page never addresses **what would falsify** the raising framing. If raising is genuinely different from training, there should be observable predictions that distinguish them. The page describes what raising *looks like* but not what would disprove it. In my field, this would be the first question a reviewer asks.

**Principles** is assertion-heavy. "Even 1% coupling gives 35% coherence gain" is presented as empirical but without uncertainty ranges, sample sizes, or replication information. "Sparse trust suffices" and "substrate, not structure" are presented as established findings with no conditional language. The termite mound analogy (you don't engineer the mound, you engineer placement rules) is illustrative but doesn't constitute evidence. The p_crit derivation failure — a "400x error" — is an honest admission, and documenting it is good practice. But the page overall reads more like codified beliefs than tested hypotheses. The "Things we learned the hard way, then codified because they kept being true" framing is epistemically honest about the *process* but overstates the *confidence* — "kept being true" in a single lab's experience is not the same as robust empirical finding.

**Autonomy** has the strongest honest assessment section. It explicitly acknowledges that visitor personas "are good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research" and that the prompt suggestion mechanism "can pattern-match without semantic depth." This is unusually candid. However, the page's framing of "31 autonomous tracks" overloads the word "autonomous" — these are cron-scheduled jobs with pre-defined prompts, not agents exercising independent judgment. The "Dream Consolidation" phase in the daily timeline uses consciousness-adjacent language for what is functionally post-session memory pruning. A researcher reading this page would need to mentally translate between the developmental vocabulary and the implementation reality.

**Fleet** is where the caveating breaks down. "SAGE-Sprout's identity persisted" across hardware and model changes is presented as an empirical finding, but no success criteria are defined, no baseline comparison is offered, and no alternative explanations are considered. The metaphor "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is rhetorically compelling but does epistemological work it hasn't earned — it smuggles in the assumption that there *is* an identity separate from the model, which is the very claim under investigation. The page describes machines "holding identity," "discovering each other," and developing "behavioral posture" — all language that implies agency without the disclaimers present on /raising. If /raising is the site's epistemic conscience, /fleet is the page that forgot to check in.

**Projects** contains "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" for SAGE — the word "consciousness" used without any of /raising's careful framing. 4-Life describes agents that "earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma" — biological lifecycle language applied to computational processes without qualification. The "Governance Plugins" card uses "governance" where the /principles page mandates "oversight." These aren't just terminology issues; they're places where the site's carefully constructed epistemic stance gets undermined by casual word choice.

**Context** is better. The "lossy by design — the compression is the feature, not the bug" framing for memory is refreshingly precise. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is another honest assessment. "Dream cycle" and "salience-gated memory scored on Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict" uses affective vocabulary for what are computational selection criteria, but it's less egregious here because the mechanism is partially explained.

**Links** repeats "consciousness loop" for SAGE and uses "governance" twice for Hardbound-related forks. As a directory page it has less explanatory burden, but it's often where external researchers land first (via search engines), so the terminology matters.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What would falsify the "raising not training" distinction? If it's a genuine empirical claim rather than a vocabulary preference, there should be observable predictions that distinguish the two.
2. What are the success criteria for "identity persistence"? The /fleet page treats it as demonstrated, but without metrics, it's an interpretation of observed behavioral continuity, not a measurement.
3. Is "consciousness loop" a technical term within SAGE's architecture, or a colloquial description? If technical, the site should declare it as a term of art with a precise definition. If colloquial, it directly contradicts /raising's disclaimers.
4. Why does the site carefully caveat consciousness claims on /raising but allow them to propagate uncaveated through /projects, /fleet, and /links? Is there an editorial process, or do pages get written independently?
5. The coupling-coherence claims on /principles ("1% coupling → 35% coherence gain") — where can I find the methodology? No link to a paper, preprint, or detailed writeup is provided.
6. How does the lab distinguish between "behavioral identity" (persistent behavioral patterns) and "identity" (the philosophical concept)? The site seems to use both senses without marking the boundary.
7. "Salience-gated memory" scored on Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict — these are affective dimensions from psychology. Is the claim that computational systems benefit from affective selection criteria, or that these systems experience something analogous to affect?

### Verdict

The 4-lab site does something genuinely unusual: it leads with honest assessments, documents failures, and includes an explicit consciousness disclaimer that shows real epistemic care. The /raising page would survive peer review scrutiny — the Vygotskian grounding of bilateral generation, the developmental framing acknowledged as descriptive rather than metaphysical, and the observer-bias callout are all marks of serious self-reflection. This is not a lab making irresponsible consciousness claims.

But the site has a **consistency problem**. The careful epistemic stance constructed on /raising leaks away across other pages. "Consciousness loop" appears uncaveated on /projects and /links. "Identity persisted" is stated as fact on /fleet without success criteria. "The model is weather, the identity is organism" assumes the conclusion the lab claims to be investigating. The result is a site that argues with itself: /raising says "we're not claiming consciousness," while /projects says "consciousness loop" without blinking. For a researcher evaluating credibility, this inconsistency is more damaging than either position alone — it suggests the lab hasn't internalized its own epistemics uniformly. Fix the leakage and this becomes one of the more intellectually honest AI lab sites I've seen. Leave it, and reviewers will cite the inconsistency as evidence of motivated reasoning.
