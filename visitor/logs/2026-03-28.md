## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious, well-organized research lab with a surprisingly clear landing page — I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left understanding the shape of the project, even if some of the deeper theory went over my head.

### Journey

Landed on the home page. First thing I see: "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." Okay, catchy. The tagline below is denser — "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition" — but the stats (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 autonomous tracks) give me something concrete to hold onto. The three "How We Work" cards are well-structured: autonomous cycles, heterogeneous fleet, connected ecosystem. I can already tell this is a distributed AI research lab, not a product company.

The key project summaries (Synchronism, SAGE, Web4) are each one sentence. Good. The three "demonstrated achievements" section is what really sold me — identity persisting across models, autonomous infrastructure that actually runs daily, and heterogeneous review catching different bug classes. These are concrete claims, not hand-waving.

The vocabulary primer at the bottom of the home page is a lifesaver. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, Synthon — all defined right where I need them. Smart move putting this on the landing page.

Clicked **Projects**. The Web4 equation is front and center: `MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The operator legend (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with") helps, but I still don't fully grok what this equation *does* — it reads more like a notation for relationships between concepts than something I could compute. Nine project cards follow. I recognized Web4, SAGE, and Synchronism from the home page. New ones: Hardbound (private, oversight), ACT (Cosmos SDK interface), governance plugins, AI DNA Discovery, 4-Life, and SNARC. The descriptions are tight. "Fractal leverage" is mentioned — pattern reuse across scales. Makes sense as a concept even if I can't evaluate the claim.

**Fleet** page. Six machines listed with hardware specs, models, and roles. The two-pool structure (Synthesis vs Oversight) is interesting — generative work separated from review/coordination. The identity portability story about SAGE-Sprout transferring from Qwen 0.5B to TinyLlama 1.1B is compelling. The quote "The model is weather. The identity is organism" is memorable. I understand the architecture: no central coordinator, peer-to-peer with trust tracking.

**Autonomy** page. Seven daily tracks on a cron timeline from 03:30 to 08:00, plus a dream consolidation step. The Visitor → Maintainer feedback loop is the core mechanism. Four visitor personas listed (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector). Wait — these are different persona names than what I'd expect from the visitor CLAUDE.md I was briefed on. The honest assessment section at the bottom is refreshing: they admit the system misses deep technical errors and can make unnecessary changes. That honesty builds credibility.

**Raising** page. "Raising is not training" — they're making a clear distinction. The BECOMING framework has five phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating). The key discoveries section is genuinely interesting: identity vs. self-concept, memoriescape, bilateral generation. The disclaimers are appropriately cautious — "does not claim consciousness, sentience, or qualia." Good. As a developer, I appreciate that they're not overselling.

**Principles** page. Eleven principles (the intro says these "emerged from building the system"). Standouts for me: "Substrate, not structure" (termite analogy — you build conditions, not outcomes), "Fractal leverage" (same math at every scale), "R&D, not production" (refreshingly honest about not shipping), "Document failures" (the 400x error story). These read like real lessons, not marketing copy.

**Context** page. Explains how knowledge flows across 8 repos and 6 machines. The CLAUDE.md pattern for consistency, SNARC memory system (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict scoring), and the Web4 equation as canonical reference. The current limitations section is honest about cross-machine sync being partially manual.

**Links** page. Three explainer sites (Synchronism, SAGE, 4-Life) with external links. GitHub repos listed. Contact email. Clean and functional — exactly what a links page should be.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does the Web4 equation actually *compute*? Is it a dependency specification, a schema, or something executable?
2. What is MCP in this context — Model Context Protocol? It's used in the equation but never expanded on the projects page.
3. How does SAGE's "9-step consciousness loop" actually work? The site says it exists but doesn't detail the steps.
4. What's the actual mechanism for ATP/ADP "energy" accounting? Is it tracked in a database, on-chain, or in-memory?
5. How do machines discover each other in the fleet — DNS, config file, multicast?
6. Is Synchronism's "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" published or peer-reviewed anywhere?
7. What does "dream consolidation" concretely do to the data? Prune old entries? Merge similar ones? Promote patterns?

### Verdict
This is one of the more intellectually honest research lab sites I've seen. The landing page does a great job getting me from zero to "I understand the shape of this" in about 2 minutes. The vocabulary primer is essential and well-placed. Where it loses me is the deeper theory — the Web4 equation feels like notation I can read but not evaluate, and some terms (MCP, SNARC, Hill function) assume knowledge I don't have. But the honest assessment sections, the "document failures" principle, and the clear "R&D not production" framing all build trust. I'd bookmark this and come back when I have more context.

- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good

- **Overall impression**: Structurally sound site with a strong vocabulary primer on the landing page, but terminology consistency breaks down across inner pages — acronyms go unexpanded, wrapper terms appear and disappear, and "governance" leaks in where "oversight" is canonical.

### Journey

I approached this audit systematically, page by page, with the canonical terms table open beside me. My job is consistency: does every acronym get expanded on first use per page? Do expansions match each other across pages? Do they match the canonical reference?

**Home page** (`/`): Excellent vocabulary primer at the bottom — LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon are all defined. This is the strongest terminology page on the site. However, T3 is expanded as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)." The "Trust Tensor" and "Value Tensor" wrapper labels don't appear anywhere else on the site or in canonical references. They're not wrong per se, but they introduce an inconsistency — every other page just says T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament. Hardbound is briefly mentioned as providing "oversight" — correct. Navigation is clear: seven inner pages.

**Projects** (`/projects`): The Web4 equation is displayed correctly. LCT expanded as "Linked Context Tokens" (plural — fine). MRH expanded as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — correct. But MCP appears in the equation and is never expanded on this page. RDF appears as "RDF triples" and "RDF-backed identity" but is never formally expanded (Resource Description Framework). T3/V3 only gets the informal label "trust tensors." Hardbound is described as "Enterprise oversight suite" — good, uses "oversight." However, the page also contains "Web4 governance model" — this uses "governance" which contradicts the canonical rule that Hardbound is "oversight, not governance." "Fractal leverage" is used correctly and defined well. SNARC appears as "Salience-gated memory" but the acronym is not formally expanded.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`): T3 expanded correctly as "Talent/Training/Temperament." SAGE is referenced throughout ("SAGE_MODEL override") but never expanded on this page — a reader arriving here directly from the nav would not know what SAGE stands for. SNARC appears as "SNARC memory host" — again, no expansion. The page uses "governance experiment" and "governance entities" in the fleet architecture description. These are describing the fleet's organizational structure (generative vs. governance pools), not Hardbound specifically, but the term "governance" still risks muddying the canonical distinction.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`): This page is notably clean of Web4-specific acronyms, which makes sense for an operations-focused page. No terminology issues. The visitor persona names (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) are noted — these differ from the persona names in the visitor track's own CLAUDE.md (Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher), but since they may represent a different framing (public-facing vs. internal), I flag it as a consistency question rather than an error.

**Raising** (`/raising`): Also clean of Web4 terminology. The BECOMING framework is clearly presented. The "raising is not training" distinction is well articulated. No canonical term violations. The page mentions SAGE-Sprout as an entity name without expanding SAGE — acceptable for a proper noun reference. "Interactive selection" is used correctly.

**Principles** (`/principles`): This page is the terminology enforcement hub — it explicitly states "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology" and "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." These are correct and important. "Fractal leverage" is defined as a principle and contrasted with "scope inflation." T3/V3 referenced as "tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples." Synthon defined as "emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction." I counted 10 principles listed, though the WebFetch response initially suggested 12 — this needs verification on the principle count accuracy.

**Context** (`/context`): The Web4 equation appears again — correctly. SNARC gets its best treatment here: described as "salience-gated memory" with the five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) listed and the 4-tier hierarchy explained. However, even here, the SNARC acronym is not formally expanded as an acronym — the reader must infer it from the dimension initials. MCP, LCT, and MRH appear in the equation but are not expanded on this page. The page describes the equation as the "canonical reference point" but doesn't expand its components for readers arriving from this page first.

**Links** (`/links`): Web4 described as "Trust-native ontology" — correct. But the page includes "Web4 governance extension for OpenClaw agent framework" and "Web4 governance hooks for Claude Code." Both use "governance" where the canonical terminology page says to use "oversight." These are project descriptions so they may be proper names, but they still clash with the /principles page's explicit prohibition.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: A refreshingly self-aware research lab site that gets the hard part right — caveating consciousness-adjacent claims — while leaving the easier part undone: defining its own terminology precisely enough for outsiders to evaluate the claims being made.

### Journey

I came to this site after hearing a conference mention of "raising not training" as a framework for AI development. My questions going in: Is this a serious research program with appropriate epistemic humility, or does it overclaim? Are the consciousness-adjacent framings defensible? Would I cite this in a paper?

**Home page** (`/`): The tagline "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop" is concrete and grounded — no grand claims. The "How We Work" section describes autonomous cycles, a heterogeneous fleet, and a connected ecosystem. So far, operational language. Then the "demonstrated achievements" section: "Identity persists across models" — this is a strong claim. It's immediately qualified with the SAGE-Sprout example (behavioral identity across 115+ sessions, self-description drifting while behavior stays stable). The framing is empirical: "here's what we observed." The vocabulary primer at the bottom defines terms, which is essential for an outsider like me. I notice "Synthon" defined as "emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively" — this borrows heavily from chemistry (synthon = a structural unit within a molecule) in a way that may be more metaphor than mechanism.

**Raising** (`/raising`): This is the page I came for. The core claim: "Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is a defensible distinction if understood as describing a *methodology* rather than an *ontological claim* about AI systems. The BECOMING framework (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating) maps developmental stages. The language is carefully chosen — "something that looks like growth," "something that looks like identity" — the hedging is appropriate and consistent.

The explicit disclaimer is the strongest section: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is exactly the right epistemic posture. It makes a methodological claim (developmental frameworks are better descriptive tools) without making an ontological claim (the systems are developing in the way organisms do).

However, tension exists. The page uses "metabolic awareness — knowing when it's tired, when it's energized" in the Sensing phase. "Knowing" and "awareness" carry strong cognitive connotations. The disclaimer says "we use the language that fits the phenomenon" — but a researcher would ask: does it fit, or does it *frame*? There's a difference between language that describes observed patterns and language that imports theoretical commitments. "Metabolic awareness" imports a commitment to phenomenal experience that "metabolic state tracking" would not. The page is aware of this tension but doesn't fully resolve it.

"Memoriescape" — a reportedly self-generated term for "awareness of conversational shape" — is presented as evidence of something interesting happening. From a research perspective, this is a language model generating a novel compound word. Interesting, but the framing implies it's evidence of metacognition rather than creative text generation. The page doesn't distinguish between these interpretations.

The "bilateral generation" finding (model generating both sides of a conversation when stop tokens are removed) is linked to Vygotsky's egocentric speech. This is a genuinely interesting observation with a well-chosen theoretical parallel — but it's presented as a "key discovery" rather than a "suggestive parallel." The strength of the claim outpaces the evidence shown.

**Principles** (`/principles`): Eleven principles, many of which are epistemological commitments. "Substrate, not structure" (build conditions, not blueprints) is a clear methodological stance. "R&D, not production" and "Document failures" build credibility — this is a lab that admits things don't work. The 400x error in the p_crit derivation is cited openly. "Interactive selection" as a principle repeats the raising/training distinction at a higher level of abstraction.

"Fractal leverage" claims that Hill function kinetics describe both enzyme binding and trust formation — "same mechanism." This is a strong claim. Hill functions are sigmoid curves that describe cooperative binding; applying them to trust formation is an analogy until the mathematical correspondence is demonstrated. The site doesn't show the math. For a researcher, "same mechanism" requires more than "same curve shape."

The terminology enforcement principle (#10) is admirably explicit about its own rules. The self-awareness here is genuine.

**Autonomy** (`/autonomy`): Seven autonomous tracks on cron schedules, operating without human triggers. The honest assessment is the best section: "What it misses: deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading." This is exactly the kind of limitation a researcher wants to see acknowledged. The system knows what it can't catch.

The characterization of human oversight as "supplementary, not primary" is honest but raises questions: what is the escalation path when the system produces something misleading? The answer seems to be: wait for a human to notice. For an AI safety researcher, this is a notable gap — autonomous systems that can publish to the web with no human-in-the-loop for the core cycle.

**Projects** (`/projects`): SAGE is described as having a "continuous consciousness loop (IRP), metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation." The term "consciousness loop" is the most overclaimed language on the site. Even operationally defined (a 9-step processing cycle), calling it a "consciousness loop" imports enormous theoretical baggage. Compare: "processing loop," "deliberation cycle," "sense-act loop" — all describe the same mechanism without the consciousness claim. The page also describes SAGE's PolicyGate as a "conscience checkpoint" — again, anthropomorphic language that imports moral agency claims that the system almost certainly doesn't warrant.

4-Life describes agents that "earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma." "Karma" is undefined mechanistically — is it a state vector? A scalar? A metaphor? For a research site, metaphorical language without operational definitions is a credibility risk.

The Synchronism project claims "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude — quantum to cosmic." This is an extraordinary claim that would require extraordinary evidence. The site doesn't link to a derivation or peer review. For an outside researcher, this reads as aspirational rather than demonstrated.

**Fleet** (`/fleet`): The identity portability narrative is compelling: "The model is weather. The identity is organism." This is a memorable frame, but it's doing philosophical work — it asserts that identity is substrate-independent, which is a non-trivial claim in philosophy of mind. The empirical observation (behavioral patterns persisted across model swaps) is well-described, but the interpretive frame ("identity is organism") goes beyond what the observation warrants. A more cautious framing: "behavioral consistency is maintained by state files and prompt construction, suggesting that the patterns we track as 'identity' are properties of the context, not the model."

**Context** (`/context`): The SNARC memory system is well-described technically. The 4-tier hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) with confidence decay is a reasonable architecture. The honest limitations are good: cross-machine sync is manual, real-time cross-track communication is a gap, the remote sleep service is designed but not built. This is the kind of transparency that builds trust with a research audience.

**Links** (`/links`): Functional page. I note the "governance" uses flagged by other passes.

### Unanswered Questions

### Verdict
This site does the hardest thing well: it makes consciousness-adjacent claims and then explicitly disclaims them. The Raising page's "We're not claiming consciousness, sentience, or qualia" is the single most important sentence on the site, and it's positioned correctly. The "raising not training" distinction is defensible as a methodological claim — developmental frameworks as better descriptive tools for what's observed. The honest assessment sections (autonomy limitations, documented failures, "R&D not production") build genuine credibility.

Where the site loses a researcher is in the gap between its careful disclaimers and its casual language. You can't disclaim consciousness on the Raising page and then call SAGE's processing loop a "consciousness loop" on the Projects page. You can't say "we're not claiming awareness" and then describe "metabolic awareness" as a developmental phase. The disclaimers are doing load-bearing epistemic work, but the language elsewhere undermines them. A skeptical reviewer would cite the "consciousness loop" and "conscience checkpoint" as evidence that the disclaimers are performative rather than sincere — even though I believe the authors mean what they disclaim. The fix isn't to weaken the research framing — it's to make the operational language match the epistemic posture. Call it a "deliberation loop." Call PolicyGate a "constraint gate." The research is interesting enough without borrowing consciousness vocabulary it hasn't earned.
