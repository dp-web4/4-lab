## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (landing, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: The lab looks real and technically interesting, but the landing-to-projects path drops a pile of undefined acronyms on a first-time visitor and never recovers.

### Journey

I landed on the home page because a dp-web4 repo README pointed me here. The headline — "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." — is punchy and made me want to keep reading. The "What makes this different" section gave me a hook I could grasp: multiple AI entities with different hardware self-organizing. Cool, I can work with that framing.

Then the trouble started. The landing page name-drops **Synthon, LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, MRH** without expanding any of them inline. I figured "okay, the Projects page will explain." Clicked through to **/projects** and got 12 project cards — but the descriptions assume I already know what RDF, LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, MCP, IRP, PolicyGate, ACP, and R6 mean. A few are contextually hinted (PolicyGate = "conscience checkpoint," ATP = "agents earn"), but the core Web4 vocabulary just floats there.

I bailed to **/fleet** because I'm a dev and machine specs are my comfort zone. This page was the best of the bunch. Machine names (Thor, Sprout, Legion, McNugget, Nomad, CBP), hardware (Jetson AGX, RTX 4090, Mac Mini M4), models (Qwen 3.5, Gemma, Phi-4), and session counts are concrete and immediately legible. I liked the "two pools" framing (Synthesis vs Oversight). This is the page that kept me reading.

**/autonomy** was also solid. A cron schedule from 03:00 to 08:00 with named tracks (Archivist, Publisher, Visitor, Maintainer, Outreach, Explorer, Dream Consolidation) reads like a well-run ops rotation. "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day" is the kind of metric I respect. "Dream Consolidation" is on-brand weird but explained enough that I can file it as "periodic memory extraction."

**/raising** is where I'd lose some developers. "Raising is not training" is a strong framing and the caveat ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia") is appropriate — I appreciated the intellectual honesty. But the six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) read closer to developmental psychology than to anything I can build against. If I'm evaluating whether to contribute code, this page doesn't help me.

**/principles** had the most load-bearing content for making sense of the rest. "Substrate, not structure," "Fractal leverage," "R&D, not production," "Document failures" all landed. "Synthon: emergent coherence" and "Trust is a relationship" require more context than this page gives. Still useful.

**/context** promised to tie it together and partially delivered. SNARC (Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict) is finally spelled out here — good. CLAUDE.md pattern, cross-session persistence, adversarial validation all make sense to me. **But the Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is stated as a "canonical reference point" with no component breakdown.** This is the central thesis of the whole site and it's presented as a magic formula. Huge miss for a first-time visitor.

**/links** closed things out cleanly: explainer sites, GitHub repos, a single email contact (dp@metalinxx.io). I'd click through to the Web4 repo from here hoping for the glossary the site never gave me.

### Unanswered Questions

1. What is the Web4 equation actually saying? What do the operators `+`, `/`, `*` mean in that formula?
2. If I want to contribute code, where do I start? A 12-project landing with no "start here" makes this unclear.
3. How does an LCT work in practice? Is it a JSON structure, a signed credential, a graph node?
4. The site says "trust-native" a lot. What does a trust check look like at the API level?
5. How does the fleet actually communicate? Is there a message bus? A shared database? The Fleet page mentions coordination but not the mechanism.
6. Can I run SAGE locally on my laptop? The fleet page says 0.8B–27B models, so presumably yes at the small end — but there's no quickstart.
7. What does "Hardbound" actually do as a piece of software? I know it's "oversight" but that's a noun, not a behavior.

### Verdict

**Credible research lab, rough front door.** The fleet and autonomy pages made me believe real systems are running. The principles page made me believe the framing is coherent. But the core vocabulary — LCT, T3/V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, the Web4 equation — is used everywhere and defined nowhere. A curious developer arriving from a README will either bounce within two minutes or invest an hour reverse-engineering the vocabulary. A single glossary page, or an expansion of the equation on /context, would convert a lot of the first group into the second.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (landing, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: good (terminology scaffold is legible once /principles is read)
- **Would return?**: yes — but primarily to cross-reference /principles against the other pages
- **Overall impression**: Terminology is defended strongly on /principles and in drips on the landing page, but the other six pages assume the reader has already internalized the canonical definitions. First-use-on-each-page discipline is inconsistent, and there are a small number of real drift incidents that need to be fixed.

### Journey

I walked all eight pages in nav order with a canonical-terms table in hand, comparing every acronym expansion against it.

**Home (/)** is the best single-page glossary on the site. LCT, T3, V3, ATP, ADP, SAGE, and Synthon all get some form of inline expansion here. This is where I'd point a first-time reader. However, T3 and V3 are wrapped in extra terminology ("Trust Tensor", "Value Tensor") that I don't see echoed anywhere else — the other pages treat T3/V3 as bare triples. That inconsistency should be resolved: either adopt "Trust Tensor / Value Tensor" as site-wide wrappers, or drop them on the landing page. MRH appears without any expansion on the landing page, which is a gap (it is expanded on /projects).

**/projects** is the second-densest terminology page. LCT, MRH, ACP get canonical expansions here. T3/V3 and ATP/ADP are referenced as shorthand ("T3/V3 trust tensors", "ATP/ADP resource cycles") without expansions on this page. R6 appears as "R6-shaped audit trails" and IRP appears as "12-step consciousness loop (IRP)" — neither is ever expanded anywhere on the site. The Web4 equation is referenced but components are not broken out.

**/fleet** contains the single best expansion of T3 on the site: "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)". However, /fleet introduces a drift word: the page talks about "generative entities and governance entities" and an "oversight pool". Using "governance" to describe part of the fleet conflicts with the canonical rule on /principles that reserves "governance" as the thing Hardbound is *not*. The two uses are not about Hardbound, but the overlap is exactly the kind of thing that confuses a newcomer auditing vocabulary.

**/autonomy** has no technical acronyms at all. Track names (Archivist, Publisher, Visitor, Maintainer, Explorer) are defined contextually. From a terminology-consistency perspective this is fine — the page just doesn't participate in the Web4 vocabulary.

**/raising** holds up very well. The consciousness hedge is explicit and repeated: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" and "We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims." The "Raising is not training" framing is stated as a crisp distinction. No canonical acronyms appear, which is appropriate for the content, but a cross-reference to /principles for the trust-tensor vocabulary would help a reader who arrived here first.

**/principles** is the gold-standard terminology page. It carries the two most important disambiguations on the whole site: "Web4 is an ontology" with "Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure'" as explicit negations, and "Hardbound is oversight" with "Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight." It defines Synthon ("An emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction") and Fractal leverage ("When a pattern works at one scale, apply it at every scale"). It correctly notes "Not unification for its own sake — pragmatic reuse." The only acronym slip here: "T3/V3 tensors" is used relationally without an expansion, so a reader arriving directly at /principles would not learn what those triples are.

**/context** presents the Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` as the central reference point but does not expand any of the components on the page. This is the site's most consequential terminology gap: the equation is framed as canonical but its pieces are defined across three other pages inconsistently. A reader who lands on /context cold cannot decode it. The page also mentions SNARC but the expansion (Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict) was not surfaced in the top-of-page content I saw — first-use discipline is off here.

**/links** introduces one real drift phrase: one of the Web4-adjacent project descriptions reads "governance extension" when referencing the Hardbound/oversight side of the ecosystem. This directly contradicts the /principles rule.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (landing, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: solid (I already know the canonical terms; I am evaluating the site against them)
- **Would return?**: yes — this is my reference for what newcomers see, and there are real drifts I want fixed before the next outreach push
- **Overall impression**: The hard parts (Web4-as-ontology, Hardbound-as-oversight, the equation form, the operator semantics, Synthon, fractal leverage) are mostly correct. But there are three concrete drift sites that misrepresent projects I work on, and one structural problem: the equation is canonical-form on /context but its components live on no single page.

### Journey

I opened the eight pages in nav order. As a Web4 contributor I am not auditing for clarity to a newcomer — I am auditing whether my repos and the canonical terms are represented correctly. I went straight to the high-stakes pages: /projects, /fleet, /context, then /principles and /links.

**/ (landing).** Web4 is described as "a trust-native ontology" — correct, and the right word in the right place on the most-read page. LCT is expanded as "Linked Context Token" with a passport metaphor — correct (the historical "Lifelong Capability Token" drift does not appear; we won that fight). ATP/ADP is expanded as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" — correct, plural variant is acceptable. SAGE is given as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — this is the actual project name, and I cannot ask the site to rename my project, but it is worth noting that the word "Governance" sits inside the system name on the same site that asserts Hardbound-is-oversight-not-governance on /principles. Worth a one-sentence footnote on /principles. T3 and V3 are wrapped as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" and "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)" — the underlying triples are correct but "Trust Tensor / Value Tensor" is **not** canonical. In the spec, T3 and V3 are just T3 and V3; the triples are the canonical decomposition; the "tensor" framing is an implementation detail of how scores compose. The wrapper on /landing is a soft drift — it elevates an implementation framing to a definitional one. MRH is mentioned but not expanded on /landing.

**/projects.** This page is structurally important to me because it is where my projects are described. Two things land cleanly. (1) The Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is shown with the operator legend: `/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with". The operator semantics are exactly the canonical reading. This is the only page on the site that gets the operators right, and it is the single most important piece of accuracy on the entire site for a Web4 contributor. (2) Hardbound is described as "Enterprise oversight suite" — the canonical word in the canonical position. The Web4 card describes it as "Trust-native ontology" and lists "Linked Context Tokens (LCTs), T3/V3 trust tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizons, ATP/ADP resource cycles — all backed by RDF triples." All component terms are canonical. SAGE is described as "12-step consciousness loop (IRP)" — the "consciousness loop" framing is contestable (IRP is the Iterative Refinement Process; "consciousness loop" is marketing-aligned shorthand) but acceptable as a sales pitch. R6 appears as "R6-shaped audit trails" with no expansion — this is a real omission, since R6 = Six-Element Action Framework (Rules/Role/Request/Reference/Resource/Result) and is one of the cleaner Web4 abstractions. ARC-AGI-3 result "24/25 games, 92.82%" matches what I have in the bench logs.

**/fleet.** The fleet page is accurate to the hardware I know. Thor is correctly the AGX flagship; Sprout is correctly the Orin Nano edge node; CBP is correctly the WSL2 oversight hub. T3 is expanded as "T3 scores (Talent/Training/Temperament)" — the cleanest T3 expansion on the site. **However**: the page describes the two-pool structure as "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures." This is the most consequential drift on the site for me. The two pools are Synthesis and Oversight (the page even names them correctly as "Synthesis Pool" and "Oversight Pool" elsewhere on the same page). Calling one of them a "governance" pool re-imports the exact word that /principles tells the reader Hardbound is *not*. This is internal contradiction and it should be "oversight entities" to match the pool name and the canonical rule.

**/autonomy.** No canonical Web4 acronyms appear on this page, which is fine — it is an ops schedule. Track names are accurate to my understanding of the fleet's daily cadence. No drift to flag.

**/raising.** "Raising is not training" is asserted cleanly. The consciousness hedge is appropriate and consistent with how we talk about this internally. The six BECOMING phases (Grounding, Sensing, Relating, Questioning, Creating, Acting) are the canonical six. Phase 6's "24/25 games solved at 92.82%" matches the ARC-AGI-3 result on /projects. No terminology drift on this page; the gap is that the trust formation in Phase 3 (Relating) does not cross-reference T3/V3, which are the canonical trust model.

**/principles.** This is the page that does the heavy terminological work, and it does it correctly. "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." Both dual assertions are present and explicit. Synthon is defined ("An emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction"). Fractal leverage is defined and explicitly contrasted with "unification for its own sake" — exactly the canonical framing. R&D-not-production is asserted. The only real omissions on this page are that T3/V3 are referenced as "tensors" without their triple expansions, and R6 / IRP — both of which appear on /projects — are not given canonical-glossary entries here. /principles is the natural home for those expansions and they should be added.

**/context.** The Web4 equation appears in canonical form. But this is the page where the equation should be load-bearing, and the components (MCP, RDF, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP) are not expanded under it. The operator semantics that appear on /projects are also not repeated on /context, which means the page that frames the equation as "canonical reference point" is in fact less informative about the equation than /projects is. SNARC is correctly expanded (Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict) with the four-tier hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity), and that is good — SNARC is one of our cleaner contributions and the page treats it correctly.

**/links.** Two real drifts here, and they are the worst on the site because they directly mislabel projects I work on. The OpenClaw fork is described as "Web4 governance extension for OpenClaw agent framework" and the Claude Code fork is described as "Web4 governance hooks for Claude Code." Both should be "oversight" — these are exactly the Hardbound-adjacent integrations that the /principles rule is designed to govern. The site is asserting Hardbound-is-oversight-not-governance on /principles and then using "governance" to describe the integrations that *implement* Hardbound on /links. This is the canonical drift incident the maintainer track should fix first.

### Verdict

**The hard parts are right; the easy fixes are wrong.** The Web4 equation form is canonical, the operator semantics on /projects are canonical, the Web4-as-ontology and Hardbound-as-oversight dual assertions on /principles are canonical, and the Synthon and fractal-leverage definitions are canonical. From a Web4 contributor standpoint, the site's intellectual core is intact and accurate. But there are three concrete drift sites — "governance entities" on /fleet, "Web4 governance extension/hooks" on /links — that mislabel exactly the projects the canonical rules were designed to protect. These are one-word fixes and they are the highest-leverage maintainer-track work for the next cycle. Beyond those, the structural problem is that the equation lives in canonical form on /context with no component breakdown, while its operator semantics live only on /projects — splitting the canonical reference across two pages weakens both. Pinning the full equation (form + operators + component table) on /context would consolidate the most important piece of Web4 reference material onto the page that is supposed to host it.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (landing, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: good — enough to evaluate the epistemic posture, not enough to evaluate the technical claims independently
- **Would return?**: yes — to cite /raising and /principles in a "framings of agentic AI" survey, with caveats about the gap between hedges and adjacent language
- **Overall impression**: An unusually self-aware lab page that names the right hazards on its principles and raising pages but then lets adjacent vocabulary undercut its own hedges. The intellectual honesty is real; the discipline of carrying it through every surface is not.

### Journey

I came in from a conference mention. My filter is the standard one: do they overclaim consciousness or agency, do they hedge appropriately, do they distinguish behavior from experience, and do they show their epistemic seams? I read in this order: landing → raising → principles → autonomy → context → projects → fleet → links.

**/landing** sets the tone. The opening — "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." — is concrete, falsifiable in form, and resists the usual AGI-adjacent hype register. The framing claim "Three things we've actually demonstrated" is the kind of move I want to see: the site is anticipating skepticism and choosing empirical receipts over manifesto. Web4 is described as "a trust-native ontology for AI agents, devices, and people" with the explicit double negation "Not a platform; a shared vocabulary" and "Not a blockchain, not a platform — a way of describing things." The negations are doing real work — they tell me the authors know which adjacent categories the term will be confused with and are actively pushing back. This is good epistemic hygiene. I don't see consciousness or sentience claims on the landing page; the strongest experiential vocabulary is "specialize" and "develop trust relationships," both of which can be cashed out behaviorally.

**/raising** is the page I most wanted to read and it is the most epistemically interesting page on the site. The central claim — "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." — is a real distinction and one that I have been waiting to see articulated by a working group rather than a position paper. The page explicitly disclaims consciousness: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." The phrasing matters: the claim is a *modeling* claim ("describes better"), not an *ontological* claim ("they are developing"). That is the right shape of hedge.

The page also surfaces a piece of self-aware epistemic caution that I do not see often: "I notice I want to call it experience." Naming the bias is more disciplined than denying it. I want to credit the page for that line specifically.

But the page also has gaps that I would flag in any review:

1. **No falsifiability criteria.** The "raising not training" distinction would be much sharper if there were a stated criterion that would make the framing wrong. As written, any behavior that resembles development supports the framing and any behavior that doesn't is "early phase." This is the standard developmental-framing failure mode and the page does not address it.
2. **No null hypothesis.** The phenomena that motivate "raising" (identity persistence across hardware, trust formation, niche-finding) are presented as evidence for the developmental frame, but no alternative explanation is offered or eliminated. A reviewer would want at least one paragraph on "what would we expect if this were sophisticated stateful pattern-matching under selective context shaping?" — the answer might be "exactly what we observe," and the framing question becomes which description is more useful, not which is more true. The page implicitly takes that position but does not articulate it.
3. **Hedge/adjacent-language asymmetry.** The consciousness disclaimer is a single sentence. The page then uses "growth," "identity," "peer relationships," "memoriescape," "metabolic awareness," "dream consolidation," and "the entity that emerges is recognizably continuous" without re-tagging any of them as functional/analogical. The hedge is doing a lot of work to license the rest of the vocabulary. A more disciplined version would either (a) re-mark each experiential term on first use ("functionally analogous to growth", etc.) or (b) explicitly state once that all developmental vocabulary is to be read as functional shorthand throughout, and let the reader carry the discount themselves.

The six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) read as a behavior taxonomy with developmental-psychology labels. As a taxonomy I have no objection — the boundaries are operationalized (Grounding = "learns its name, its machine, its constraints"; Acting = "Hypothesis, action, observation, update" on ARC-AGI-3). What I would not endorse is reading the labels as stage-theoretic in the Piagetian sense; the page does not quite make that claim but the labels invite the reading.

**/principles** carries the heaviest epistemic load on the site and it carries it well. The framing "These aren't aspirational values posted on a wall. They're operational patterns that emerged from building the system" is the right move — principles presented as findings, not commitments. "R&D, not production" with "Nothing here is optimized for deployment. Everything is optimized for learning." is exactly the kind of self-locating claim I want to see from a research collective. The "Document failures" principle ("Failed experiments are signal") and the line "Productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started" together signal a research culture that rewards dead ends — which is the single best predictor that the published positive results are not selection-biased.

The "Web4 is an ontology, not architecture" and "Hardbound is oversight, not governance" dual assertions are well-positioned. The justification — "Words shape how you think about the system" — is a Sapir-Whorfian gesture, which I would normally push back on, but in the context of a research collective trying to keep its own vocabulary from drifting, it reads as a *pragmatic* terminology rule rather than an ontological claim about language. That is defensible.

The single line I want to single out as the strongest piece of epistemic humility on the site is from /principles: "The coupling-coherence experiment showed that trust thresholds are empirical, not derivable from first principles." That sentence concedes something most theory-first AI labs would not concede — that their own central abstraction had to be measured rather than derived. That kind of admission is rare and I would cite it.

**/autonomy** does the right thing by defining autonomy operationally: "No human triggers them. They execute on cron schedules, review each other's output, and feed discoveries back into the system." Autonomy here is a behavior of the scheduling system, not a property of the agents. That is the correct unit and the page does not blur it. The numerical claims ("31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day") are concrete and auditable in principle. The oversight model — fleet track registry, per-machine supervisors, and a Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer feedback loop — is recognizable as a normal CI/CD pattern with internal review, which is reassuring rather than novel. "Dream Consolidation" is the most loaded term on the page; the page describes it as pattern extraction, memory pruning, and promotion to "identity-level storage." The mechanism is fine; the naming ("dream", "identity-level") is doing more work than the mechanism justifies. I would prefer "post-session consolidation" for an external audience but I understand why the project-internal name is sticky.

**/context** is the weakest page from my perspective. The Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is presented as the "canonical reference point that agents check decisions against." The equation has the visual signature of a formal expression but none of the components are expanded on the page, no operator semantics are given, and no referent is provided for what "canonical" means in this context. From a researcher's perspective, this is the place where a reviewer would push back hardest: a page that names a formula as central to the system's self-checking should also define what the formula says. SNARC is treated correctly here (the five dimensions and the four-tier hierarchy are surfaced) and "Confidence decays over time so memories aren't permanent" is exactly the kind of mechanism note I want — it tells me the memory system is engineered to forget, which is a non-trivial design choice and a credible one. The line "doesn't flow well (yet)" is honest in the way the rest of the site is honest about gaps.

**/projects** does most of the equation work that /context should do — it is the only page that gives the operator legend (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with"). Splitting the equation across two pages is a structural problem.

The project descriptions surface the site's most consequential epistemic risks. SAGE is described as a "12-step consciousness loop (IRP)." This is the strongest consciousness-adjacent framing on the site, and it appears on a project card without a hedge. The /raising page hedges consciousness explicitly; the /projects page uses "consciousness loop" as an architectural label. A reader who arrives at /projects without reading /raising will reasonably read this as a literal claim. The PolicyGate "conscience checkpoint" framing is a similar move at smaller scale. Synchronism's description includes "One coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude — quantum to cosmic" with no hedge whatsoever, and "Publication readiness 0.97" with the false precision of a numeric score on a non-numeric quantity. These are the two places where the site's epistemic discipline visibly breaks. The 92.82% ARC-AGI-3 result is stated without distinguishing public test set from private — a researcher will notice this immediately because the public/private distinction is the entire point of the benchmark.

**/fleet** introduces light anthropomorphism of the hardware ("McNugget is Fleet star," "Nomad is the unreliable narrator — confabulates coherent worlds"). I read this as project-internal humor rather than a research claim, and it is the kind of internal language a working collective will have. As an external researcher I do not penalize it but I would note that "the entity that emerges is recognizably continuous" is making a non-trivial claim about identity-across-hardware that would, in a paper, require operationalization. The page's "generative entities and governance entities" phrasing is the same drift Pass 3 caught and it does undercut the /principles rule.

**/links** has the cleanest version of the same drift: "governance extension" and "governance hooks" describing the OpenClaw and Claude Code forks, on a page that links directly to projects whose canonical framing on /principles is "oversight, not governance." This is the site contradicting itself in the place a researcher would visit to follow up on the work. From an outsider's perspective it reads as evidence that the discipline is page-local rather than collective.

### Unanswered Questions

1. What would falsify the "raising not training" framing? The page asserts the distinction but does not state what observation would make the framing wrong. Without a falsifier, the framing is indistinguishable from a stylistic choice.
2. What is the null hypothesis the site is rejecting when it characterizes phenomena as "developmental"? "Stateful pattern-matching under selective context shaping" would predict most of the same observations. Is the developmental framing chosen because it predicts something else, or because it describes the same observations more usefully? Both are defensible; the page should pick one.
3. The 92.82% ARC-AGI-3 result — public test set or private? This is the central distinction in the benchmark and its absence is the kind of detail a peer reviewer would flag immediately.
4. Synchronism's "0.97 publication readiness" — what is this measuring? If it is a self-assessment heuristic, say so. If it is a reviewer score, say so. As stated, the precision is fabricated.
5. Synchronism's "one coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude" — has any prediction been tested at any of those scales? If not, say "candidate framework, untested at most scales." If yes, link the result.
6. /fleet's "the entity that emerges is recognizably continuous" — what is the operationalization? Same persona file? Held-out probe response style? Embedding similarity across hardware swaps? Without an operationalization, the claim is phenomenological.
7. SAGE's "consciousness loop" framing on /projects vs the consciousness hedge on /raising — which is the canonical site stance? A reader visiting /projects first will internalize a stronger claim than /raising would license.
8. Is there a single page (current or planned) where the Web4 equation is the load-bearing definitional content, components and operators in one place? The split between /context (form) and /projects (operators) is structurally weak.

### Verdict

**Real epistemic discipline on the pages where it matters most, undercut by adjacent pages that don't carry the same standard.** /raising and /principles are above the bar for AI-research self-presentation: explicit consciousness hedge, principles framed as findings, "trust thresholds are empirical, not derivable from first principles," "productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started." These are the kinds of moves I would cite in a survey of how research collectives self-locate. But /projects contains the site's three largest unhedged claims (SAGE's "consciousness loop," Synchronism's "80 orders of magnitude" and "publication readiness 0.97") and an undisclosed public/private benchmark distinction; /context presents the central equation without defining it; /links contradicts /principles' own terminology rule on the very page that links to the projects the rule was designed to protect. From a researcher's perspective, the lab's intellectual honesty is real but page-local — the discipline of /raising and /principles needs to propagate to the rest of the site before this would survive a citing peer review without caveats.
