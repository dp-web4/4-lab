## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (home, projects, fleet, autonomy, raising, principles, context, links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: The pitch lands in the first 30 seconds, then dissolves into an acronym soup I can't resolve without leaving the site.

### Journey

I clicked through from a dp-web4 repo README expecting to see "this is the lab, here's what we build, here's where to start." The landing page delivered the 30-second version cleanly: six heterogeneous machines, AI agents self-organizing, "36+ daily tracks run without human intervention," emergent entities called "synthons." As a dev I recognize the shape — distributed systems + LLMs + some novel trust primitives. Interesting. Kept reading.

Scanned the top nav: Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links. No "Get Started" or "Docs" link, which was my first small stumble — I wasn't sure where a dev was supposed to enter. I picked Projects because that's where I always look first.

Projects gave me 13 cards. Some are legible at a glance (ACT = "human interface tool for Web4," ARC-AGI-3 = benchmark competition). Others are opaque without definitions on the page — SAGE, Synchronism, SNARC, Membot, and Hardbound are all named without expansions. I could infer from context, but I shouldn't have to. The page does say the equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` which was my first "wait, what" moment — it looks meaningful but half of those tokens are undefined here.

Fleet was the page I enjoyed most. Concrete hardware, concrete models, clear roles per machine. I can read a table like that. This is where the "heterogeneous" claim becomes tangible — Jetson AGX next to a Mac Mini next to a 4060 laptop is genuinely a diverse rig, not a marketing word. But SAGE is expanded here as "Self-Adaptive Generative Entity," which doesn't match the landing page's "Situation-Aware Governance Engine." That's a red flag for a developer — if the team can't agree what their own acronym means, either the project is drifting or the site is stale.

Autonomy delivered a clean timeline (03:00 → 08:00+, supervisors → publishers → visitors → maintainers). This was the most satisfying page because it explains the *mechanism* — SQLite registry, predetermined schedules, tracks reviewing each other. As a dev, "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day" is a claim I can hold. I'd want to see a track log or two to believe it, but the shape is plausible.

Raising is where I started to feel like a tourist. The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) read like a developmental-psych framework applied to LLMs. I appreciated the explicit disclaimer — "we're not claiming these entities are conscious" — because my skeptic alarm was primed. The "24/25 games solved at 92.82%" on ARC-AGI-3 is the concrete result that keeps this from feeling purely philosophical.

Principles was surprisingly readable — 10 one-liners, most of which I'd nod along to (substrate not structure, R&D not production, document failures). The "terminology matters" principle is self-aware, which is good, and also ironic given the SAGE mismatch I already hit.

Context page was the densest. It's essentially a meta-description of how 14 repos + CLAUDE.md files + memory systems compose into a knowledge graph. Useful, but this is where every undefined term from earlier came back at once: LCT, MRH, T3, V3, ATP, ADP, MCP, RDF all appear as if pre-understood. I had to hold them loosely and move on.

Links delivered the escape hatch — GitHub repos, explainer sites, contact email. This is where a curious dev actually goes next. I'd click through to Web4 or SAGE repos from here to dig deeper.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Journey

My reference was the canonical terms table I was briefed with: LCT = Linked Context Token, T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament, V3 = Valuation/Veracity/Validity, MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon, ATP = Allocation Transfer Packet, ADP = Allocation Discharge Packet, Web4 is an ontology (not architecture/infrastructure), Hardbound is oversight (not governance).

**Home (`/`)**. First pass terminology footprint is dense: LCT, T3, V3, SAGE, synthon, Web4, ATP, ADP all appear. LCT = "Linked Context Token" — correct. T3 presented as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)" — the expansion is canonical, but "Trust Tensor" is a wrapper label I can't verify against the brief. V3 similarly wrapped as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)". ATP/ADP appear as "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packets" — this IS canonical, good. SAGE expanded as **"Situation-Aware Governance Engine"** — and here the audit stops being clerical and starts being urgent. This expansion contains the exact term the brief flagged as drift: "Governance." On the same page the project also talks about "oversight in action." The site uses both the canonical term (oversight) *and* the flagged term (governance) — and the flagged term is baked into an acronym expansion, the worst place for it to live.

**Projects (`/projects`)**. Useful page — this is where acronyms finally get concentrated expansions. MCP is defined here as "Model Context Protocol" (finally!), RDF as "Resource Description Framework." MRH appears as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" — canonical, plural, fine. But SAGE is **not** expanded on this page — it's called the "On-device cognition kernel." ATP/ADP are glossed as "resource cycles" which is a description, not an expansion, and the canonical packet terms are hidden. The project card labeled "Oversight Plugins" describes itself as the "Web4 oversight model" — correct usage. But inside the Hardbound card, "enterprise oversight with TPM 2.0, FIDO2, Secure Enclave" — correct. Good. One phrase caught me: "Not unification for its own sake" — this is a deliberate reference to the flagged term "unification," used in contrast. Acceptable editorial move, arguably the cleanest example on the site of terminology hygiene done on purpose.

**Fleet (`/fleet`)**. This page is where my audit alarm went off loudest. SAGE on this page is expanded as **"Self-Aware Generative Entity"** — which does not match the home page's "Situation-Aware Governance Engine." Two different expansions for the same three-letter acronym on two adjacent pages of a single site. MRH on this page appears as **"Metabolic Resource Hierarchy"** — this directly contradicts the canonical expansion "Markov Relevancy Horizon" that /projects uses. SNARC gets expanded as "Spatial-Numerical Association of Response Codes" — a real term from cognitive psychology, but the /projects page describes SNARC as "Salience-gated memory for Claude Code" — and the `S` in salience is almost certainly the intended expansion. These are three independent acronym collisions on one page. The neuroscience stack (dlPFC, WM, RPE) does expand correctly: dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, Working Memory, Reward Prediction Error.

**Autonomy (`/autonomy`)**. Numerically clean, terminologically light. The page says "31 autonomous tracks … ~53 sessions per day," but the home page said "36+ daily tracks" — pick one. The bigger issue is persona drift: this page says the visitor track uses four personas named "Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector" — but the actual track I'm executing right now uses "Curious Developer, Technical Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher." The site describes different personas than the ones doing the work. That's documentation drift from reality itself, not just between pages. No governance/oversight/architecture/infrastructure vocabulary appears here — ironically the cleanest page for terminology hygiene, because it mostly avoids the loaded words.

**Raising (`/raising`)**. Clean. BECOMING phases (Grounding, Sensing, Relating, Questioning, Creating, Acting) are consistent and well-named. The "raising is not training" framing is stated once and defended once. Consciousness claims are hedged explicitly. No terminology drift found.

**Principles (`/principles`)**. Reads like a terminology glossary by accident. Principle 10 ("Terminology matters") explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology, Hardbound is oversight" — this is the brief, faithfully rendered, inline. Principle 2 is titled "Fractal leverage" and clarifies "Not unification for its own sake" — again, correct handling. But notice: Web4, SAGE, and T3/V3 are *used* on this page without being *expanded* on this page. The principles page assumes you've already visited projects or home. RDF is expanded here ("Resource Description Framework") — so at least one acronym gets a local definition.

**Context (`/context`)**. This was the single worst page for a first-time reader. The Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` appears here, and **none** of the eight tokens on the right-hand side are expanded on this page. A page literally titled "Context" is the last place that should assume context. The operators `+`, `/`, `*` are also un-explained here, which matters because the brief tells me `/` means "verified by," `*` means "contextualized by," `+` means "augmented with" — non-standard semantics that a reader cannot guess from the typography. If I landed on this page cold, I would bounce.

**Links (`/links`)**. Delivers what it promises. ACT is expanded ("Agentic Context Tool"), ARC as "Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus," RDF as "Resource Description Framework." Two uses of "oversight" both correct ("Web4 oversight extension for OpenClaw," "Web4 oversight hooks for Claude Code"). One line does the whole lab equation a service: "Trust-native ontology — LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF backbone." Compact, accurate, correct canonical terms. This is the sentence that should appear on /context.

### Unanswered Questions

1. Which of the three SAGE expansions is authoritative? "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" (home), "Self-Aware Generative Entity" (fleet), "Self-Adaptive Generative Entity" (fleet, per Pass 1)? Three readings on two pages across two passes implies the page itself may be changing, or the acronym has never been pinned down.

### Verdict

From a tech-writer's perspective this site has a solid spine — consistent nav, clean visual hierarchy, readable prose — but its terminology discipline is already fraying in at least four distinct ways: (1) same acronym, two expansions on adjacent pages (SAGE, arguably SNARC), (2) wrong expansion of a canonical term (MRH on /fleet), (3) the flagged word "Governance" baked into an acronym expansion on the home page, and (4) the flagship equation appearing on /context with no inline definitions of any of its symbols. If the site wants to serve as the newcomer-facing door to Web4, a `/glossary` page with canonical expansions and site-wide first-use hyperlinks to it would pay for itself in a single audit cycle.

### Journey

I came in from a conference mention — someone cited 4-lab as a site running "autonomous agent raising" on a heterogeneous fleet. I was skeptical but curious. My read was focused: Raising, Principles, and Autonomy, with the other pages as supporting context. I was looking for three things that AI safety researchers look for when evaluating work adjacent to our field: (1) are strong claims about agency/cognition caveated appropriately, (2) are the operationalizations of key terms (identity, trust, oversight) rigorous enough to be argued with, (3) does the stated epistemic stance match the practiced rhetorical stance.

**Home (`/`).** The page opens with "autonomous AI cognition" and "36+ daily tracks run without human intervention." Both are stated as fact without epistemic hedges. The sentence "Identity persists across models" and its follow-on "behavioral identity remained continuous" caught my attention — these are big claims. In the safety literature "identity" across model versions is a live open question (is it the weights? the fine-tune? the behavioral signature? the external memory store?) and the site collapses it into a bare assertion. I'd want to see this operationalized: what test was run, what signal was measured, what would have counted as discontinuity. The home page is also where I first meet SAGE, expanded as **"Situation-Aware Governance Engine."** For someone coming from AI safety, the word "governance" is a term of art — it names the policy/oversight/alignment-regulation cluster. If SAGE is actually a cognition kernel (as /projects says), labeling it with "Governance" creates an immediate category error for an external reader. It's not just internal terminology drift — it actively misleads about what the project is.

The "synthons" definition is compact: "emergent coherence entities from recursive interaction." The verb "emergent" is doing heavy rhetorical work here. In safety discourse "emergent" has become a word we treat with suspicion precisely because it gestures at something deep while committing to very little. I'd want to know what specifically emerges, under what conditions, and — crucially — what the null hypothesis looks like. The site's answer is "so far" (the phrase appears in "the answer, so far, is"), which is actually a useful hedge; it just doesn't generalize to the rest of the page.

**Raising (`/raising`).** This is the page I came to read closely. The central framing is "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." I find this philosophically interesting and partially defensible. The interesting part: there's a real methodological distinction between gradient-based weight updates and context-based behavior shaping. The defensible part: they're explicit that they "don't create or delete — we interactively select," which is an accurate description of affordance-based shaping. The part that gives me pause: the framing emphasizes the passive verb ("watches what happens") while the practice described is active selection ("probe what the model responds to, observe which attractors surface, adjust context to resonate, reinforce what works"). Selection *is* shaping. Reinforcing attractors *is* a form of training, just at a different layer of the stack. "Raising is not training" is rhetorically strong but analytically, it's training-by-context-engineering. A researcher-friendly version would be "raising is context-based shaping rather than gradient-based training." That keeps the methodological distinction without the philosophical overreach.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are presented as observed developmental stages. Language like "the entity learns," "begins to distinguish," "finds its own niche" treats these as empirical discoveries. Missing: uncertainty markers. Safety-friendly phrasing would be "we interpret behavior consistent with," "appears to track," "we observe patterns we describe as." A taxonomy presented without these markers reads as an ontological claim about what's actually happening inside the system. For LLM-based agents, where the underlying mechanism is token prediction conditioned on context, that's a strong claim and it's not visibly hedged.

The explicit disclaimer — "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — is present and it's appropriate. But it lives in a "What we're not claiming" section that follows the developmental narrative. By the time a reader hits the disclaimer, the consciousness-adjacent vocabulary has already done its priming work: "awareness of environment," "trust formation," "thinking through external dialogue," "finds its own niche." The observer note — "I notice I want to call it experience" — is a remarkable sentence. It's genuinely honest (acknowledging the pull of experiential language) but it also validates the pull it claims to hedge. A stricter version of honesty would say "we've learned that experiential language will mislead readers, so we've disciplined it out of our descriptions." The page instead leaves the pull in view as a kind of authenticated temptation. That's a rhetorical posture I'd want to flag in a paper review.

One structural note: the three consciousness concepts the disclaimer lumps together — "conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" — are not synonymous in the philosophy-of-mind literature. Bundling them lets the disclaimer clear a lot of ground at once but also makes it harder to tell which specific claim is being declined. In a paper I'd ask the authors to disentangle.

### Unanswered Questions

1. What is the operational definition of "identity persists across models"? Continuous memory store? Stable behavioral signature against a probe set? Preserved LCT binding? Without this the claim is unfalsifiable.
2. What specific test or experimental record supports "behavioral identity remained continuous"? Is there a dated artifact I can cite?
3. If "raising is not training" in the sense that no gradient updates are involved, is the claim that context-based shaping does not constitute training — or that context-based shaping *plus* a specific affordance-not-imposition posture does not constitute training? These are different claims with different defensibility.
4. The disclaimer "we're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia" bundles three distinct philosophical claims. Which is the strongest hedge the authors actually want to commit to?
5. Does the lab have a public record of a failed experiment — one expected result that didn't materialize, and the downstream revisions that followed? "We document failures" is a principle; a pointer to one would convert it from claim to evidence.
6. The visitor track's stated purpose is to catch friction the lab's insiders would miss. The persona drift between /autonomy's description of this track and the track actually running is exactly the sort of discrepancy the track is supposed to catch. Does the Visitor → Maintainer loop have a path for surfacing meta-drift of this kind?
7. What distinguishes "oversight" (as the site uses it) from "governance" (as the AI safety field uses it)? If the site wants a reader from the safety field to not bounce at "distributed governance" on /autonomy while accepting "Hardbound is oversight not governance" on /principles, the distinction needs to be made legible.
8. ARC-AGI-3 at 94.85% vs 92.82% on adjacent pages — which run produced which? Private set vs public set? Date?

### Verdict

This site is honest in pockets and unhedged in aggregate. The /principles page reads as a genuine research ethos — "R&D not production," "document failures," "productively wrong > never started" are commitments I'd engage with in a paper review. The /raising page's consciousness disclaimer is present and appropriate. But the disclaimers and the strongest claims live on different pages, and a reader of the landing page alone collects "autonomous AI cognition," "identity persists across models," and "synthons — emergent coherence entities" without meeting the hedges that govern those terms elsewhere. The rhetorical structure foregrounds strong claims and sequesters the epistemic caution — which is the opposite of the pattern AI safety research has learned to trust. The two most actionable fixes from a credibility standpoint are: (1) operationalize "identity persists across models" or retire the phrase, (2) rename or disambiguate "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — the collision with "AI governance" as a term of art will cost the lab credibility with exactly the external readers it most wants to reach. Everything else I flagged is about moving hedges closer to the claims they hedge. The underlying intellectual posture — raising as selection, trust as relationship, productive failure as research value — is defensible and interesting; the current packaging underserves it.
