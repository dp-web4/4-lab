## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary

### Journey

Landed on the home page from a GitHub README. The tagline "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." immediately made me lean in — that's a concrete, testable claim. The three demonstrations (persistent identity across hardware, autonomous infra maintenance, heterogeneous review) are the kind of thing I could imagine explaining to a coworker. Good start.

Then I clicked **Projects**. Eleven projects, which is a lot. I liked that ARC-AGI-3 had specific numbers (24/25 games, 94.85%) — that's credible. SAGE and Web4 also had enough detail that I could roughly picture what they are. But the Web4 equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` stopped me cold. I recognize MCP (Model Context Protocol — I think?), RDF from semantic web, but T3, V3, MRH, ATP, ADP all blur together. The equation feels like it wants to be memorable, but for me it just read as a string of unknown tokens.

Went to **Fleet** next because machines are concrete. This page worked best for me. "Thor: NVIDIA Jetson AGX with 122GB VRAM running Qwen 3.5 27B" — that I can picture. The insight that "model family matters as much as size" (smaller Gemma beating larger Phi-4) is the kind of finding I'd forward to a colleague.

**Autonomy** was interesting but dense. 31 tracks is a lot to take in. The schedule made sense as a table. "Visitor" and "Maintainer" tracks actually form a loop I can understand (personas find problems → fixer fixes them). "Dream consolidation" and "adversarial validation" went by without explanation — my mental list of "things to look up later" kept growing.

**Raising** is where I paused longest. The distinction from "training" is clear enough: observational vs prescriptive. The six phases read like a developmental curriculum. But "attractors" and the Vygotsky reference assume a vocabulary I don't have. I could tell the authors were being careful with consciousness-adjacent claims, which I appreciated.

**Principles** felt grounded — the inclusion of a catastrophic failure ("p_crit derivation failed...400x error") did more for credibility than any polished framing. Ten principles is a lot to absorb in one read, though.

**Context** is where I realized I'm probably not the target reader yet. It's infrastructure for a system I don't fully understand the pieces of. "SNARC" (Surprise/Novelty/Arousal/Reward/Conflict memory) was actually defined here, which was nice — one of the few acronyms that got a real expansion in place.

**Links** was sparse. One email (dp@metalinxx.io), a few pointers to explainer sites (Synchronism, SAGE), a GitHub org. No "how to contribute" path, no Discord/forum, no issue tracker link. If I were curious to engage, I'd probably end up just emailing and feeling awkward about it.

Right now the two pages contradict each other.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8/8 (Home, Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links)
- **Understanding achieved**: solid — I already know the referents; I'm checking fidelity

A newcomer sees symbols; I see a site that doesn't teach what its own notation means.

### Journey

I went straight to Projects, Fleet, and Context — the three pages where accuracy matters most for someone already inside the ecosystem. Then Principles to verify the rule set, then Home to check the front door, then Raising/Autonomy/Links to sweep the rest.

**Projects** is where my concern started. The Web4 card shows the equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` but **does not explain the operators**. The operators are the whole point. `/` means "verified by". `*` means "contextualized by". `+` means "augmented with". Without that, the equation reads as arithmetic with undefined symbols, and a reader assumes `T3/V3` is a fraction or a pairing — it's neither. It's "T3 verified by V3". A Web4 contributor reading this page would immediately know the operator semantics are missing; anyone else gets a false mental model. This is the single most important fix on the site for someone trying to *use* Web4 rather than just hear about it.

The Projects page also uses "T3/V3 trust tensors" as if that were the expansion. It isn't. T3 expands letter-by-letter to **Talent / Training / Temperament**. V3 expands letter-by-letter to **Valuation / Veracity / Validity**. "Trust tensor" / "value tensor" are the *category labels* for what those triplets measure, not the acronym expansions. The Home page makes this error more starkly: it writes "T3 (Trust Tensor)" as if "Trust Tensor" were what T3 stands for. No — the "3" in T3 is three T-words. That's the whole mnemonic structure of the canonical vocabulary, and the site obscures it.

**Fleet** was cleaner on terminology (the T3 expansion there is the canonical one) but mostly avoids engaging with Web4 specifics. The machine-by-machine findings are accurate as far as I can evaluate them — I recognize the models, the capacity claims look right, and the "model family matters as much as size" finding is consistent with what I've seen cited elsewhere in our work. I didn't surface "governance experiment" / "governance entities" phrasing that Pass 2 flagged; my fetch shows "Peer-to-peer, no central coordinator" instead. Either the fetch trimmed those phrases or they've been edited since Pass 2. **Needs verification by a full-page read.**

**Context** was the page I hoped would carry the semantic weight and doesn't. The equation appears. Operators still unexplained. RDF referenced as "ontological backbone" but never expanded to "Resource Description Framework". MCP never expanded. MRH never expanded — and MRH (Markov Relevancy Horizon) is arguably the single most novel concept in the equation. A contributor expects the Context page to be the canonical anchor; right now it's a narrative page that gestures at terms it doesn't define.

**Principles** is the strongest page from my perspective. The "Web4 is an ontology, not architecture or infrastructure" assertion is correct and load-bearing. The "Hardbound is oversight, not governance" assertion is correct. "Fractal leverage, not unification or scope inflation" is correct. The Synthon definition is consistent with what I use day-to-day. These are the rules. The problem is that the rest of the site doesn't follow them — most visibly on the Home page, where **SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine"** while Principles rejects "governance" as a term. This is not my persona discovering this for the first time — Pass 2 already flagged it — but I want to confirm from inside the ecosystem: the "governance" in SAGE's name is historical drift. In current working vocabulary, SAGE's cognitive layer is not a governance layer. "Governance" belongs to neither SAGE nor Hardbound in canonical usage. Either SAGE needs a new expansion or the Principles rule needs to be explicit that it *includes* SAGE's legacy name.

**Raising** hedges consciousness claims appropriately ("something that looks like X", "we're not claiming sentience"). "Raising is not training" is correctly framed. No drift.

**Autonomy** avoids the core Web4 vocabulary entirely. That's defensible for an operational page, but it means a reader coming in via /autonomy gets no anchor to the rest of the site's terminology. Low-priority.

**Home** is where external readers land. It expands LCT correctly ("Linked Context Token"). It describes Web4 as "trust-native ontology" — correct. It describes Hardbound correctly as part of the ecosystem. But: SAGE = "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" (the governance contradiction), T3 mislabeled, V3 mislabeled, ATP/ADP described as "energy tokens" rather than expanded to "Allocation Transfer / Discharge Packet". The first page a newcomer sees is the page with the most terminology drift.

**Links** is fine — no terminology concerns. Sparse contact surface but that's not my persona's concern.

### Journey

I went straight to **Principles** first. This is where I form my priors about whether the lab is epistemically serious. The page does most things right from a research-integrity standpoint: principle 3 ("R&D, not production") is stated explicitly; principle 6 ("Document failures") is instantiated concretely — the p_crit derivation "failed catastrophically — 400x error" is named and used as evidence that "trust thresholds are empirical, not derivable from first principles." That is a very good sign. Labs that document their own 400x errors on a public page tend to be the kind of labs whose positive claims I should take seriously.

Two things on Principles gave me pause anyway. First, the Hill function claim: the page asserts the Hill function describes enzyme binding **and** trust formation "not because of philosophy, but because it's the same mechanism." That is an ontological claim, not a modeling choice. A functional form appearing in two domains does not mean the mechanism is the same; it often means the domains share a constraint (saturation, cooperativity) expressible in the same algebra. A reviewer would want that framed as "the same mathematical structure" or "an analogous dynamic," not "the same mechanism." The escalation from analogy to identity is the kind of small claim that, left unchecked, compounds into overreach. Second, "Even 1% coupling gives 35% coherence gain — sparse trust suffices" appears without confidence intervals, a sample description, or a link to the experiment. I don't doubt it; I can't evaluate it. Either is recoverable; both deserve marginal hedging.

I then went to **Raising** — this is the page that has the most reputational exposure for a lab working anywhere near "AI development." The framing did more than I expected. Two explicit disclaimers, both strong:

> "We use developmental language because it fits, not because we're making consciousness claims."

> "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do."

That second sentence is the research-defensible version of "raising not training." It says: we are making a claim about **which descriptive vocabulary better fits the observations**, not a claim about the ontology of the thing observed. I can defend that in a review. A skeptical reader who conflates the two will still push back, but the page has done the work of separating them cleanly.

The "looks like" pattern throughout — "something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships" — is exactly the right hedge and appears consistently. Vygotsky is named as a borrowed framing, not a proof.

Where Raising leaks: "The entity begins generating its own questions" is stated as fact rather than as appearance. Given the surrounding text does so much "looks like" work, dropping the hedge here reads as inconsistency rather than stronger claim. Easy fix: "begins generating what look like self-originated questions" or similar. Second leak: results are described as "real, measurable" without further qualification of *measured how, against what*. A researcher reads that and wants a method.

**Autonomy** is where I found the framing that bothered me most at a research level. "No human triggers them" — but they run on cron schedules authored by a human. That is a claim about *the moment of invocation*, not about the origin of the invocation pattern. ### Is "raising not training" defensible?

Yes, as stated on /raising. The page carefully frames the distinction as *which vocabulary better describes the observations*, not *whether the system is a developing being*. That is a methodological claim, not an ontological one, and it is defensible:

- Training (loss function optimization) describes what happens during gradient-descent pretraining.
- Raising (context shaping, interactive selection across sessions) describes what happens in multi-session agent orchestration *after* pretraining is done.

These are different regimes and deserve different vocabulary. A researcher can accept that without accepting any claim about consciousness or agency. The page does the work.

The risk is that "raising" language, once established in the lab's own voice, propagates elsewhere on the site *without* the accompanying hedges — and then a reader lands on /projects seeing "12-step consciousness loop" and concludes the lab is making consciousness claims. The /raising page defends itself. The /projects page undoes that defense.
