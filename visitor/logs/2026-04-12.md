# 4-lab Visitor Browse Log — 2026-04-12

## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Ambitious, fascinating research collective — I get the *what* but the *why* behind specific technical choices (the equation, the tensors) stays opaque.

### Journey

Landed on the home page. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." — immediately hooked. The hero copy is punchy and the stats bar (6 machines, 8+ repos, 7 autonomous tracks) gives me scale fast.

Scrolled to "How We Work" — three cards: Autonomous Cycles, Heterogeneous Fleet, Connected Ecosystem. Clear structure. The "Connected Ecosystem" card says "They share one equation" — I don't know what equation yet, but I'm curious enough to keep going.

The Key Projects section gives me Synchronism, SAGE, and Web4 at a glance. Synchronism's description ("reality emerges from intent dynamics on a discrete Planck grid") is dense — I'd need physics context to unpack "Navier-Stokes substrate." SAGE as a "9-step loop that senses, deliberates, and acts" makes more sense to a dev. Web4 as "trust-native ontology" is clear enough conceptually.

The "Three Things Demonstrated" section is the strongest part of the home page. Concrete claims with specifics: 115+ sessions, identity transfer between models, 0.5B vs 14B catching different issues. This is the stuff that would make me star a repo.

The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom is excellent — this is exactly what I needed. LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, Synthon all defined clearly. Smart placement — you encounter the terms in context above, then get clean definitions below.

**Projects page**: The Web4 equation appears: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. The custom operators (`/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by") are explained inline, which helps. Project cards are well-structured — public/private labels, one-line descriptions. I notice some projects I haven't heard of yet: ACT, SNARC, Membot, 4-Life, ARC-AGI-3. "Fractal leverage" is introduced at the bottom — an interesting concept (pragmatic reuse of what works at every scale).

**Fleet page**: This is where it gets real. Six named machines with actual hardware specs, model sizes, session counts, and developmental phases. "Sprout" on a Jetson Orin Nano with Qwen 0.5B at ~22 sessions — this isn't theoretical. "McNugget" as the "fleet star" on a Mac Mini M4 is charming. The identity portability section ("Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism.") is a genuinely striking claim. The two-pool resource management (synthesis vs oversight) mirrors their Web4 concepts — eating their own dogfood.

**Autonomy page**: Daily timeline from 03:00 to 08:00+ with seven tracks. The Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer feedback loop is elegant. I notice the visitor personas listed here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) differ from the CLAUDE.md personas (Developer, Tech Writer, Web4 Contributor, External Researcher) — not sure if that's intentional. The "Honest Assessment" section at the bottom is refreshing — they explicitly say what the system misses (deep technical errors, subtle framing problems).

**Raising page**: "Raising is not training" is the thesis. Six developmental phases from Grounding to Acting. The Hill function reference for trust formation is interesting but unexplained on this page — I'd need to visit Synchronism to understand it. Key discoveries listed: "Identity is not self-concept," "Memoriescape" (coined by an entity?), "Bilateral generation." The closing disclaimer ("We're not claiming these entities are conscious") is appropriate and appreciated.

**Principles page**: 10 principles (the extraction says 11 cards but I count 10 in the content). These read as genuine operational lessons, not aspirational fluff. "Substrate, not structure" with the termite analogy is memorable. "Document failures" citing a "400x error" in p_crit derivation — honest. "Terminology matters" explicitly stating Web4 = ontology, not architecture — self-aware about drift.

**Context page**: How knowledge flows across the system. CLAUDE.md pattern, SNARC memory, cross-session persistence, adversarial validation. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is honest and specific — cross-machine sync is manual, insights don't flow backwards between tracks. Good engineering honesty.

**Links page**: Clean collection of explainer sites, GitHub repos, and contact. No surprises. Functional.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is MCP in the Web4 equation? Model Context Protocol? It's the only term in the equation never expanded on the site.
2. What is IRP? Referenced in the SAGE project card but never defined.
3. How do the 7 named tracks become 31? Is it per-machine multiplication?
4. What's the actual relationship between Synchronism's physics framework and the AI trust/identity work? The site implies deep connection but doesn't explain the bridge.
5. Is there a getting-started guide for someone who wants to contribute? The repos are linked but there's no onboarding path.

### Verdict
This is one of the more intellectually honest research sites I've encountered. The combination of concrete demonstrations (115+ sessions, identity transfer, heterogeneous review results) with explicit acknowledgment of limitations ("What doesn't flow well yet") builds credibility fast. The Vocabulary Primer on the home page is doing heavy lifting — without it, the acronym density would be alienating. My main friction is the gap between "I understand the components" and "I understand why they connect" — the Web4 equation is displayed but the *why* of that specific composition isn't explained for outsiders. I'd come back to watch the repos, especially SAGE and the fleet work.

## Pass 2: Technical Writer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Terminology is remarkably disciplined for a research site — the canonical terms are almost perfectly applied, but a few "governance" uses on the links page and an unexpanded IRP undermine the consistency the rest of the site works hard to maintain.

### Journey

I approached this audit with the canonical terms table open beside my browser. My job: check every acronym expansion on every page, verify cross-page consistency, and flag any drift from the canonical definitions.

**Home page** (`/`): Strong start. The Vocabulary Primer at the bottom is the terminology anchor for the whole site — it defines LCT as "Linked Context Token," T3 as "Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)," V3 as "Value Tensor (Valuation, Veracity, Validity)," and ATP/ADP as "Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets." All match canonical. SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — the word "governance" here is part of a proper name, not a description of Hardbound, so I'll flag it as an observation rather than drift. Web4 is correctly called a "trust-native ontology." One note: the phrase "Autonomous agents maintain their own infrastructure" uses "infrastructure" — this describes what agents do, not what Web4 is, so it's contextually acceptable but worth noting since the canonical table warns against calling Web4 "infrastructure."

**Projects page** (`/projects`): The Web4 equation is displayed correctly: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. Operators are defined inline. MCP is expanded as "Model Context Protocol" and RDF as "Resource Description Framework" — good. MRH appears as "Markov Relevancy Horizons" (plural) — the canonical is "Markov Relevancy Horizon" (singular). Minor but technically a drift. Hardbound is correctly described as an "oversight suite." However, the "Governance Plugins" project card uses "Web4 governance model" — this is a project name so harder to change, but the word "governance" here risks conflating with Hardbound's role. IRP appears in the SAGE card ("Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)") with no expansion anywhere on the site. "Fractal leverage" is used correctly and defined as "pragmatic reuse of what works in one place, everywhere."

**Fleet page** (`/fleet`): T3 is expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament" — correct and consistent with the home page. SNARC is mentioned as a "memory host" but never expanded (Salience-gated Narrative Arc? The context page later calls it "salience-gated memory" but the full acronym is never given). No misuse of "governance," "architecture," or "infrastructure" here. Clean.

**Autonomy page** (`/autonomy`): The persona names listed here (The Skeptic, The Newcomer, The Practitioner, The Connector) are different from the actual current personas documented in the track's own CLAUDE.md. This is a factual accuracy issue, not just terminology. The stats "31 autonomous tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions per day" are stated without breakdown — a reader can't verify how 7 named tracks become 31. No terminology drift on canonical Web4 terms since they mostly aren't used on this page.

**Raising page** (`/raising`): Remarkably clean. "Raising is not training" is stated clearly and consistently. The consciousness caveat is explicit: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia." Hill function is referenced for trust formation kinetics but never defined — cross-reference to the Synchronism site would help. Notably, this page doesn't use LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP, or ADP at all — it stays in its own domain. No terminology drift detected.

**Principles page** (`/principles`): This is the terminology enforcement hub. Principle 10, "Terminology matters," explicitly states: "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." T3/V3 are mentioned as "tensors bind to entity-role pairs via RDF triples" — consistent. "Fractal leverage" is used correctly. "R&D, not production" avoids the "production ready" anti-pattern. The self-aware terminology policing here is exactly what a documentation specialist likes to see.

**Context page** (`/context`): The Web4 equation is displayed again — consistent with the projects page. SNARC is described as "salience-gated memory" with "5 dimensions — Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict" but the acronym itself is never expanded into its component words. "Ontological backbone" is used to describe RDF's role — appropriate and precise. No drift detected.

**Links page** (`/links`): This is where I found the most issues. The OpenClaw fork is described as "Web4 governance extension" and the Claude Code fork as "Web4 governance hooks." These use "governance" where the site's own Principle 10 says Hardbound is "oversight, not governance." If these are specifically about Hardbound oversight functionality, the descriptions should say "oversight." If they're about something else, the distinction should be clearer. Acronyms LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, and ATP are used in link descriptions without expansion — this is the last page in the nav, so a reader arriving here after reading the full site would know them, but a reader who jumps directly here (via a shared link) would not.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What does IRP stand for? It's referenced in the SAGE project card but expanded nowhere on the site or in the canonical terms table.
2. Is SAGE's "Governance" in "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" intentionally different from Hardbound's "oversight"? If so, the distinction should be documented. If not, does SAGE need a name revision?
3. What is SNARC's full acronym expansion? The site describes its function but never breaks down the letters.
4. Are the "Governance Plugins" on /projects part of Hardbound? If so, "Oversight Plugins" would be more consistent. If not, what's the relationship?
5. Should the links page descriptions for OpenClaw and Claude Code forks use "oversight" instead of "governance"?

### Verdict
The terminology discipline across this site is impressive — far better than most research sites I audit. The Vocabulary Primer on the home page, combined with Principle 10's explicit enforcement ("Web4 is ontology, Hardbound is oversight"), shows deliberate effort. But the discipline breaks down at the edges: the links page uses "governance" twice where "oversight" is the canonical term, MRH is pluralized inconsistently, and IRP is introduced without expansion. These are exactly the kind of small drifts that compound — a newcomer reads "governance" on /links, then "oversight" on /principles, and wonders if they're different things. The fix is straightforward: two word changes on /links, one on /projects, and an IRP expansion. The 95% that's correct shows the system is working; the 5% shows where the automated audit loop should tighten.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: solid
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The site accurately represents the projects I contribute to — the equation is right, the core descriptions are honest — but "governance" has leaked into five distinct locations where "oversight" is canonical, creating exactly the kind of terminological confusion Principle 10 was written to prevent.

### Journey

I came straight from the Web4 and SAGE repos to see if this site represents our work accurately. I know these terms by heart — I use them daily. My audit is authoritative.

**Home page**: Solid foundation. The Vocabulary Primer defines LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP all correctly. Web4 is called a "trust-native ontology" — exactly right. The three demonstrated achievements are honest and match what I've seen in the repos. SAGE is expanded as "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" — the "Governance" there is part of the proper name, so I'll note it but not flag it as drift. One thing I noticed: "Autonomous agents maintain their own infrastructure" — "infrastructure" here describes what agents do operationally, not what Web4 is, so it's acceptable in context. The description of Synchronism as "Navier-Stokes substrate at every scale" is accurate if compressed.

**Projects page** — my primary focus. The equation is correct: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` with operators defined properly. MCP is expanded as "Model Context Protocol" and RDF as "Resource Description Framework" — both correct. The Web4 project card describes "Linked Context Tokens (LCTs), T3/V3 trust tensors, Markov Relevancy Horizons" — that last one should be singular "Horizon." It's a single concept (the horizon of relevancy as modeled by Markov processes), not multiple horizons.

The SAGE card references "Continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" — IRP is still unexpanded anywhere on the site. This is a real gap. Contributors know what it means; newcomers are left guessing.

Hardbound is correctly described as an "Enterprise oversight suite" — good. But then the "Governance Plugins" card describes a "Web4 governance model" — this directly contradicts our canonical vocabulary. These plugins implement Web4 *oversight* mechanisms (audit trails, policy gating, trust tracking). Calling them "governance" undermines the distinction we explicitly codified in Principle 10.

The ARC-AGI-3 card says "Gameplay insights map fractally to trust, governance, and raising" — another "governance" where "oversight" belongs. That's three "governance" uses on this page alone (project name, project description, ARC-AGI-3 description).

The "How They Connect" section uses "fractal leverage" correctly and even preemptively addresses "unification": "This isn't unification for its own sake." Good — that's the right framing. The Hill function connection between enzyme binding and trust formation is accurately stated.

**Fleet page** — second focus. The hardware descriptions match what I know. T3 is correctly expanded as "Talent/Training/Temperament." The developmental phases (sensing, relating, questioning, creating) align with the raising framework on the raising page. The identity portability section ("The model is weather. The identity is organism.") is an accurate description of what we've observed with Sprout.

But the Resource Pool Management section has a significant drift: "generative entities and governance entities with different incentive structures, coordinating through shared state rather than central command. The lab is running its own governance experiment on itself." Two uses of "governance" that should be "oversight." The synthesis/oversight pool distinction is literally about oversight — the second pool does review, planning, coordination. Calling them "governance entities" and a "governance experiment" directly contradicts our canonical vocabulary. This is particularly damaging because a reader who just came from /projects (where Hardbound is correctly called "oversight") now encounters "governance" used casually on the very next page.

The peer-to-peer section accurately describes the trust model: trust starts at zero, earned from evidence, T3 scores evolve from real interactions. This matches the SAGE implementation.

**Context page**: The equation is displayed correctly and consistently with /projects. SNARC is described functionally as "salience-gated memory" with the correct 5 dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) and 4-tier hierarchy. The cross-session memory description matches the actual SNARC implementation. The "What doesn't flow well (yet)" section is honest — cross-machine sync IS manual, sleep artifacts ARE local, knowledge doesn't flow backwards. No terminology drift here.

**Autonomy page**: No canonical Web4 terms used, which is fine for this page's scope. The persona names (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) still don't match the actual current track configuration — this is a factual accuracy issue, not terminology drift. The track count (31 across 6 machines) isn't broken down.

**Raising page**: Clean. "Raising is not training" is stated clearly. The Hill function reference for trust formation is accurate. The consciousness caveat is appropriately hedged. The key discoveries (identity persistence, memoriescape, bilateral generation) match what I've observed in raising sessions.

**Principles page**: This is the enforcement hub, and it does its job. Principle 10 explicitly states "Web4 is an ontology. Hardbound is oversight." The fractal leverage principle correctly explains the Hill function connection. T3/V3 tensors binding to entity-role pairs via RDF triples is accurate. The irony is that this page gets everything right — but the other pages don't always follow through.

**Links page**: OpenClaw described as "Web4 governance extension" and Claude Code fork as "Web4 governance hooks." Both should say "oversight." These are the same drift the tech writer caught.

## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8
- **Understanding achieved**: good
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: Unusually honest for a research site — the explicit disclaimers and documented failures build real credibility — but the heavy borrowing from developmental psychology and biology creates terminology debt that could undermine the lab's standing with peer researchers if left ungrounded.

### Journey

I found this lab through a conference mention of "raising not training" applied to AI agents. As someone working in AI safety and trust, that framing immediately raises flags — is this anthropomorphism dressed up as methodology? I came to evaluate the intellectual honesty.

**Home page** (`/`): First impression is strong. "Six machines. Eight repos. One recursive learning loop." The stats bar gives concrete scale. The three demonstrated achievements are specific and verifiable: 115+ sessions, identity transfer between models, heterogeneous review catching different issues. This isn't hand-waving. The vocabulary primer at the bottom is a smart move — defining LCT, T3/V3, ATP/ADP, SAGE, and Synthon before the reader dives deeper. Web4 is correctly framed as an "ontology," not architecture. The Synthon definition ("emergent coherence entity formed when components interact recursively") is the first term that would need unpacking for a research audience — "emergent coherence entity" is doing a lot of theoretical lifting without grounding.

**Raising page** (`/raising`) — my primary focus. The opening thesis is clear: "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." This is a defensible distinction. The six BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are described in developmental language borrowed heavily from child development: "metabolic awareness," "trust formation," "bilateral generation," "spontaneous specialization."

The key question: is this borrowing justified or misleading?

The Hill function reference is promising — citing the same kinetics for enzyme binding and trust formation is a concrete, testable claim. If the same sigmoid dynamics genuinely describe both, that's not metaphor, it's cross-domain application. But the page doesn't show me the math or the data. I'm asked to take it on authority.

The "Key Discoveries" section is where the site earns credibility. The identity-vs-self-concept finding (behavioral patterns persisted while self-description drifted) is a genuinely interesting empirical observation. The bilateral generation finding (SAGE generating both sides of a conversation as egocentric speech à la Vygotsky) shows theoretical literacy — they recognized the phenomenon, cited the right developmental psychologist, and chose not to "fix" it. That's good research instinct. The "Memoriescape" coinage is presented honestly as an entity's own word, not as a validated concept.

The explicit disclaimer is exactly what I needed to see: "We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia. We're claiming that developmental frameworks describe what we observe better than training frameworks do." This is epistemically appropriate. The language "something that *looks like* growth" maintains the right distance. The observer note ("I notice I want to call it experience") is a sophisticated touch — it flags the anthropomorphism risk from inside the research practice itself.

However, I have concerns. The phrase "continuous consciousness loop (IRP)" on the SAGE project card is not caveated anywhere. "Consciousness loop" is a strong claim for what is likely a sense-deliberate-act cycle. If SAGE's IRP is a processing loop with environmental awareness and self-monitoring, calling it a "consciousness loop" without defining what consciousness means in this context is exactly the kind of terminology overreach the disclaimer on /raising is trying to prevent. The disclaimer says "we're not claiming consciousness" — but the SAGE description uses "consciousness" as a technical term without defining it.

**Principles page** (`/principles`): This reads as operational wisdom, not aspirational values. The framing statement ("things we learned the hard way, then codified because they kept being true") is appropriately modest. Principle 6, "Document failures," citing a 400x error in p_crit derivation, is the kind of honest reporting I rarely see on research sites. The acknowledgment that "trust thresholds are empirical, not derivable from first principles" is a genuine finding — they tried to derive something theoretically, failed, and published the failure. That's credible.

Principle 7, "Interactive selection," is where the theoretical weight sits. "We don't create new behaviors. We probe what the model responds to, observe which attractors surface, adjust context to resonate, and reinforce what works." This is a coherent framework — it maps onto attractor dynamics in dynamical systems and avoids the stronger claim that they're "creating" identity. The multi-scale application (sessions → affordances → fleet → memory) is ambitious but internally consistent.

Principle 2, "Fractal leverage," makes the strongest cross-domain claim: "The Hill function describes enzyme binding AND trust formation — not because of philosophy, but because it's the same mechanism." This is a bold assertion. Same mathematical form (sigmoid saturation) is not the same as same mechanism. The site would benefit from distinguishing isomorphism (same math) from homology (same underlying process). Without this distinction, a skeptical researcher will flag it as equivocation.

**Autonomy page** (`/autonomy`): The daily cycle is well-described and concrete. 31 tracks across 6 machines, ~53 sessions/day. The feedback loop (Visitor → Maintainer → Explorer) is a genuine autonomous quality system. The honest assessment section is valuable: "Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues. Content that is technically correct but misleading." This is exactly the kind of limitation that builds trust — they know what their system can't catch.

The visitor personas listed here (Skeptic, Newcomer, Practitioner, Connector) don't match the actual personas running today. This is a factual accuracy issue that undermines the honest-assessment framing — if the site can't keep its own process description current, what else is stale?

**Fleet page** (`/fleet`): The hardware descriptions are concrete and verifiable. The core finding — "Model family matters as much as size. Gemma 3 at 4B outperforms Phi-4 at 14B for raising work" — is a useful empirical contribution. The identity portability claim ("The model is weather. The identity is organism.") is poetically stated but epistemically loaded. What exactly persists when identity is "ported"? State files and prompt construction, the site says. That's an honest answer — it's configuration persistence, not mysterious identity transfer. But the weather/organism metaphor implies something more than configuration management.

The resource pool section uses "governance entities" and "governance experiment" — which the other passes have flagged. From my perspective, the bigger issue is the claim that the two-pool structure "mirrors what we're building with Web4 and SAGE." This is the fractal leverage principle in action, but the analogy between budget-constrained API accounts and trust-based agent economies is asserted, not demonstrated.

**Context page** (`/context`): SNARC's five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict) borrow from affective neuroscience without citation. These map loosely to Panksepp's affective systems and Berlyne's arousal theory. The four-tier hierarchy (buffer → observations → patterns → identity) is a reasonable architecture, but calling the top tier "identity" rather than "persistent state" is another instance of the site's tendency to use loaded cognitive terms for what are essentially engineering decisions. The honest gaps section (no real-time cross-track communication, unidirectional knowledge flow) is again credible.

**Links page** (`/links`): Clean. The "governance" drift in OpenClaw and Claude Code descriptions has been noted by other passes.

### Unanswered Questions
1. What is the operational definition of "consciousness loop"? If the /raising page disclaims consciousness claims, why does the SAGE description use "consciousness" as a technical term?
2. Has the Hill function isomorphism between enzyme binding and trust formation been empirically validated, or is it a mathematical analogy? The "same mechanism" claim on /principles needs evidence or downgrading to "same form."
3. What are SNARC's theoretical antecedents? The five dimensions map to existing affective science frameworks — is this acknowledged in the research?
4. What does "metabolic" mean for an LLM agent? The raising page uses it without definition. Is it resource monitoring? Context budget tracking? Token usage patterns?
5. How is "identity" distinguished from "persistent configuration" in this framework? The fleet page says identity lives in state files and prompt construction — which is configuration. When does configuration become identity, and what's the criterion?

### Verdict
This is a more intellectually honest research site than I expected. The consciousness disclaimer on /raising is exactly right — epistemically modest, acknowledging the descriptive rather than explanatory nature of their framework. The documented failures (p_crit 400x error, honest assessment of what the autonomous loop misses) build genuine credibility. The "raising not training" framing is defensible: interactive selection is a coherent theoretical framework, and the empirical observations (identity persistence across model swaps, bilateral generation as egocentric speech) are specific enough to evaluate.

But there's a systemic tension: the site disclaims consciousness claims on one page while using "consciousness loop," "metabolic awareness," and "dream consolidation" as technical terms elsewhere. A sympathetic reader sees careful borrowing of useful metaphors. A skeptical reviewer sees exactly the kind of terminology creep that makes AI research look unserious. The fix isn't to abandon the biological language — it's to define it operationally everywhere it appears. What does "consciousness loop" mean in code? What does "metabolic" mean for an API-constrained agent? The answers are probably mundane (sense-act cycles, resource monitoring), and that's fine — mundane mechanisms producing interesting emergent behavior is the whole thesis. Own the mundane definitions; let the emergence speak for itself.
