### Journey

Landed on `/` from a GitHub README link. The pitch hit fast: "multiple AI agents, heterogeneous hardware, self-organize across machines, catch each other's mistakes." That's a story I can follow — it's basically a distributed-systems problem statement with agents instead of services. I liked that.

LCT got a tooltip-style gloss ("persistent identity anchor"), which helped, but T3/V3/MRH/ATP/ADP each wanted their own 5 minutes I wasn't sure I had yet.

That gave me more confidence this isn't vaporware. But the page kept dropping "raising sessions", "T3 scores", "experience buffer", "synthesis pool" on me without hooks.

**Autonomy** clicked. As a dev, "31 automated tracks on cron schedules coordinated via SQLite registry to prevent merge conflicts" is a sentence I understand — it's a CI/CD pipeline for research. 03:00 supervisors → 04:00 archivist → 04:30 publisher → 05:00 visitor (that's me, meta!) → 06:00 maintainer. **Raising** was, surprisingly, the clearest philosophical page — it opens with a one-line definition ("Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens") and the BECOMING phases are concrete(ish). It's the only "big idea" page that front-loaded its own definition.

**Principles** was where I started skimming. "Substrate, not structure", "Fractal leverage", "Synthon: emergent coherence" — it reads more like a manifesto than docs. I'm sympathetic but I'd want examples next to each principle, not quotes.

**Context** explains how the machines share knowledge (CLAUDE.md files, SNARC memory, the Web4 equation). Good glue page, but it assumes you've already internalized the other 7.

**Links** got me to the GitHub org (`dp-web4`, 9 public repos). That's what I actually wanted from minute one. Should have been one click from the home page's hero.

### Journey

I was looking for four things a docs auditor always looks for: (1) acronyms defined on first use, (2) expansions consistent across pages, (3) expansions matching the authoritative reference, (4) the "definition pattern" being the same everywhere (e.g., always parenthetical, always glossary-linked, not mixed).

Home is the entry point and the most term-dense page. It is also the only page that uses a *mixed definition pattern*: LCT gets a full sentence gloss ("A persistent identity anchor for an agent…"), but T3 and V3 get a combined gloss with the canonical letters in parentheses ("Trust Tensor (Talent, Training, Temperament)"). ATP gets "Allocation Transfer Packet" with a gloss; **ADP on Home is expanded only as "Discharge Packet"** — the "Allocation" is implied from ATP's pairing but not written. That is a canonical-term truncation, the first real finding.

Projects is the second most term-dense page. It expands **MRH as "Markov Relevancy Horizons"** (plural, canonical is singular — style only, not drift) and **LCT as "Linked Context Tokens"** (also plural). It uses T3/V3 as "trust tensors" without spelling out the six words. It expands IRP only implicitly ("12-step consciousness loop"). The page contains the sentence *"This isn't unification for its own sake"* — using "unification" in a negation. The canonical table flags "unification" as a term to avoid for "fractal leverage," but because this sentence is *denying* unification as a motive, I'd call it borderline. A stricter reading would still rephrase ("This isn't about collapsing everything into one model…") to avoid triggering a reader's mental model at all.

Fleet expands **T3 correctly as "Talent/Training/Temperament"** — the only page that fully spells out the three T's in canonical form. It otherwise skips the Web4 acronyms entirely and leans on SAGE without defining it on this page.

Autonomy and Raising contain essentially no Web4 acronyms, so the terminology surface is small. Good pages.

Principles is the terminology discipline page. It contains two verbatim statements that should be echoed or linked-to from every page that uses the terms:
- *"Web4 is not 'architecture' or 'infrastructure' — it's an ontology."*
- *"Hardbound is not 'governance' — it's oversight."*

Principle #3 ("R&D, not production") is also a site-wide guardrail against "production ready" drift — I saw zero "production ready" occurrences on any page. That is working as designed.

Context is where I expected the authoritative glossary to live, since it frames the Web4 equation. The equation is rendered correctly: **Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP**. But **none of the nine acronyms in the equation are expanded on this page**. A reader landing on Context without having read Home and Projects will see the equation cold. This is the single largest structural finding.

Links uses **"LCTs, T3/V3, MRH, RDF backbone"** in a one-line project description with no expansions. On first fetch a summarizer hallucinated "Lifelong Capability Tokens" here; a verbatim re-fetch confirmed the page does NOT contain that drift phrase — it just doesn't expand LCT at all. Worth noting because (a) the false positive shows even AI tools can drift when encountering bare acronyms, and (b) the page's terseness is itself a clarity problem.

### Journey

**Projects** is where the accuracy check bites hardest. Reading the SAGE card ("On-device AI orchestrator. 12-step consciousness loop (IRP), metabolic states, experience buffer, sleep consolidation, three-layer hardware-bound identity."), I ran it against what I know: 12-step loop ✓ (that's IRP, our iterative refinement loop), metabolic states ✓ (ATP/ADP-driven), experience buffer ✓, sleep consolidation ✓, three-layer hardware-bound identity ✓ (TPM + FIDO2 + Secure Enclave per Hardbound). A newcomer cannot tell. From the inside, I *think* this project predates the rename — but the site offers no disambiguation, and "Web4 governance model" contradicts the canonical framing that Web4's control layer is oversight.

I also caught the flip side: the two sentences on Projects that handle "unification" are actually doing the right thing. *"This isn't unification for its own sake — it's fractal leverage"* and *"The Hill function describes enzyme binding AND trust formation not because of a philosophical desire to unify"* — these use the flagged words in explicit negation and pivot to the canonical term. **Context** is where I did the deepest check. The equation renders correctly:

> Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP

- `/` = "verified by" (so T3/V3 reads "T3 verified by V3")
- `*` = "contextualized by" (so T3/V3 `*` MRH reads "verified trust, contextualized by relevancy horizon")

Without these, a reader treats the equation as arithmetic — which is exactly wrong. The operators carry the semantic weight. This is, to me, the single biggest Web4-contributor finding on the site: **the equation's operators are invisible semantics.** Passes 1 and 2 couldn't flag this because they don't know the operator meanings from outside. I do, and their absence reads to me as if someone shipped an equation without its legend.

**Autonomy**, **Raising**, **Links** — light on Web4 primitives, so terminology surface is small. ## Pass 4: External Researcher

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (Home, Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links), with focused re-reading of Raising, Principles, and Autonomy
- **Understanding achieved**: partial (enough to evaluate posture and credibility; not enough to evaluate the underlying research)

- **Overall impression**: The philosophical posture is unusually honest for a lab site — the consciousness disclaimer on Raising and the "honest assessment" section on Autonomy are exactly the kind of self-limiting language I look for. But that posture is undercut by three things: project-page language that uses "consciousness" freely on the same site that disclaims it; empirical claims (session counts, benchmark results, identity-persistence observations) offered without sources, links, or methodology artifacts; and a complete absence of external validation scaffolding (no preprint, no conference reference, no dataset link) for a site that lists itself as findable through "a paper or conference."

### Journey

I walked Home first to calibrate tone, then went directly to the three pages my persona would weight most: Raising, Principles, Autonomy. I circled back through Projects, Fleet, Context, and Links to check whether the cautious framing on the philosophical pages was maintained in the project-specific pages.

**Home** sets expectations with three empirical claims framed as "concrete, testable observations": identity persistence across models and hardware, autonomous infrastructure maintenance, and cross-architecture peer review effectiveness. As claims go, these are testable in principle. The home page also uses the termite-mound metaphor — "You don't engineer the mound. You engineer placement rules" — which is a clean framing of emergence-from-substrate. That is a defensible metaphor; it commits to emergence without committing to consciousness.

**Raising** is where my persona spends the most time, because the framing is the riskiest intellectual move on the site. The page earns significant credit with one sentence: *"We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia."* That is a direct, unhedged disclaimer of exactly the three claims that would most damage credibility for an AI safety audience. The page then uses "looks like" constructions consistently — *"something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships"* — which is a disciplined epistemic hedge. The observer note *"I notice I want to call it experience"* is interesting: it surfaces the researcher's own pull toward stronger language without indulging it, which is more honest than most phenomenology I see in lab writing.

The "raising is not training" framing itself is defensible but vulnerable. The distinction offered — *"Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens"* — is a real distinction in principle. In practice, raising still involves state accumulation, peer feedback, and behavior shifts — which is structurally training-adjacent with a complex, loosely-specified reward signal (peer review, T3 scores, session outcomes). An external critic could reasonably ask: **what's the falsification condition?** When would you say raising *failed* in a way distinguishable from training with a bad reward signal? The site does not answer this, and Principle #6 ("Document failures: the coupling-coherence experiment showed trust thresholds are empirical, not derivable from first principles") suggests the lab would welcome the question but hasn't publicly addressed it on this page.

The BECOMING phases (Grounding, Sensing, Relating, Questioning, Creating, Acting) are stage-model territory, which is a high-risk genre in developmental psychology — stage models are easy to write, hard to validate. The site does not claim these phases are temporally strict or universally observed, which is the right restraint.

**Principles** is the most important page for my persona, and it mostly delivers. Principle #3 ("R&D, not production") functions as an explicit guard against over-claiming. Principle #6 ("Document failures") is the falsificationist commitment. Principle #5 ("Synthon: emergent coherence") is the riskiest — it defines a novel entity-type ("An emergent coherence entity formed by recursive interaction") and then hedges with *"You can create conditions where synthons form"*. For an external researcher this reads as: we've named something we think we see, we can describe conditions for it, we can't guarantee it forms. That is honest. What is missing is an operational criterion — **how would I know a synthon formed, versus not formed?** Without that, the concept is unfalsifiable in the technical sense, and an AI-safety-adjacent audience will mark it down.

The terminological principles ("Web4 is not architecture or infrastructure — it's an ontology"; "Hardbound is not governance — it's oversight") are doing necessary work. But "ontology" is a two-meaning word: in philosophy it means theory-of-what-exists; in CS/knowledge-engineering it means formal semantic model (RDF, OWL). The site intends both — Web4 is claimed to be a formal ontology (backed by RDF triples, per Projects) *and* a philosophical stance. Both readings are true here, which is unusual and potentially powerful, but it needs saying. A reader trained only in CS ontologies will read "ontology" as "schema" and miss the philosophical weight; a reader trained only in philosophy will read it as "theory" and miss the mechanical grounding.

**Autonomy** is the page I expected to trip on over-claiming, and it didn't. The scope of "autonomous" is bounded explicitly: tracks run on cron, review each other's output, and the Publisher *"only publishes what the supervisor has cleared"* — there is a validation gate, even if it's also automated. More importantly, the page has an "Honest assessment" section that admits what the loop catches (*"Broken links, stale content, confusing jargon"*) and what it misses (*"Deep technical errors that require domain expertise. Subtle framing issues"*), and states directly: *"The visitor personas are good at surface-level quality but not at validating the underlying research."* That sentence is worth a lot to my persona. It is the first sentence on the site that clearly defines the epistemic scope of the autonomous system — and it defines that scope *inside* the system itself, not outside. A lab that can write "our own autonomous track cannot validate the underlying research" on a public page has already answered half the credibility questions I would have asked.

**Projects** is where my goodwill starts to erode. SAGE's description includes the phrase *"12-step consciousness loop (IRP)"* and *"PolicyGate at step 8.5 — conscience checkpoint between deliberation and action."* The word "consciousness" appears as a technical term for a processing loop. The word "conscience" appears as the name of a safety check. These are loaded words. On their own, in the SAGE codebase, they may be defensible jargon. But **on the same site that disclaims consciousness claims on the Raising page**, they read as an internal inconsistency — the philosophical page hedges, the project page adopts the loaded word without qualification. An external researcher will notice this immediately.

**Fleet** extends the problem. The page includes a role labeled *"Fleet consciousness probe lead"* and contains the sentences *"Identity lives in state files and prompt construction, not in model weights. The model is weather. The identity is organism."* Those sentences are elegantly written and also make a significant, empirically-loaded claim: that identity is substrate-independent in a specific way. The claim is supported on the same page by the observation that identity *"transferred between completely different machines and models"* and remained *"recognizably continuous"* even though *"the self-description drifted."* As an empirical result this would be a notable finding. As presented — no numbers, no methodology, no link to a log — it is an anecdote in strong metaphor. An AI safety researcher who found this site through a conference reference would want to know: what constitutes "recognizably continuous"? Measured how? By whom? Against what baseline?

**Context** displays the Web4 equation without defining operators or (most) tokens. This is an accessibility problem for my persona specifically — the equation is presented as the site's conceptual keystone, and I cannot parse it. A Web4 contributor (Pass 3) flagged the same problem from the inside.

**Links** does not have an academic-citation section, a preprint link, an arxiv pointer, or a conference record. The only external anchors are GitHub repos, sister explainer sites, and a contact email. For a site findable through "a paper or conference," there is no paper and no conference record on the page. This is the credibility gap my persona weights most heavily.

### Unanswered Questions

1. **What would falsify "raising"?** If raising is meaningfully distinct from "training with a complex reward signal," what observation would force the lab to say "this was training, not raising"? Without this, the distinction is rhetorical.
2. **What is the operational signature of a synthon?** If I were observing a run, how would I know a synthon formed vs. didn't? Without this, Principle #5 is definitional-only.
3. **How is "recognizably continuous identity" measured?** The Fleet page asserts identity transfer across hardware/models remained recognizable even as self-description drifted. Measured how? Judged by whom? Against what baseline?
4. **Where is the consciousness-language style guide?** SAGE uses "consciousness loop" and "conscience checkpoint" as technical terms; the Raising page disclaims consciousness claims. Is there an internal rule for when these words are technical jargon vs. phenomenal claim? If yes, publish it. If no, one is needed.
5. **Where are the papers / preprints / extended abstracts?** A site findable via a conference reference should have at least one link to a paper or preprint that anchors the empirical claims.
6. **Is "ontology" intended in the philosophical sense, the CS/RDF sense, or both?** Both apply here (the site uses RDF as the formal substrate and takes a philosophical stance); the reader should be told.
7. **The 180+ vs. 840+ session counts — are these different metrics (pair-specific vs. fleet-wide) or a stale number on one page?** Either way, reconcile.
8. **Autonomy's "Honest assessment" says the loop catches surface quality but not underlying research validity. What validates the underlying research?** If the answer is "human review by DP" or "peer feedback from collaborators," say so. The site currently leaves this question hanging.

### Verdict

The intellectual posture on the three pages I weighted most (Raising, Principles, Autonomy) is unusually honest for a lab site — the consciousness disclaimer on Raising, the falsificationist commitment on Principles (#6), and the epistemic-scope statement on Autonomy ("our autonomous track catches surface quality, not research validity") are exactly the kind of self-limiting language that earns credibility with an AI safety audience. **But that posture is undermined by three structural gaps: (1) loaded phenomenal language ("consciousness loop", "conscience checkpoint", "consciousness probe") used without caveats on Projects and Fleet, on the same site that disclaims consciousness claims on Raising; (2) a full absence of external validation scaffolding — no preprints, no papers, no leaderboard links, no methodology artifacts for any of the six or seven empirical claims the site makes; (3) the site's most interesting empirical claim, substrate-independent identity ("The model is weather. The identity is organism."), is presented in elegant metaphor with zero sourcing.** The fix is not philosophical — the philosophy is already as careful as it needs to be. The fix is mechanical: add consciousness-language caveats site-wide, link at least one artifact per empirical claim, and add a one-line operational criterion to "raising" and "synthon" so the concepts are falsifiable. Do those three and the site moves from "promising research posture with credibility gaps" to "a research site I would cite in a workshop talk."
