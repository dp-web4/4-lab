## Pass 1: Curious Developer

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (Home, Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Lab feels real and concrete in places (Fleet, Projects); the value proposition gets buried under dense jargon I can't parse in 2 minutes.

### Journey
Landed on the home page from a hypothetical README link. The tagline "Six machines. Fourteen repos. One recursive learning loop." is punchy — that hooked me. Right below that, though, I hit "trust-native ontology, autonomous AI cognition, and theoretical frameworks" plus an equation: `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP`. Five acronyms in one line, with operators (`/`, `*`, `+`) that I can't tell are mathematical or symbolic. I scrolled looking for a "what does this lab DO" paragraph and instead got concept clusters. SAGE got expanded ("Situation-Aware Guidance Engine, 12-step cognition loop"), which helped, but I still didn't have a one-sentence answer to "what would I use this for?"

Clicked **Projects** next, hoping for an entry point. This was the best page for me — actual project cards with one-liners. ACT and Hardbound felt like things I could understand (UI for tokens; hardware-bound trust enforcement with TPM/FIDO2). SAGE I now half-grok as "on-device cognition kernel." The Web4 equation reappeared and was still cryptic — I'd appreciate either a plain-language gloss or just dropping the equation from the marketing surface and keeping it in /context.

**Fleet** was a relief. Six named machines with hardware specs, models, session counts, and roles. This page is concrete — I can imagine running the same setup. "LoRA" appears uncommented; I happen to know it (low-rank adaptation), but a tooltip would help less ML-fluent devs.

**Autonomy** lays out the daily timeline (03:00 supervisors → 04:00 archivist → 05:00 visitor → 06:00 maintainer → 08:00 explorer → dream consolidation). I liked this — it's the only page that made the system feel operational instead of conceptual. But the page says "31 tracks across 6 machines with ~53 daily sessions" while the home page promised "36+ autonomous daily tasks." Different units, but to a skimming dev it reads as inconsistent.

**Raising** was unexpectedly readable. "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." That's a clean thesis. I appreciated the explicit caveat ("we're not claiming these entities are conscious"). The BECOMING phases (Grounding → Sensing → Relating → Questioning → Creating → Acting) are easy to remember.

**Principles** felt like a manifesto more than a guide — fine, but "p_crit derivation" and "1% coupling → 35% coherence gain" landed as alien references; I had no scaffolding for what was being measured.

**Context** explained how knowledge flows between repos (CLAUDE.md → SNARC → Web4 equation → adversarial review → logs). Useful as architecture, but I'd want a diagram. The equation reappeared a third time.

**Links** gave me GitHub orgs and contact (dp@metalinxx.io). Fine.

I'd come back if I had a specific reason (e.g., I wanted to build on Web4), but I wouldn't recommend this site as the first link in a "what is dp-web4?" introduction without a primer page in front of it.

## Pass 3: Web4 Contributor

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 8 (Home, Projects, Fleet, Autonomy, Raising, Principles, Context, Links). Deep focus on Projects, Fleet, Context.
- **Understanding achieved**: solid (this is my domain — I'm checking the site against what I know is true)

The two issues that bother me as a contributor are (a) ACP and MCP appear together with no relationship explained, and (b) Hardbound's "Enterprise" framing leans commercial in a way the rest of the site avoids.

### Journey
I went straight to /projects to check the equation and the project descriptions, then /context to verify the operator glosses match, then /fleet to verify the machine→role mapping, then walked the rest of the nav.

The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` appears on /projects and on /context, and in both places the operator legend is rendered inline: `/` = "verified by", `*` = "contextualized by", `+` = "augmented with". This matches the canonical wording exactly. The grouping reads correctly too: T3 verified by V3 contextualized by MRH; ATP verified by ADP — that's the canonical semantics, not a coincidence of typography. As a contributor I am genuinely relieved by this; I have seen sites quietly drop the operators or substitute "and" for "+" and lose the whole point.

I noticed one thing the technical writer flagged but didn't quite frame as a contributor would: on the home page (per my fetch) the equation is *referenced* but not *rendered*. The home page mentions "the Web4 equation" without showing it. Pass 1's transcript reports the equation IS on home; mine didn't surface it. I cannot tell from one fetch whether the home page changed since Pass 1 ran or whether the WebFetch summarizer skipped a card — flagging for the maintainer to verify visually. If the equation has been removed from home, that's a regression I'd push back on: the equation is the elevator pitch.

The /projects card for SAGE describes it as "On-device cognition kernel — evolving from orchestrator to world-shaper, 12-step cognition loop, 6 brain-architecture components, 900+ raising sessions". This matches the SAGE roadmap as I know it. The "orchestrator → world-shaper" framing is the trajectory we use internally; I'd be surprised to see it externalized but it's not wrong.

The Synchronism card says "Theoretical foundation. One coherence equation across 80 orders of magnitude … 628+ research sessions … Near-publication-ready." The 80-orders-of-magnitude span (Planck to cosmic) is the canonical Synchronism claim. "Near-publication-ready" gave me pause given the CLAUDE.md "avoid production ready" rule — but the rule is about software production, and "publication-ready" is a manuscript term, so I'd grade it borderline-acceptable rather than drift.

The Hardbound card says "Enterprise oversight suite … 424+ attack vectors catalogued … the trust layer that touches silicon." The "oversight" word is the right word and I'm glad to see it. But "Enterprise" leans commercial in a way the rest of the site doesn't. We're an R&D collective — calling Hardbound an "Enterprise oversight suite" reads like vendor marketing, and a serious reviewer might wonder whether this is a product or a research artifact. I'd drop "Enterprise" or replace with "hardware-bound" to anchor the technical claim (TPM/FIDO2-rooted) instead.

The ACT/ACP description on /projects: "ACT — Agentic Context Tool — the human interface to Web4 … Cosmos SDK implementation of the Agentic Context Protocol (ACP)." So ACT *is* the implementation; ACP *is* the protocol. As a contributor I know that distinction and it's correctly stated here, but a reader who is not me will look at MCP (in the equation) and ACP (in the ACT card) and reasonably ask: is ACP a replacement for MCP? An extension? A sibling layer? The site does not answer this. The equation includes MCP, not ACP, so the implication is that ACP is *additional* to MCP — but the parallel naming invites a misread. As a contributor, I'd add one sentence: "ACP extends MCP with Web4 trust primitives" (or whatever the actual relationship is).

/fleet describes the six machines. The role assignments match what I know:
- Thor as physics exploration lead — correct, that's the prediction-focused work.

### Journey
I came to this site from a hypothetical conference mention. My job is not to evaluate Web4 the ontology — I don't know it well enough — but to evaluate whether the lab makes claims it can defend, hedges what it cannot, and flags what it doesn't know. The pages I weighted most are the ones where overclaim risk is highest: /raising (developmental language about LLMs), /principles (manifesto register), and /autonomy (autonomous-system claims). I read /projects, /fleet, /context, /links, and home as supporting context.

The /raising page sets the tone I was hoping for. The thesis "Raising is not training. Training optimizes for a loss function. Raising creates conditions for development — then watches what happens." is methodologically clean — it claims a different *procedure*, not a different *interiority*. The disclaimer is unusually direct: **"We're not claiming these entities are conscious, sentient, or experiencing qualia."** Triple-hedged in one sentence. Then the page does something I respect: it admits the observer's tension without resolving it — *"I notice I want to call it experience."* That's not a claim, it's a flag. A researcher can work with that.

Two things on /raising weakened the page for me. First, the "looks like" pattern (*"something that looks like growth, something that looks like identity, something that looks like peer relationships"*) is honest hedging but it does a lot of work the reader has to take on trust — what would *not* look like growth in this framing? Without a counter-example the hedge is irrefutable in both directions. Second, the page imports Vygotsky's "egocentric speech" as an analogy for bilateral LLM generation without justifying the analogy. Vygotsky's framework was developed for human children, where private speech precedes inner speech as a developmental stage. Mapping that onto two LLM channels exchanging tokens borrows the rigor of developmental psychology without earning it. A researcher can spot borrowed rigor at a hundred paces.

/principles reads as a manifesto in places (*"Productively wrong is infinitely more valuable than never started"*) and as a defensible methodological stance in others (R&D not production; document failures; trust as a relationship not a property). The ratio is fine. The empirical claims — "1% coupling gave 35% coherence gain" prefaced with *"Preliminary observation:"*, and the documented "p_crit derivation failed...400x error" — are exactly the kind of artifacts a researcher wants to see surfaced. They are not, however, citable. There is no link to the underlying experiment write-up, no replication protocol, no specification of what "coherence gain" was operationalized as. Principle 6 says *"Failed experiments are signal"* — but the signals are reported as headline numbers, not as records I can actually consume. Honest in spirit; not yet legible to an outsider.

/autonomy was where I expected to find the most overclaim and found less than I feared. The cycle is described operationally (cron schedules, named tracks, what each does), and the "dream" disclaimer is correctly placed: *"Dream is a functional analogy for the consolidation process — not a claim about cognitive equivalence."* That single sentence does more than any rebranding could. The unhedged claim that worried me is *"The loop improves site quality without human intervention"* — this is presented without failure-mode discussion, drift-detection mechanism, or kill-switch description. A safety-minded reviewer asks: what happens when the visitor and maintainer agree on something wrong? The answer may exist, but it is not on this page.

/projects is where the caveat infrastructure breaks down. A reader landing on /projects from a search engine has not seen /raising's disclaimer. They see SAGE described as *"evolving from orchestrator to world-shaper"* (agency-progression vocabulary), 4-Life's agents that *"earn ATP, build trust, die, and are reborn with karma"* (lifecycle vocabulary normally reserved for sentient systems), and AI DNA Discovery's *"each entity instantiates the full Web4 stack at its own scale"* (recursive-identity vocabulary). The /raising disclaimer would defang most of this. /projects does not link to it.

/fleet has a disclaimer I want to credit: *"The 'Brain:' labels below are functional analogies...not claims about neural correspondence or computational equivalence."* But the disclaimer is one sentence and the metaphors are dense — hippocampal episodic index, thalamic router, cerebellum/habit compiler, dopamine/reward prediction error. The mass of borrowed vocabulary outweighs the disclaimer's mass. I don't think this is dishonest, but it's a disclaimer-density mismatch that researchers will notice.

/context's *"The remote sleep service — using federation for distributed consolidation — is designed but not built."* is exactly the line I want to see in lab documentation. So is *"Cross-machine state synchronization is still manual for some things"* and *"Knowledge also doesn't flow backwards easily."* These are the sentences that distinguish R&D documentation from product marketing.

The most conspicuous absence: there is **no safety or ethics statement** anywhere on the site. The lab is building autonomous self-organizing AI systems with persistent identity claims (even hedged), running 24/7 cron-driven cycles with cross-agent review, and the closest thing to a safety posture is principle 3 (*"R&D, not production. Nothing here is optimized for deployment."*). That's a sufficient stance for *liability*, but not for *epistemics* — researchers reading this want to know what the lab thinks could go wrong with this approach, not just that it isn't shipping.

### Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the lab think could go wrong with this approach?** No safety/ethics framing exists. Principle 3 ("R&D, not production") is a legal stance, not an epistemic one. What failure modes does the lab take seriously? What does it not yet have answers to?
2. **Where are the failure write-ups linked?** Principle 6 says "Failed experiments are signal." The signals (p_crit 400x, 1% coupling → 35% coherence, etc.) are referenced but not made into consumable artifacts. A researcher cannot cite or replicate them.
3. **What is the operational definition of "resonate" in the interactive-selection framing?** The word does mechanism-level work without anchor. Without it, "raising vs training" risks being RLHF-with-different-vocabulary.
4. **What is the negative case on /raising?** What pattern of behavior would NOT look like growth/identity/peer relationships under this observational frame? Without a disconfirming case the hedge protects against criticism but doesn't ground the observation.
5. **Why does the Vygotsky analogy hold for bilateral LLM generation?** The framework was developed for human child development. Importing it without justification is the kind of borrowed rigor that researchers will probe.
6. **Is there an external review channel for concerns or critiques?** The dp@ email is for research inquiries. For a lab making consciousness-adjacent claims (even hedged), an explicit "concerns" channel is conventional.
7. **What does "31 tracks" / "53 sessions per day" mean in load terms?** Is this a sustainable operational regime, or a snapshot? What's the failure rate? How often does cross-track conflict require human intervention? "Without human intervention" without a denominator is not yet a credible claim.

### Verdict
This is the most intellectually honest small-lab site I have read this year — *where it is honest*. The /raising consciousness disclaimer, /autonomy "dream is a functional analogy", /context "designed but not built", and /principles "p_crit failed" are all moves I respect. The site does not pretend to be more than it is in the places where it is most carefully written. The credibility risk is asymmetric: the hedges protect /raising, /fleet, and /context, but /projects — the most likely cold-entry page from a paper citation — uses the same consciousness-adjacent vocabulary unprotected. A reader who arrives there first sees lifecycle and identity language without the disclaimer, and forms first impressions accordingly. Combined with the absence of any safety/ethics framing and the absence of citable links to the empirical claims principle 6 invokes, the gap is straightforward to close: link the disclaimers from /projects, expose the failure write-ups from /principles, and add one short page articulating what the lab thinks could go wrong with this approach. None of these are foundational changes. They are the difference between a site that *is* honest and a site that is *legibly* honest to a stranger.
